\section{Introduction}


This document sketches which (linguistic) measures have been taken or 
could be taken to improve efficiency.

It is a report of a discussion among the linguists on how to improve the
efficiency, a report on some measurements done to determine the effect 
of certain changes, and a report on changes made to improve efficiency.

\section{Circumpositions}

The first part of each circumposition received an Fkey equal to the 
Fkey of an independently occurring preposition. This should yield a
very slight improvement in efficiency, both in morphology and in the
surface parser. The improvement is too small to be measured 
in terms of cpu-time.

\section{Idioms, Semi-idioms}

\begin{description}
  \item[Semi-idioms] 

The current approach to semi-idioms very often leads to a doubling of
verb entries, where furthermore the relevant sets of patterns are 
very often compatible with most structures they can occur in. This leads
to an enormous amount of parses in the surface parser, most of which will
turn out to be wrong.
In the case of Dutch {\em  geven} it led to a situation where almost no
sentence with {\em  geven} could be parsed.

In certain cases it was possible to solve this problem by giving these verbs
the same Fkey ( leading to a reduction of work in morphology), 
which is turned
into an skey only if the relevant skey of the noun part of the semi-idiom
is present as well (leading to less surface parses).

This has been done partially. It was not possible to do it in all cases,
because it would have as a consequence that certain entries would have to be 
split.

Some relevant cases are:


\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
Example               & \#args & DO? & IO? & {\em  aan}-obj?\\
\hline
een schreeuw geven    & 2     & +   & -   & -\\
aan iemand iets geven & 3     & +   & +   & +\\
iemand een zoen geven & 3     & +   & +   & -\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

Such cases also indicate that something is fundamentally wrong in the current 
treatment of semi-idioms.

It appears that in cases such as these the argument structure is not determined
by the verb, but by the object-noun.

I propose to distinguish two kinds of semi-idioms, and to extend the grammar
with rules for semi-idioms where the object noun determines the argument
structure.
This wil have the advantage the only a single verb {\em  geven} need be 
distinguished for all cases mentioned.

The proposed change requires: adaptation of the surface parser and addition
of some semi-idiomatic start rules.


\item[Idioms]

A similar problem exists in the case of idioms, e.g.
{\em  *(*(aan) iemand) de bons
geven} vs {\em  ((aan) iemand) een cadeau geven} leads in certain cases
to a doubling of the entry for {\em  geven} because the relevant difference
is accounted for in two different subrules of the same pattern transformation,
so that this cannot be accounted for by the idiom-specification.

It should be possible to specify that a certain subrule of a transformation
must be applied in idiom specifications, or (clearly less desirable) 
transformations specific to idioms should be written, or the relevant
subrules should be split up into separate rules.
Doubling the lexical entries is unacceptable, because it leads to unparsable
sentences.

The fkey-skey technique could not be used in this case, because it is
impossible to indicate that an fkey should be translated into an skey
if either key K1 is present or key K2 is present.

\item[Rules specific to idioms]
Certain rules that should apply for idioms only (e.g. substitution for
{\em  argrel} and substitution for genitive NPs inside an NP should
be made in such a way that they apply only to structures containing the
relevant idioms. Otherwise they apply to non-idiomatic cases as well,
yielding more derivations than necessary which are incorrect (linguistically)
as well (and they lead to ungrammatical output in certain cases).

This has been done, by using idiom patterns. The relevant rules apply only
if the verbpatterns contain the appropriate idiom pattern. In addition
the rule reduces the set of verbpatterns to this idiom pattern, excluding
literal derivations after the application of this rule.

\end{description}

\subsection{Argument check}

Perhaps a check on the compatibility between verbs and arguments could filter
out certain absurd structures (i.e. are the arguments required by the verb
present?), a.o.

\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em Laten we zwemmen} parsed as\\
 S[ S[laten] we zwemmen] and as 
 S[ S[Laten we] zwemmen];

\item[] {\em Ik wil dat kunnen zien} parsed as \\
        S[ik wil S[ conj(dat) kunnen zien]]
\end{description}

However, this is very difficult, and it absolutely unclear how much time it
would take (in the parse process).

\subsection{Surface attributes}

The adoption of special surface attributes will make it possible
to send more information through the surface tree  In this way
more ungrammatical sentences can be excluded, without being bothered
by these attributes in M-PARSER.

\subsection{Regular Expressions}

Perhaps rewriting the regular expressions might have a positive effect on
efficiency. However, this is extremely difficult, and it is unclear whether 
there will be a positive effect.

Perhaps the regular expressions can be rewritten automatically
(after uniformizing the naming conventions for {\em  Xfound} parameters).

\section{Tsubstspeed}

This transformation checks whether the arguments that should have been
desubstituted have been desubstituted indeed. The transformation lets
special NPs, such as {\em  het, dit, dat} etc. that can be introduced
syncategorematically pass, and special measures have been taken to deal with 
idioms.
The transformation has now been written for
{\em  subjrel, objrel, indobjrel, preadvrel, locadvrel,
prepobjrel}, and it can be slightly extended.

This has been implemented.

\section{ Prepositions }


\subsection{Effect of collapsing prepositions}

The preposition {\em  tegen} is tenfold ambiguous in syntax.

A 4-fold ambiguity is caused by the fact that {\em  tegen} is the first part
of certain circumpositions.

The other 6 (except for a temporal one) have been collapsed in an experiment,
so that {\em  tegen} was only 2-fold ambiguous. (The fkey-technique was used to
throw out the circumposition cases).

Results of the experiment:

\begin{tabular}[t]{|p{.5\textwidth}|l|l|l|l|}
\hline
zin                               &  10x & \#t & 2x &\#t  \\
\hline
hij is tegen de analyse           &  1000&  4 & 490 & 3\\
ik kreeg 'm tegen de analyse      &  1410&  8 & 730 & 7\\
ik kreeg de man tegen de analyse  &  3860& 16 & 1680& 11\\
de os tegen de vulkaan tegen de os& 10740&  8 & 2660&  2\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

(\#t= the number of surface trees)

Conclusion: a considerable amelioration in speed in the surface parser
(factor 2). The fact that certain trees did not occur anymore is not
significant in the first two examples. In NPs a considerable amelioration 
is achieved, and the number of surface trees is reduced considerably,
so that M-PARSER will have parsed these structures faster.
This will probably also have a positive effect on the parsing of sentences, since
there will be less temporary ambiguities w.r.t PPs inside NPs.

A disadvantage is that the translations will be worse, unless elsewhere
(e.g. in the semantics) measures are taken.

It has also been suggested not to split up the prepositions in the semantic
component, e.g. {\em  in+loc} and {\em  in+dir} have one and the same 
translation. In generation the correct variant is chosen by remembering
which rule was applicable in the sopurdce language.

I fear, however, that this will yield problems, especially when a third
language is involved, but this should be looked into.
\subsection{PPs}

It might be taken into consideration to change the treatment of PPs in the
surface parser, i.e. to differentiate to a lesser degree in the surface
parser according to the function of a certain PP in a sentence.

This is not simple: a larger part of the surface parser should be rewritten,
a large part of M-PARSER should be rewritten, and (since VP exists) still there
is always the choice to be made whether the PP will be dominated by S or by VP.
Maybe it can be done in limited ways, e.g. for all PPs dominated by S.

\section{Speed Rules}

The grammar should be scanned systematically for optional and iterative rules 
to see whether speed rules can cut off certain false paths at an early point
in the derivation.

\section{Time Rules}

In the current system all sentences with a non momentary verb as its head 
run twice through a part of M-PARSER, once for the imperfective path, and
once for the perfective path. It would certainly be profitable to make this
split only is A-TRANSFER. It should be investigated whether those parts
of the time rules that are purely semantic and do not correlate to some
formal distinction in the relevant language can be shifted to semantics.

The following table illustrates the difference:

\begin{tabular}{|p{.3\textwidth}|c|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
sentence            & Appl.  & Tried & Tree & CPU1 & CPU2\\
\hline
hij bad 'm om de os & 201 & 2348 & 141  & 1330 & 1320\\
hij beschuldigde 'm van de os & 162 & 1923 & 74 & 1170 & 1170\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

The  difference is not spectacular, but:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The difference occurs per subsentence, so it multiplies per
subsentence
  \item  The difference probably gets larger when the arguments of the verb
get larger.
\end{itemize}


The computation of aktionsarts should be moved into the semantics, because
otherwise many verbs will have to be ambiguous already in the morphology 
(or at least in the surface parser),
which will lead to a system that does not work at all, or works only in a 
completely unacceptable time. 


\section{Summary of measures taken}

The following measures have been taken already:

\begin{description}
  \item[circumpositions] fkey-skey translation conditioned
  \item[Semi-idioms] for some the fkey-skey translation has been conditioned
  \item[Idioms] for some the fkey-skey translation has been conditioned 
  \item[Idiom specific Rules] these have been conditioned to apply to idioms
                  only.
  \item[Tsubstspeed] has been put on
\end{description}
