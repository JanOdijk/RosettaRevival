\section{Control Expression}


 The control expression can be represented in the following way:

\begin{verbatim}

(
 (  TIsNotIdiom . (RC_Startverbprules (no id, sid)  ) )
 |
 (  TIsIdiom .      (RC_Startverbprules (id,sid)      ) )
)
.( TC_verbpatternrules )
.(Tnoadvvars)
.{ RC_tempadvvar}
.{ RC_Sadvvar}
.{ RC_locadvvar}
.( RC_Voicerules)
.( TC_ReflexiveSpelling)
.[ TC_PossAdjSpelling]!
.( TC_Aktionsartcalculation )
.(Tsuperdeixisok)
\end{verbatim}

The notation {\em []!} and {\em \{\}!} is used to indicate that there are
filters associated to the rule or transformation class.

The real control expression used in the system does not use names of
rule and transformation classes, but only names of individual rules and
transformations.

There is one deviation from the restricted version of control expressions.
This is necessary for a correct treatment of idioms and semi-idioms. See the
document on idioms and semi-idioms, doc R0???. The transformations {\em  
TIsIdiom} and {\em  TIsNotIdiom} are discussed there as well.

The transformation {\em  Tsuperdeixisok} is a speed rule, associated to
the transformation class {TC\_superdeixisadaptation}. It is discussed in the
subgrammar {XPPROPtoCLAUSE}.

\def\whit{wheather-{\em it}\ }

The status of \whit.

In Rosetta3 a distinction is made between {\em  arguments} and 
{\em  nonarguments}. The word {\em  het} in sentences such as {\em  het regent}
or {\em  it} as in {\em  it rains} is treated in Rosetta3 as a nonargument.

It seems to me now, however, that this {\em  het} resp. {\em it}(we will call 
them \whit) should
be treated as an argument. If done so, verbs like {\em  regenen, rain} can be
treated as normal intransitive verbs. Similarly verbs like
{\em  stormen, regenen} etc. in constructions such as {\em  het stormde 
protesten, het regende klachten} can be considered to be normal transitive 
verbs. 

% hebben conjugation
% Passive !!

Now let's turn to the motivation for considering the relevant {\em  het} as an
argument. It has been noticed by several linguists that \whit has a behavior
somewhat in between real arguments and real nonarguments (cf. Chomsky
1981, pp. 323-325, who calls it a quasi-argument, see also Bennis(1986)).
Chomsky concludes that {\whit} has no reference, but behaves in syntax as
if it has. I see no way to deal with \whit as a quasi-argument (as sketched by
Chomsky) in a compositional grammar such as Rosetta's. 
The problem of dealing with \whit in Rosetta as a nonargument is
the fact that
from the following three sentences only the first one can be derived by 
Rosetta3. The other two cannot, and for rather principled reasons:

\begin{description}
  \item[] Het is beginnen te regenen 
  \item[] Het is begonnen te regenen
  \item[] Het is opgehouden te regenen
\end{description}
 
If {\em  het} is considered to be an argument in such sentence, then we can 
derive all three sentences, just as we can derive e.g.:

\begin{description}
  \item[] Jan is beginnen te zwemmen 
  \item[] Jan is begonnen te zwemmen
  \item[] Jan is opgehouden te zwemmen
\end{description}

This may seem quite mysterious, but it can be explained in the following 
manner. The first sentence contains an instance of
so-called Verb-Raising. The second and third sentence contain an instance of
Extraposition (and no Verb Raising). 
In addition it is assumed in the grammar that the following 
relation holds (in Dutch):

\begin{description}
  \item If a verb takes an infinitival sentence as a complement and no argument
as a subject (Subject-Raising Verb), then the infinitival sentence must 
 undergo Verb raising. Short: All subject-raising Verbs are Verbraisers in Dutch. 
\end{description}

It is furthermore the case that verbs that have no argument in subject position
can occur in infinitival sentences only if the superordinate verb does
not require control. i.e. it must require `object-raising' (e.g. 
{\em I believe it to rain}) or 'subject-raising' (e.g. {\em It seems to rain}).

In the sentences two and three the embedded verb ({\em  regenen}) has no 
argument in subject position; it occurs in an infinitival sentence, hence the
superordinate verb must not require control, but `object-raising' (this is
not the case), or `subject-raising'. If there is subject-raising, then 
we must be dealing with verb-raising (by the generalization stated). But in
fact we are dealing with extraposition structures, and not with verb-raising
structures.

We have reached a contradiction, and for this reason the relevant sentences
cannot be derived in Rosetta3. This implies that one of the assumptions made
must be wrong. One of the following assumptions  (or both ) must be wrong:

\begin{description}
  \item[] The generalization {\em  Subject-Raising Verbs are Verb-raisers} 
  \item[] The assumption that {\em  het} in {\em  het regent} is a non-argument
\end{description}

I will now show that the crucial examples, i.e. the behavior of the verbs
{\em  ophouden} and {\em  beginnen} do  not provide evidence against the 
first assumption, and in
fact lend independent support to it. As a consequence, we must assume that
the second assumption is wrong.


The generalization made is well-motivated. Not only is the correlation
perfect (except perhaps for the examples to be discussed), but there are
also theoretical reasons to expect this correlation.
The potential counter-examples
to be discussed are the verbs {\em  ophouden} and {\em  beginnen}. 
I will restrict attention to the verb {\em  ophouden}.
Notice that {\em  ophouden}
allows \whit as its subject despite the fact that it does not allow verb-
raising, and hence cannot be a subject-raising verb by the assumption mentioned.

Now let us assume that the generalization is incorrect, i.e. we assume
that there are subject-raising verbs that allow extraposition, e.g.
{\em  ophouden} and {\em  beginnen}. If this is assumed, then the well-
formedness of {\em  het is opgehouden te regenen} follows directly, but it
is also predicted that any other non-argumental subject can appear as a subject
of {\em  ophouden} in combination with extraposition. This prediction, however,
is not fulfilled: 

\begin{description}
  \item[] *Er is opgehouden gedanst te worden 
  \item[] *Er is opgehouden kippen te zijn
  \item[] *Het bijltje is opgehouden er bij neergegooid te zijn
  \item[] ?Het is opgehouden duidelijk te worden dat hij ongelijk heeft
\end{description}
 
Neither {\em  er} of impersonal passives, nor {\em  er} of existential verbs,
nor idiom chunks can appear as a subject of {\em  ophouden}. Whether
extraposition-{\em it} can appear as a subject of {\em ophouden} is unclear to 
me.

% It rained itself out
%Het is mistig zonder echt koud te zijn
% ophouden

We must now conclude that {\em  het} in {\em  het regent} is an argument.

Also in other contexts control of and by \whit appears to be possible.

Chomsly cites {\em  It sometimes rains after snowing} as grammatical. 
On the other hand, the status of
sentences such as {\em  Het is mistig zonder echt koud te zijn} or
{\em  Het mistte zonder te regenen} is unclear.

But of course, there are reasons independent  of their behavior in infinitival
construction for assuming that \whit is not an argument. One cannot
ask {\em  What is raining?}, though one can ask {\em  Who is swimming?}.
One cannot say {\em  het regent graag}, but one can say {\em  Jan zwemt graag}.
And of course, we must guarantee that the contrast between {\em  *it tried to
rain} and {\em  it seemed to rain} is accounted for.

\def\ec{{\em c}}
In my opinion these facts do not necessarily imply that \whit is
no argument. They are compatible with the hypothesis that \whit
is an argument, but an argument of a different type than the argument expected
by {\em  graag}, an argument of a different type than where {\em  what} can
ask for. (comparable e.g. to the fact that the answer {\em  at 3 o'clock} 
is not a well-formed answer to the 
question {\em  What did John see}), and an argument of  a type
incompatible with the argument required by {\em  try}. 
If we assume that   \whit is
an argument, has reference (meaning), and its meaning is of type \ec, though
the type of the meaning of {\em what} is {\em  e} and the adverb {\em  graag}
expects an argument of type {\em  e}, then these facts are accounted for as 
well, as type violations (or: violations of selectional restrictions).
We assume then that verbs like {\em rain, snow} etc. are intransitive verbs.
These verbs expect an argument of type \ec\  as their complement. A Verb such as
{\em  ophouden} requires a subject argument, but it allows as its 
subject both arguments of type {\em e} and arguments of type \ec. Other verbs,
{\em  try, proberen, willen, overtuigen} require arguments of 
type {\em  e} as their
subject.

In this manner all relevant facts are accounted for.


The only strange aspect of this analysis is 
that \whit is the only word with a meaning
of type \ec. However, I see no other way to account for all facts.
