d0079.txt	  External specification of the morphological capacities                          of Rosetta3           Authors      :  Elly van Munster, Harm Smit and Agnes Mijnhout                   Doc.Nr       :  79           Date         :  23/01/86           Status       :  concept           Supersedes   :             Distribution :  project           Clearance    :  project           Keywords     :  external specification, morphology $fn 7     ---------------------------------------------------------          Philips Research Laboratories      Copyright (c) Nederlandse Philips Bedrijven B.V.  $ph Rosetta          Doc.nr: 79                    date:23/01/86 $LD 4 $Np 1 $C1 Introduction&*& $ This document contains the external specification of Rosetta3 with respect to the morphological component. An inventory of derivational affixes taking part  in  morphological processes that Rosetta3 should be able to handle is included, as well as inventories of affixes that will not be taken into consideration now  and affixes that are not eligible for treatment by means of rules. The  inventories included here are based on extensive inventories of morphological data in Dutch, English and Spanish, which are described in the documents nrs 55, 56, 58, 59, 61 and 76. The rest of this document explains the considerations that have led to these inventories. Topics that will be discussed are: inflectional and derivational morphology, dictionary strategies and the priority rating of the affixes and the criteria  for assigning these priorities.$  $FN 3 $ *In the phase preceding this document Jan Odijk made inventories of the Dutch morphological data. This document is therefore for a considerable part based on his work.$ $C1 Inflection and derivation $ In linguistics, a distinction is sometimes made between inflectional and derivational morphology. Inflectional morphology concerns itself with changes in the form of a word, such as inflection of verbs, plurals of nouns, genitive _s_ with adjectives (_iets wits_) and nouns, inflection of adjectives (in  Dutch:  _dik_/_dikke_) and comparative and superlative forms of adjectives. The  distinction between inflectional and derivational morphology is not always clear, but if we assume the above-mentioned processes to be inflectional, we  can suggest that Rosetta3 should be complete in this respect: for the three languages (Dutch, English and Spanish) Rosetta3 should be able to accept and generate all these forms. This will be done, as in Rosetta2, by means of rules, not by listing all the forms in the dictionary (see also section 3 on the use of dictionaries.)$ $ Derivational morphology deals with processes that make new words from words, by adding a prefix or a suffix. Derivational morphology is different from inflectional morphology in that it often changes the syntactic category and the meaning of the word to which it applies. The meaning of the derived word can be inferred from the meaning of the word plus the meaning of the affix. Another difference with inflectional morphology is that whereas inflectional paradigms generally show no gaps (e.g., verbs normally have a complete inflectional paradigm, although the forms may be irregular), derivation does not show the same completeness: it is very common that a derivational process lacks regularity. Still, there are several derivational processes that are so regular, that it is very easy to use them to make up new words. These derived words will  therefore not be all listed in the (Van Dale) dictionary and it would be profitable if Rosetta3 contained rules to make such words. If we are going to incorporate such morphological rules in Rosetta3, there are three points that we should look at: productivity, semantic regularity and translatability.$ $C2 Productivity $ If a derivation rule can be formulated in such a way that it does not produce any incorrect output, the rule can be said to be fully productive. The meaning of words made by productive rules can be derived compositionally from the meaning of the basic word plus the meaning of the affix. If a rule is highly productive, new words can easily be made and there is a reasonable chance that not all of these possible (but perhaps not yet existing) words are listed in the dictionary. Some morphological processes, however, have now ceased to be productive; it may still be possible to recognize a prefix or suffix in the word, but they cannot be used to make new words anymore. Between these clearly productive and clearly unproductive processes, there is a large area of morphological processes that show some irregularity, but which can nevertheless sometimes be used to make new words. A typical sort of irregularity is that words that could be made by means of these processes have acquired a meaning different from the compositional meaning that would be the result of a fully productive rule. Such words are listed in the dictionary and should not be allowed to be derived by rule, because it would result in an incorrect meaning. In fact, these words can be seen as idioms: the meaning cannot be inferred from the meaning of the parts. Sometimes, words are ambiguous between the idiomatic meaning and a compositional meaning. However, in the large majority of the  cases the idiomatic meaning will be preferred.$ $ If Rosetta3 will contain rules that make derived words, the productive processes are the first candidates. The basic word and the affix are both basic expressions to be translated and there are rules that combine them, predicting their meaning. Possible words made by productive processes may not all be  listed in the Van Dale dictionary and rules to make those words will improve the translation possibilities of Rosetta3. Processes that are not fully productive are hard to capture in rules, because it should be guaranteed that there will be no incorrect output. If we can make use of the fact that words with irregular meaning (which should therefore not be made by rule) are listed  in the Van Dale dictionary by blocking application of the rule, we might be able to formulate rules even for processes that are not fully productive. (See also the sections on the use of dictionaries and the priority rating of the affixes)$ $C2 Semantic regularity $ If Rosetta3 contains rules for making derived words, the result will only be correct if the meaning of these words is compositional. If the meaning is not compositional, the words should be treated as idioms, i.e., listed in the dictionary. In fact, productivity and semantic regularity coincide: productive rules produce semantically regular words, if a word is not semantically  regular, we claim that the rule is not productive. Semantic regularity is part of the notion productivity. So, for decisions about which morphological  processes could be captured by rules in Rosetta3, the same considerations apply as above in the section on productivity.$ $C2 Translatability $ Apart from productivity and semantic regularity, translatability of affixes is important for derivational rules in Rosetta3. If a productive derivational process is handled by an M-rule in Rosetta3, both the basic word and the affix will be basic expressions (in case the basic word is not a derived word itself, of course). So, a rule is only possible if for an affix in one language we can find an affix or another basic expression in the other language. Sometimes, an affix is not translatable in such a way: the derived words in one language may correspond to non-derived words or paraphrases in the other language. In that case it is impossible to capture the word-formation process in a rule. A good example of such a case are "letter-words" in Dutch, such as _A.O.W.'er_, _P.T.T.'er_, _A.D.V.'er_, _P.v.d.A.'er_, etc. The _-er_ suffix cannot be used in English in such a way and it is impossible to include an M-rule for this very productive process in Rosetta3. An interlingual system, such as Rosetta, cannot incorporate rules for these kinds of processes if one of the languages involved does not have a (derivational or other) process parallel to the other languages. This means that the more languages are taken into consideration, the less derivational processes will be eligible for treatment by rule.$ $ In cases such as these, where a very productive process in one language can nevertheless not be handled by rule, a problem may arise, because many of the possible derived words may not be listed in the dictionary (due to the productivity of the process). In these cases it may be necessary to enlarge the dictionary (although it will be impossible to attain completeness, as is  obvious).$ $C1 Use of dictionaries $ In ROSETTA, translation takes place through the interlingua; two types of interlingual expressions are used: meaning rules and basic meanings. Only the second of these two types, the basic meaning, will be considered here because the way we deal with it is -as we will see- of  importance for the morphology.$ $ Basic expressions are translated into basic meanings by means of  dictionaries. Dictionaries can be seen as ordered lists of basic expressions combined with basic meanings (represented as keys).  The usual input for translation, however, will not consist of basic  expressions, but of sentences, which, like all non-basic expressions  (i.e. phrases), have to be split up into smaller expressions (according to the principle of compositionality), which, in their turn, again have to be split up, and so on, until the moment is  reached, where all remaining expressions are basic. Then, translation can take place without any problems.$ $ The dictionaries of ROSETTA3 will be made out of tape-versions of ordinary dictionaries, which of course contain words as entries instead of basic  expressions. In principle, this won't be a problem, for normally basic  expressions have the status of "word" and, in reverse, words form basic expressions. But this is not necessarily the case: on the one hand phrases can be basic expressions (as is the case in idiomatic constructions, which have, as a whole, one specific meaning), on the other hand single words can have a complex meaning (think of compounds, derivatives and  inflected forms). Here we are facing a problem: it must be guaranteed that every basic expression can be found in the dictionaries. Several solutions are possible for this problem: in the first place we can  enrich our dictionaries. This certainly will be necessary -at least to a  certain extent- if we want to be able to deal with idioms properly.  It would also be possible -but not necessary- to list all inflected forms  (the number of which is limited for each stem); this implies that every inflected form is regarded as a basic expression. Derivatives however, cannot  be listed (unless the affix in question is not or no longer productive) because there exists an infinite number of combinations. The same holds for compounds. Secondly, we can make rules for derivation, inflection and combining words to compounds. This means that inflected and derived forms are not simply  considered to be basic expressions.  In ROSETTA3, a combination of these two solutions may be chosen; with respect  to idioms and derivation with non-productive affixes, the dictionaries could be enriched, for dealing with inflection and derivation with productive affixes,  rules could be made. For the time being, compounds will be left out of  consideration, as it seems to be very hard to make rules for combining words because of unpredictable semantics. However, some robustness measures will be considered for unknown compounds,  i.e., those that are not found in the dictionary.$ $ With respect to derivation a second problem arises: sometimes derived forms  deviate from their "regular" meaning. In general, such forms have been entered  in the dictionary, so we do not need to enrich it. Of course it would be nice  to have our rules blocked now, unless a particular form is ambiguous and has a regular meaning as well. At the moment it is not obvious if a blocking mechanism is really needed so it seems the best to make a decision about it later on. In cases of ambiguity, the dictionary meaning should be preferred; this will be enabled by the (planned) bonus system(see section 4).$ $UC ` $C1 Priority rating of affixes. $ In the appendix a classification of affixes has been made according to their priority rating:$    - `Priority I affixes`:     fully productive affixes, for which derivation                              rules will have to be written.   - `Priority II affixes`:    in the first instance no rules will be made for                              the affixes belonging to this class. An inventory of derivational affixes taking part  in  morphological processes that Rosetta3 should be able to handle is included, as well as inventories of affixes that will not be taken into consideration now  and affixes that are not eligible for treatment by means of rules. The  inventories included here are based on extensive inventories of morphological data in Dutch, English and Spanish, which are described in the documents nrs 55, 56, 58, 59, 61 and 76. The rest of this document explains the considerations that have led to these inventories. Topics that will be discussed are: inflectional and derivational morphology, dictionary strategies and the priority rating of the affixes and the criteria  for assigning these priorities.$  $FN 3 $ *In the phase preceding this document Jan Odijk made inventories of the Dutch morphological data. This document is therefore for a considerable part based on his work.$ $C1 Inflection and derivation $ In linguistics, a distinction is sometimes made between inflectional and derivational morphology. Inflectional morphology concerns itself with changes in the form of a word, such as inflection of verbs, plurals of nouns, genitive _s_ with adjectives (_iets wits_) and nouns, inflection of adjectives (in  Dutch:  _dik_/_dikke_) and comparative and superlative forms of adjectives. The  distinction between inflectional and derivational morphology is not always clear, but if we assume the above-mentioned processes to be inflectional, we  can suggest that Rosetta3 should be complete in this respect: for the three languages (Dutch, English and Spanish) Rosetta3 should be able to accept and generate all these forms. This will be done, as in Rosetta2, by means of rules, not by listing all the forms in the dictionary (see also section 3 on the use of dictionaries.)$ $ Derivational morphology deals with processes that make new words from words, by adding a prefix or a suffix. Derivational morphology is different from inflectional morphology in that it often changes the syntactic category and the meaning of the word to which it applies. The meaning of the derived word can be inferred from the meaning of the word plus the meaning of the affix. Another difference with inflectional morphology is that whereas inflectional paradigms generally show no gaps (e.g., verbs normally have a complete inflectional paradigm, although the forms may be irregular), derivation does not show the same completeness: it is very common that a derivational process lacks regularity. Still, there are several derivational processes that are so regular, that it is very easy to use them to make up new words. These derived words will  therefore not be all listed in the (Van Dale) dictionary and it would be profitable if Rosetta3 contained rules to make such words. If we are going to incorporate such morphological rules in Rosetta3, there are three points that we should look at: productivity, semantic regularity and translatability.$ $C2 Productivity $ If a derivation rule can be formulated in such a way that it does not produce any incorrect output, the rule can be said to be fully productive. The meaning of words made by productive rules can be derived compositionally from the meaning of the basic word plus the meaning of the affix. If a rule is highly productive, new words can easily be made and there is a reasonable chance that not all of these possible (but perhaps not yet existing) words are listed in the dictionary. Some morphological processes, however, have now ceased to be productive; it may still be possible to recognize a prefix or suffix in the word, but they cannot be used to make new words anymore. Between these clearly productive and clearly unproductive processes, there is a large area of morphological processes that show some irregularity, but which can nevertheless sometimes be used to make new words. A typical sort of irregularity is that words that could be made by means of these processes have acquired a meaning different from the compositional meaning that would be the result of a fully productive rule. Such words are listed in the dictionary and should not be allowed to be derived by rule, because it would result in an incorrect meaning. In fact, these words can be seen as idioms: the meaning cannot be inferred from the meaning of the parts. Sometimes, words are ambiguous between the idiomatic meaning and a compositional meaning. However, in the large majority of the  cases the idiomatic meaning will be preferred.$ $ If Rosetta3 will contain rules that make derived words, the productive processes are the first candidates. The basic word and the affix are both basic expressions to be translated and there are rules that combine them, predicting their meaning. Possible words made by productive processes may not all be  listed in the Van Dale dictionary and rules to make those words will improve the translation possibilities of Rosetta3. Processes that are not fully productive are hard to capture in rules, because it should be guaranteed that there will be no incorrect output. If we can make use of the fact that words with irregular meaning (which should therefore not be made by rule) are listed  in the Van Dale dictionary by blocking application of the rule, we might be able to formulate rules even for processes that are not fully productive. (See also the sections on the use of dictionaries and the priority rating of the affixes)$ $C2 Semantic regularity $ If Rosetta3 contains rules for making derived words, the result will only be correct if the meaning of these words is compositional. If the meaning is not compositional, the words should be treated as idioms, i.e., listed in the dictionary. In fact, productivity and semantic regularity coincide: productive rules produce semantically regular words, if a word is not semantically  regular, we claim that the rule is not productive. Semantic regularity is part of the notion productivity. So, for decisions about which morphological  processes could be captured by rules in Rosetta3, the same considerations apply as above in the section on productivity.$ $C2 Translatability $ Apart from productivity and semantic regularity, translatability of affixes is important for derivational rules in Rosetta3. If a productive derivational process is handled by an M-rule in Rosetta3, both the basic word and the affix will be basic expressions (in case the basic word is not a derived word itself, of course). So, a rule is only possible if for an affix in one language we can find an affix or another basic expression in the other language. Sometimes, an affix is not translatable in such a way: the derived words in one language may correspond to non-derived words or paraphrases in the other language. In that case it is impossible to capture the word-formation process in a rule. A good example of such a case are "letter-words" in Dutch, such as _A.O.W.'er_, _P.T.T.'er_, _A.D.V.'er_, _P.v.d.A.'er_, etc. The _-er_ suffix cannot be used in English in such a way and it is impossible to include an M-rule for this very productive process in Rosetta3. An interlingual system, such as Rosetta, cannot incorporate rules for these kinds of processes if one of the languages involved does not have a (derivational or other) process parallel to the other languages. This means that the more languages are taken into consideration, the less derivational processes will be eligible for treatment by rule.$ $ In cases such as these, where a very productive process in one language can nevertheless not be handled by rule, a problem may arise, because many of the possible derived words may not be listed in the dictionary (due to the productivity of the process). In these cases it may be necessary to enlarge the dictionary (although it will be impossible to attain completeness, as is  obvious).$ $C1 Use of dictionaries $ In ROSETTA, translation takes place through the interlingua; two types of interlingual expressions are used: meaning rules and basic meanings. Only the second of these two types, the basic meaning, will be considered here because the way we deal with it is -as we will see- of  importance for the morphology.$ $ Basic expressions are translated into basic meanings by means of  dictionaries. Dictionaries can be seen as ordered lists of basic expressions combined with basic meanings (represented as keys).  The usual input for translation, however, will not consist of basic  expressions, but of sentences, which, like all non-basic expressions  (i.e. phrases), have to be split up into smaller expressions (according to the principle of compositionality), which, in their turn, again have to be split up, and so on, until the moment is  reached, where all remaining expressions are basic. Then, translation can take place without any problems.$ $ The dictionaries of ROSETTA3 will be made out of tape-versions of ordinary dictionaries, which of course contain words as entries instead of basic  expressions. In principle, this won't be a problem, for normally basic  expressions have the status of "word" and, in reverse, words form basic expressions. But this is not necessarily the case: on the one hand phrases can be basic expressions (as is the case in idiomatic constructions, which have, as a whole, one specific meaning), on the other hand single words can have a complex meaning (think of compounds, derivatives and  inflected forms). Here we are facing a problem: it must be guaranteed that every basic expression can be found in the dictionaries. Several solutions are possible for this problem: in the first place we can  enrich our dictionaries. This certainly will be necessary -at least to a  certain extent- if we want to be able to deal with idioms properly.  It would also be possible -but not necessary- to list all inflected forms  (the number of which is limited for each stem); this implies that every inflected form is regarded as a basic expression. Derivatives however, cannot  be listed (unless the affix in question is not or no longer productive) because there exists an infinite number of combinations. The same holds for compounds. Secondly, we can make rules for derivation, inflection and combining words to compounds. This means that inflected and derived forms are not simply  considered to be basic expressions.  In ROSETTA3, a combination of these two solutions may be chosen; with respect  to idioms and derivation with non-productive affixes, the dictionaries could be enriched, for dealing with inflection and derivation with productive affixes,  rules could be made. For the time being, compounds will be left out of  consideration, as it seems to be very hard to make rules for combining words because of unpredictable semantics. However, some robustness measures will be considered for unknown compounds,  i.e., those that are not found in the dictionary.$ $ With respect to derivation a second problem arises: sometimes derived forms  deviate from their "regular" meaning. In general, such forms have been entered  in the dictionary, so we do not need to enrich it. Of course it would be nice  to have our rules blocked now, unless a particular form is ambiguous and has a regular meaning as well. At the moment it is not obvious if a blocking mechanism is really needed so it seems the best to make a decision about it later on. In cases of ambiguity, the dictionary meaning should be preferred; this will be enabled by the (planned) bonus system(see section 4).$ $UC ` $C1 Priority rating of affixes. $ In the appendix a classification of affixes has been made according to their priority rating:$    - `Priority I affixes`:     fully productive affixes, for which derivation                              rules will have to be written.   - `Priority II affixes`:    in the first instance no rules will be made for                              the affixes belonging to this class. The  inventories included here are based on extensive inventories of morphological data in Dutch, English and Spanish, which are described in the documents nrs 55, 56, 58, 59, 61 and 76. The rest of this document explains the considerations that have led to these inventories. Topics that will be discussed are: inflectional and derivational morphology, dictionary strategies and the priority rating of the affixes and the criteria  for assigning these priorities.$  $FN 3 $ *In the phase preceding this document Jan Odijk made inventories of the Dutch morphological data. This document is therefore for a considerable part based on his work.$ $C1 Inflection and derivation $ In linguistics, a distinction is sometimes made between inflectional and derivational morphology. Inflectional morphology concerns itself with changes in the form of a word, such as inflection of verbs, plurals of nouns, genitive _s_ with adjectives (_iets wits_) and nouns, inflection of adjectives (in  Dutch:  _dik_/_dikke_) and comparative and superlative forms of adjectives. The  distinction between inflectional and derivational morphology is not always clear, but if we assume the above-mentioned processes to be inflectional, we  can suggest that Rosetta3 should be complete in this respect: for the three languages (Dutch, English and Spanish) Rosetta3 should be able to accept and generate all these forms. This will be done, as in Rosetta2, by means of rules, not by listing all the forms in the dictionary (see also section 3 on the use of dictionaries.)$ $ Derivational morphology deals with processes that make new words from words, by adding a prefix or a suffix. Derivational morphology is different from inflectional morphology in that it often changes the syntactic category and the meaning of the word to which it applies. The meaning of the derived word can be inferred from the meaning of the word plus the meaning of the affix. Another difference with inflectional morphology is that whereas inflectional paradigms generally show no gaps (e.g., verbs normally have a complete inflectional paradigm, although the forms may be irregular), derivation does not show the same completeness: it is very common that a derivational process lacks regularity. Still, there are several derivational processes that are so regular, that it is very easy to use them to make up new words. These derived words will  therefore not be all listed in the (Van Dale) dictionary and it would be profitable if Rosetta3 contained rules to make such words. If we are going to incorporate such morphological rules in Rosetta3, there are three points that we should look at: productivity, semantic regularity and translatability.$ $C2 Productivity $ If a derivation rule can be formulated in such a way that it does not produce any incorrect output, the rule can be said to be fully productive. The meaning of words made by productive rules can be derived compositionally from the meaning of the basic word plus the meaning of the affix. If a rule is highly productive, new words can easily be made and there is a reasonable chance that not all of these possible (but perhaps not yet existing) words are listed in the dictionary. Some morphological processes, however, have now ceased to be productive; it may still be possible to recognize a prefix or suffix in the word, but they cannot be used to make new words anymore. Between these clearly productive and clearly unproductive processes, there is a large area of morphological processes that show some irregularity, but which can nevertheless sometimes be used to make new words. A typical sort of irregularity is that words that could be made by means of these processes have acquired a meaning different from the compositional meaning that would be the result of a fully productive rule. Such words are listed in the dictionary and should not be allowed to be derived by rule, because it would result in an incorrect meaning. In fact, these words can be seen as idioms: the meaning cannot be inferred from the meaning of the parts. Sometimes, words are ambiguous between the idiomatic meaning and a compositional meaning. However, in the large majority of the  cases the idiomatic meaning will be preferred.$ $ If Rosetta3 will contain rules that make derived words, the productive processes are the first candidates. The basic word and the affix are both basic expressions to be translated and there are rules that combine them, predicting their meaning. Possible words made by productive processes may not all be  listed in the Van Dale dictionary and rules to make those words will improve the translation possibilities of Rosetta3. Processes that are not fully productive are hard to capture in rules, because it should be guaranteed that there will be no incorrect output. If we can make use of the fact that words with irregular meaning (which should therefore not be made by rule) are listed  in the Van Dale dictionary by blocking application of the rule, we might be able to formulate rules even for processes that are not fully productive. (See also the sections on the use of dictionaries and the priority rating of the affixes)$ $C2 Semantic regularity $ If Rosetta3 contains rules for making derived words, the result will only be correct if the meaning of these words is compositional. If the meaning is not compositional, the words should be treated as idioms, i.e., listed in the dictionary. In fact, productivity and semantic regularity coincide: productive rules produce semantically regular words, if a word is not semantically  regular, we claim that the rule is not productive. Semantic regularity is part of the notion productivity. So, for decisions about which morphological  processes could be captured by rules in Rosetta3, the same considerations apply as above in the section on productivity.$ $C2 Translatability $ Apart from productivity and semantic regularity, translatability of affixes is important for derivational rules in Rosetta3. If a productive derivational process is handled by an M-rule in Rosetta3, both the basic word and the affix will be basic expressions (in case the basic word is not a derived word itself, of course). So, a rule is only possible if for an affix in one language we can find an affix or another basic expression in the other language. Sometimes, an affix is not translatable in such a way: the derived words in one language may correspond to non-derived words or paraphrases in the other language. In that case it is impossible to capture the word-formation process in a rule. A good example of such a case are "letter-words" in Dutch, such as _A.O.W.'er_, _P.T.T.'er_, _A.D.V.'er_, _P.v.d.A.'er_, etc. The _-er_ suffix cannot be used in English in such a way and it is impossible to include an M-rule for this very productive process in Rosetta3. An interlingual system, such as Rosetta, cannot incorporate rules for these kinds of processes if one of the languages involved does not have a (derivational or other) process parallel to the other languages. This means that the more languages are taken into consideration, the less derivational processes will be eligible for treatment by rule.$ $ In cases such as these, where a very productive process in one language can nevertheless not be handled by rule, a problem may arise, because many of the possible derived words may not be listed in the dictionary (due to the productivity of the process). In these cases it may be necessary to enlarge the dictionary (although it will be impossible to attain completeness, as is  obvious).$ $C1 Use of dictionaries $ In ROSETTA, translation takes place through the interlingua; two types of interlingual expressions are used: meaning rules and basic meanings. Only the second of these two types, the basic meaning, will be considered here because the way we deal with it is -as we will see- of  importance for the morphology.$ $ Basic expressions are translated into basic meanings by means of  dictionaries. Dictionaries can be seen as ordered lists of basic expressions combined with basic meanings (represented as keys).  The usual input for translation, however, will not consist of basic  expressions, but of sentences, which, like all non-basic expressions  (i.e. phrases), have to be split up into smaller expressions (according to the principle of compositionality), which, in their turn, again have to be split up, and so on, until the moment is  reached, where all remaining expressions are basic. Then, translation can take place without any problems.$ $ The dictionaries of ROSETTA3 will be made out of tape-versions of ordinary dictionaries, which of course contain words as entries instead of basic  expressions. In principle, this won't be a problem, for normally basic  expressions have the status of "word" and, in reverse, words form basic expressions. But this is not necessarily the case: on the one hand phrases can be basic expressions (as is the case in idiomatic constructions, which have, as a whole, one specific meaning), on the other hand single words can have a complex meaning (think of compounds, derivatives and  inflected forms). Here we are facing a problem: it must be guaranteed that every basic expression can be found in the dictionaries. Several solutions are possible for this problem: in the first place we can  enrich our dictionaries. This certainly will be necessary -at least to a  certain extent- if we want to be able to deal with idioms properly.  It would also be possible -but not necessary- to list all inflected forms  (the number of which is limited for each stem); this implies that every inflected form is regarded as a basic expression. Derivatives however, cannot  be listed (unless the affix in question is not or no longer productive) because there exists an infinite number of combinations. The same holds for compounds. Secondly, we can make rules for derivation, inflection and combining words to compounds. This means that inflected and derived forms are not simply  considered to be basic expressions.  In ROSETTA3, a combination of these two solutions may be chosen; with respect  to idioms and derivation with non-productive affixes, the dictionaries could be enriched, for dealing with inflection and derivation with productive affixes,  rules could be made. For the time being, compounds will be left out of  consideration, as it seems to be very hard to make rules for combining words because of unpredictable semantics. However, some robustness measures will be considered for unknown compounds,  i.e., those that are not found in the dictionary.$ $ With respect to derivation a second problem arises: sometimes derived forms  deviate from their "regular" meaning. In general, such forms have been entered  in the dictionary, so we do not need to enrich it. Of course it would be nice  to have our rules blocked now, unless a particular form is ambiguous and has a regular meaning as well. At the moment it is not obvious if a blocking mechanism is really needed so it seems the best to make a decision about it later on. In cases of ambiguity, the dictionary meaning should be preferred; this will be enabled by the (planned) bonus system(see section 4).$ $UC ` $C1 Priority rating of affixes. $ In the appendix a classification of affixes has been made according to their priority rating:$    - `Priority I affixes`:     fully productive affixes, for which derivation                              rules will have to be written.   - `Priority II affixes`:    in the first instance no rules will be made for                              the affixes belonging to this class. An inventory of derivational affixes taking part  in  morphological processes that Rosetta3 should be able to handle is included, as well as inventories of affixes that will not be taken into consideration now  and affixes that are not eligible for treatment by means of rules. The  inventories included here are based on extensive inventories of morphological data in Dutch, English and Spanish, which are described in the documents nrs 55, 56, 58, 59, 61 and 76. The rest of this document explains the considerations that have led to these inventories. Topics that will be discussed are: inflectional and derivational morphology, dictionary strategies and the priority rating of the affixes and the criteria  for assigning these priorities.$  $FN 3 $ *In the phase preceding this document Jan Odijk made inventories of the Dutch morphological data. This document is therefore for a considerable part based on his work.$ $C1 Inflection and derivation $ In linguistics, a distinction is sometimes made between inflectional and derivational morphology. Inflectional morphology concerns itself with changes in the form of a word, such as inflection of verbs, plurals of nouns, genitive _s_ with adjectives (_iets wits_) and nouns, inflection of adjectives (in  Dutch:  _dik_/_dikke_) and comparative and superlative forms of adjectives. The  distinction between inflectional and derivational morphology is not always clear, but if we assume the above-mentioned processes to be inflectional, we  can suggest that Rosetta3 should be complete in this respect: for the three languages (Dutch, English and Spanish) Rosetta3 should be able to accept and generate all these forms. This will be done, as in Rosetta2, by means of rules, not by listing all the forms in the dictionary (see also section 3 on the use of dictionaries.)$ $ Derivational morphology deals with processes that make new words from words, by adding a prefix or a suffix. Derivational morphology is different from inflectional morphology in that it often changes the syntactic category and the meaning of the word to which it applies. The meaning of the derived word can be inferred from the meaning of the word plus the meaning of the affix. Another difference with inflectional morphology is that whereas inflectional paradigms generally show no gaps (e.g., verbs normally have a complete inflectional paradigm, although the forms may be irregular), derivation does not show the same completeness: it is very common that a derivational process lacks regularity. Still, there are several derivational processes that are so regular, that it is very easy to use them to make up new words. These derived words will  therefore not be all listed in the (Van Dale) dictionary and it would be profitable if Rosetta3 contained rules to make such words. If we are going to incorporate such morphological rules in Rosetta3, there are three points that we should look at: productivity, semantic regularity and translatability.$ $C2 Productivity $ If a derivation rule can be formulated in such a way that it does not produce any incorrect output, the rule can be said to be fully productive. The meaning of words made by productive rules can be derived compositionally from the meaning of the basic word plus the meaning of the affix. If a rule is highly productive, new words can easily be made and there is a reasonable chance that not all of these possible (but perhaps not yet existing) words are listed in the dictionary. Some morphological processes, however, have now ceased to be productive; it may still be possible to recognize a prefix or suffix in the word, but they cannot be used to make new words anymore. Between these clearly productive and clearly unproductive processes, there is a large area of morphological processes that show some irregularity, but which can nevertheless sometimes be used to make new words. A typical sort of irregularity is that words that could be made by means of these processes have acquired a meaning different from the compositional meaning that would be the result of a fully productive rule. Such words are listed in the dictionary and should not be allowed to be derived by rule, because it would result in an incorrect meaning. In fact, these words can be seen as idioms: the meaning cannot be inferred from the meaning of the parts. Sometimes, words are ambiguous between the idiomatic meaning and a compositional meaning. However, in the large majority of the  cases the idiomatic meaning will be preferred.$ $ If Rosetta3 will contain rules that make derived words, the productive processes are the first candidates. The basic word and the affix are both basic expressions to be translated and there are rules that combine them, predicting their meaning. Possible words made by productive processes may not all be  listed in the Van Dale dictionary and rules to make those words will improve the translation possibilities of Rosetta3. Processes that are not fully productive are hard to capture in rules, because it should be guaranteed that there will be no incorrect output. If we can make use of the fact that words with irregular meaning (which should therefore not be made by rule) are listed  in the Van Dale dictionary by blocking application of the rule, we might be able to formulate rules even for processes that are not fully productive. (See also the sections on the use of dictionaries and the priority rating of the affixes)$ $C2 Semantic regularity $ If Rosetta3 contains rules for making derived words, the result will only be correct if the meaning of these words is compositional. If the meaning is not compositional, the words should be treated as idioms, i.e., listed in the dictionary. In fact, productivity and semantic regularity coincide: productive rules produce semantically regular words, if a word is not semantically  regular, we claim that the rule is not productive. Semantic regularity is part of the notion productivity. So, for decisions about which morphological  processes could be captured by rules in Rosetta3, the same considerations apply as above in the section on productivity.$ $C2 Translatability $ Apart from productivity and semantic regularity, translatability of affixes is important for derivational rules in Rosetta3. If a productive derivational process is handled by an M-rule in Rosetta3, both the basic word and the affix will be basic expressions (in case the basic word is not a derived word itself, of course). So, a rule is only possible if for an affix in one language we can find an affix or another basic expression in the other language. Sometimes, an affix is not translatable in such a way: the derived words in one language may correspond to non-derived words or paraphrases in the other language. In that case it is impossible to capture the word-formation process in a rule. A good example of such a case are "letter-words" in Dutch, such as _A.O.W.'er_, _P.T.T.'er_, _A.D.V.'er_, _P.v.d.A.'er_, etc. The _-er_ suffix cannot be used in English in such a way and it is impossible to include an M-rule for this very productive process in Rosetta3. An interlingual system, such as Rosetta, cannot incorporate rules for these kinds of processes if one of the languages involved does not have a (derivational or other) process parallel to the other languages. This means that the more languages are taken into consideration, the less derivational processes will be eligible for treatment by rule.$ $ In cases such as these, where a very productive process in one language can nevertheless not be handled by rule, a problem may arise, because many of the possible derived words may not be listed in the dictionary (due to the productivity of the process). In these cases it may be necessary to enlarge the dictionary (although it will be impossible to attain completeness, as is  obvious).$ $C1 Use of dictionaries $ In ROSETTA, translation takes place through the interlingua; two types of interlingual expressions are used: meaning rules and basic meanings. Only the second of these two types, the basic meaning, will be considered here because the way we deal with it is -as we will see- of  importance for the morphology.$ $ Basic expressions are translated into basic meanings by means of  dictionaries. Dictionaries can be seen as ordered lists of basic expressions combined with basic meanings (represented as keys).  The usual input for translation, however, will not consist of basic  expressions, but of sentences, which, like all non-basic expressions  (i.e. phrases), have to be split up into smaller expressions (according to the principle of compositionality), which, in their turn, again have to be split up, and so on, until the moment is  reached, where all remaining expressions are basic. Then, translation can take place without any problems.$ $ The dictionaries of ROSETTA3 will be made out of tape-versions of ordinary dictionaries, which of course contain words as entries instead of basic  expressions. In principle, this won't be a problem, for normally basic  expressions have the status of "word" and, in reverse, words form basic expressions. But this is not necessarily the case: on the one hand phrases can be basic expressions (as is the case in idiomatic constructions, which have, as a whole, one specific meaning), on the other hand single words can have a complex meaning (think of compounds, derivatives and  inflected forms). Here we are facing a problem: it must be guaranteed that every basic expression can be found in the dictionaries. Several solutions are possible for this problem: in the first place we can  enrich our dictionaries. This certainly will be necessary -at least to a  certain extent- if we want to be able to deal with idioms properly.  It would also be possible -but not necessary- to list all inflected forms  (the number of which is limited for each stem); this implies that every inflected form is regarded as a basic expression. Derivatives however, cannot  be listed (unless the affix in question is not or no longer productive) because there exists an infinite number of combinations. The same holds for compounds. Secondly, we can make rules for derivation, inflection and combining words to compounds. This means that inflected and derived forms are not simply  considered to be basic expressions.  In ROSETTA3, a combination of these two solutions may be chosen; with respect  to idioms and derivation with non-productive affixes, the dictionaries could be enriched, for dealing with inflection and derivation with productive affixes,  rules could be made. For the time being, compounds will be left out of  consideration, as it seems to be very hard to make rules for combining words because of unpredictable semantics. However, some robustness measures will be considered for unknown compounds,  i.e., those that are not found in the dictionary.$ $ With respect to derivation a second problem arises: sometimes derived forms  deviate from their "regular" meaning. In general, such forms have been entered  in the dictionary, so we do not need to enrich it. Of course it would be nice  to have our rules blocked now, unless a particular form is ambiguous and has a regular meaning as well. At the moment it is not obvious if a blocking mechanism is really needed so it seems the best to make a decision about it later on. In cases of ambiguity, the dictionary meaning should be preferred; this will be enabled by the (planned) bonus system(see section 4).$ $UC ` $C1 Priority rating of affixes. $ In the appendix a classification of affixes has been made according to their priority rating:$    - `Priority I affixes`:     fully productive affixes, for which derivation                              rules will have to be written.   - `Priority II affixes`:    in the first instance no rules will be made for                              the affixes belonging to this class. An inventory of derivational affixes taking part  in  morphological processes that Rosetta3 should be able to handle is included, as well as inventories of affixes that will not be taken into consideration now  and affixes that are not eligible for treatment by means of rules. The  inventories included here are based on extensive inventories of morphological data in Dutch, English and Spanish, which are described in the documents nrs 55, 56, 58, 59, 61 and 76. The rest of this document explains the considerations that have led to these inventories. Topics that will be discussed are: inflectional and derivational morphology, dictionary strategies and the priority rating of the affixes and the criteria  for assigning these priorities.$  $FN 3 $ *In the phase preceding this document Jan Odijk made inventories of the Dutch morphological data. This document is therefore for a considerable part based on his work.$ $C1 Inflection and derivation $ In linguistics, a distinction is sometimes made between inflectional and derivational morphology. Inflectional morphology concerns itself with changes in the form of a word, such as inflection of verbs, plurals of nouns, genitive _s_ with adjectives (_iets wits_) and nouns, inflection of adjectives (in  Dutch:  _dik_/_dikke_) and comparative and superlative forms of adjectives. The  distinction between inflectional and derivational morphology is not always clear, but if we assume the above-mentioned processes to be inflectional, we  can suggest that Rosetta3 should be complete in this respect: for the three languages (Dutch, English and Spanish) Rosetta3 should be able to accept and generate all these forms. This will be done, as in Rosetta2, by means of rules, not by listing all the forms in the dictionary (see also section 3 on the use of dictionaries.)$ $ Derivational morphology deals with processes that make new words from words, by adding a prefix or a suffix. Derivational morphology is different from inflectional morphology in that it often changes the syntactic category and the meaning of the word to which it applies. The meaning of the derived word can be inferred from the meaning of the word plus the meaning of the affix. Another difference with inflectional morphology is that whereas inflectional paradigms generally show no gaps (e.g., verbs normally have a complete inflectional paradigm, although the forms may be irregular), derivation does not show the same completeness: it is very common that a derivational process lacks regularity. Still, there are several derivational processes that are so regular, that it is very easy to use them to make up new words. These derived words will  therefore not be all listed in the (Van Dale) dictionary and it would be profitable if Rosetta3 contained rules to make such words. If we are going to incorporate such morphological rules in Rosetta3, there are three points that we should look at: productivity, semantic regularity and translatability.$ $C2 Productivity $ If a derivation rule can be formulated in such a way that it does not produce any incorrect output, the rule can be said to be fully productive. The meaning of words made by productive rules can be derived compositionally from the meaning of the basic word plus the meaning of the affix. If a rule is highly productive, new words can easily be made and there is a reasonable chance that not all of these possible (but perhaps not yet existing) words are listed in the dictionary. Some morphological processes, however, have now ceased to be productive; it may still be possible to recognize a prefix or suffix in the word, but they cannot be used to make new words anymore. Between these clearly productive and clearly unproductive processes, there is a large area of morphological processes that show some irregularity, but which can nevertheless sometimes be used to make new words. A typical sort of irregularity is that words that could be made by means of these processes have acquired a meaning different from the compositional meaning that would be the result of a fully productive rule. Such words are listed in the dictionary and should not be allowed to be derived by rule, because it would result in an incorrect meaning. In fact, these words can be seen as idioms: the meaning cannot be inferred from the meaning of the parts. Sometimes, words are ambiguous between the idiomatic meaning and a compositional meaning. However, in the large majority of the  cases the idiomatic meaning will be preferred.$ $ If Rosetta3 will contain rules that make derived words, the productive processes are the first candidates. The basic word and the affix are both basic expressions to be translated and there are rules that combine them, predicting their meaning. Possible words made by productive processes may not all be  listed in the Van Dale dictionary and rules to make those words will improve the translation possibilities of Rosetta3. Processes that are not fully productive are hard to capture in rules, because it should be guaranteed that there will be no incorrect output. If we can make use of the fact that words with irregular meaning (which should therefore not be made by rule) are listed  in the Van Dale dictionary by blocking application of the rule, we might be able to formulate rules even for processes that are not fully productive. (See also the sections on the use of dictionaries and the priority rating of the affixes)$ $C2 Semantic regularity $ If Rosetta3 contains rules for making derived words, the result will only be correct if the meaning of these words is compositional. If the meaning is not compositional, the words should be treated as idioms, i.e., listed in the dictionary. In fact, productivity and semantic regularity coincide: productive rules produce semantically regular words, if a word is not semantically  regular, we claim that the rule is not productive. Semantic regularity is part of the notion productivity. So, for decisions about which morphological  processes could be captured by rules in Rosetta3, the same considerations apply as above in the section on productivity.$ $C2 Translatability $ Apart from productivity and semantic regularity, translatability of affixes is important for derivational rules in Rosetta3. If a productive derivational process is handled by an M-rule in Rosetta3, both the basic word and the affix will be basic expressions (in case the basic word is not a derived word itself, of course). So, a rule is only possible if for an affix in one language we can find an affix or another basic expression in the other language. Sometimes, an affix is not translatable in such a way: the derived words in one language may correspond to non-derived words or paraphrases in the other language. In that case it is impossible to capture the word-formation process in a rule. A good example of such a case are "letter-words" in Dutch, such as _A.O.W.'er_, _P.T.T.'er_, _A.D.V.'er_, _P.v.d.A.'er_, etc. The _-er_ suffix cannot be used in English in such a way and it is impossible to include an M-rule for this very productive process in Rosetta3. An interlingual system, such as Rosetta, cannot incorporate rules for these kinds of processes if one of the languages involved does not have a (derivational or other) process parallel to the other languages. This means that the more languages are taken into consideration, the less derivational processes will be eligible for treatment by rule.$ $ In cases such as these, where a very productive process in one language can nevertheless not be handled by rule, a problem may arise, because many of the possible derived words may not be listed in the dictionary (due to the productivity of the process). In these cases it may be necessary to enlarge the dictionary (although it will be impossible to attain completeness, as is  obvious).$ $C1 Use of dictionaries $ In ROSETTA, translation takes place through the interlingua; two types of interlingual expressions are used: meaning rules and basic meanings. Only the second of these two types, the basic meaning, will be considered here because the way we deal with it is -as we will see- of  importance for the morphology.$ $ Basic expressions are translated into basic meanings by means of  dictionaries. Dictionaries can be seen as ordered lists of basic expressions combined with basic meanings (represented as keys).  The usual input for translation, however, will not consist of basic  expressions, but of sentences, which, like all non-basic expressions  (i.e. phrases), have to be split up into smaller expressions (according to the principle of compositionality), which, in their turn, again have to be split up, and so on, until the moment is  reached, where all remaining expressions are basic. Then, translation can take place without any problems.$ $ The dictionaries of ROSETTA3 will be made out of tape-versions of ordinary dictionaries, which of course contain words as entries instead of basic  expressions. In principle, this won't be a problem, for normally basic  expressions have the status of "word" and, in reverse, words form basic expressions. But this is not necessarily the case: on the one hand phrases can be basic expressions (as is the case in idiomatic constructions, which have, as a whole, one specific meaning), on the other hand single words can have a complex meaning (think of compounds, derivatives and  inflected forms). Here we are facing a problem: it must be guaranteed that every basic expression can be found in the dictionaries. Several solutions are possible for this problem: in the first place we can  enrich our dictionaries. This certainly will be necessary -at least to a  certain extent- if we want to be able to deal with idioms properly.  It would also be possible -but not necessary- to list all inflected forms  (the number of which is limited for each stem); this implies that every inflected form is regarded as a basic expression. Derivatives however, cannot  be listed (unless the affix in question is not or no longer productive) because there exists an infinite number of combinations. The same holds for compounds. Secondly, we can make rules for derivation, inflection and combining words to compounds. This means that inflected and derived forms are not simply  considered to be basic expressions.  In ROSETTA3, a combination of these two solutions may be chosen; with respect  to idioms and derivation with non-productive affixes, the dictionaries could be enriched, for dealing with inflection and derivation with productive affixes,  rules could be made. For the time being, compounds will be left out of  consideration, as it seems to be very hard to make rules for combining words because of unpredictable semantics. However, some robustness measures will be considered for unknown compounds,  i.e., those that are not found in the dictionary.$ $ With respect to derivation a second problem arises: sometimes derived forms  deviate from their "regular" meaning. In general, such forms have been entered  in the dictionary, so we do not need to enrich it. Of course it would be nice  to have our rules blocked now, unless a particular form is ambiguous and has a regular meaning as well. At the moment it is not obvious if a blocking mechanism is really needed so it seems the best to make a decision about it later on. In cases of ambiguity, the dictionary meaning should be preferred; this will be enabled by the (planned) bonus system(see section 4).$ $UC ` $C1 Priority rating of affixes. $ In the appendix a classification of affixes has been made according to their priority rating:$    - `Priority I affixes`:     fully productive affixes, for which derivation                              rules will have to be written.   - `Priority II affixes`:    in the first instance no rules will be made for                              the affixes belonging to this class.
