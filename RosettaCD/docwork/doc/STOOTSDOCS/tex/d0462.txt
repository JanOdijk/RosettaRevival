\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Organization of this document}
This document contains a description of the 
linguistic content of Rosetta dictionaries. 

This document concentrates on a general explanation of the type of information
required and describes the actual situation for Dutch.
The document describes the current state of the Rosetta dictionaries, though
sometimes it will be indicated where deviations from the choices made
in Rosetta are possible.

In section~\ref{morf} we will describe the morphological information
required, in section~\ref{synpat} we will describe the syntactic information
w.r.t argument structure required, in section~\ref{synt} the other
syntactic information required and
in section~\ref{sem} the semantic information required. Section~\ref{idiom}
contains a specification of how idiomatic and semi-idiomatic 
expressions should be represented.

Several appendices have been added to recapitulate the information.

\subsection{General Remarks on the Rosetta Dictionaries}

\subsubsection{Syntactic Categories}
The notion of {\em syntactic category} plays a central role in the syntax and
morphology of Rosetta. For each syntactic category a distinct set of
attributes and their possible values is specified. The syntactic
categories of the open classes correspond directly to the traditional categories
{\em verb, noun, adjective} and {\em adverb} and are called {\em  BVERB, BNOUN,
BADJ} and {\em  BADV} resp. in Rosetta. Proper nouns are assigned
to a special category, called {\em  BPROPERNOUN}.
The sections on morphological
and syntactic information have been subdivided accordingly, though proper nouns
will be dealt with under nouns in most cases.

For certain classes of words the category assignment is not clear, or at least
controversial. For such cases choices will have to be made that are compatible
with the Rosetta grammars. Some relevant cases will be dealt with below.

This document deals only with the lexical specification of words from
the open classes, and not with words from the closed classes (prepositions,
conjunctions, pronouns, etc.).
Certain words get a very special treatment in Rosetta (and, we suppose, 
in any translation system), though they do belong to the open classes.
We will list these cases in a special subsection. 

\subsubsection{Attributes and their values}

As pointed out above, for each syntactic category a set of attributes and
their possible values is specified. This specification is part of
the definition of possible S-trees called DOMAIN T. 
Appendix~\ref{domaint} 
contains the relevant part of DOMAIN T.

For each category a finite set of attributes is specified. All attributes
are obligatory, i.e. for every occurrence of the category all 
attributes must have some specification. 
For each attribute it is specified what values it can take.
Values of attributes are either atomic or (finite) sets of atomic values.

\subsubsection{Keys}
For each stem there is  a name to identify it uniquely in morphology
(called the {\em  fkey}); for each object that must be distinguished in
syntax there  is a unique syntactic name (called the {\em  skey}) and 
for each meaning that must be distinguished there is a unique semantic name 
(called the {\em  mkey}).
To illustrate the difference between these different keys, let' s see
how
the three words {\em  lopen}, {\em  aflopen}, and
{\em  loop} would have to be incorporated in the dictionary. 
In Rosetta the stems of words are entered, and the stems for the
three words given are equal: {\em  loop}. It is clear that the three words
must be distinguished syntactically: We must distinguish between the
noun {\em  loop} and the verbs {\em  lopen} and {\em  aflopen}, and also
between the two verbs ({\em  lopen} does not take a particle, {\em  aflopen}
does). Since the stems do not uniquely identify these three objects, we
distinguish between three syntactic entities and give them unique names
({\em  skeys}), e.g. {\em loop\_N} for the noun {\em loop}, {\em  loop\_V} for the
verb {\em  loop}, {\em  loop\_af} for the verb {\em  aflopen}.
It is also the case that  from a morphological point of view the stem of the
verbs {\em  aflopen} and {\em  lopen} are the same: they are conjugated in 
exactly the same manner. To capture this, we say that the skeys {\em  loop\_V}
and {\em  loop\_af} are the same object morphologically. The morphological 
objects are given a unique name as well ({\em  fkeys}). Usually, the {\em skey}
 and
the {\em fkey} are identical, but in the case of verbs with 
particles (and also some
other cases) the {\em fkey} equals the skey of the corresponding verb without a 
particle: So the {\em fkeys} {\em  loop\_N} and {\em  loop\_V} exist.
Finally, every meaning of a word is associated with a {\em mkey}, e.g.
the verb {\em  lopen} can mean `zich te voet voortbewegen' (of animates), 
but also `zich uitstrekken' (of roads, rivers), or `geopend zijn waarbij er
vloeistof naar buiten komt' (of taps), etc.

\subsubsection{Dictionary Structure}

In most cases, when one looks from string of the stem to the meaning, a tree
structure arises: The single stem {\em  loop} is associated to two {\em fkeys}
{\em  loop\_V} and {\em  loop\_N}. The {\em  fkey} {\em  loop\_V} is associated
to several skeys, among them {\em  loop\_V} and {\em  loop\_af}. The skey
{\em  loop\_V} is associated to several meanings with their own {\em mkey}s.

\begin{description}
\item[] \mbox{}\\
\begin{tabular}{|cccp{.5\textwidth}|}
\hline
stem & fkeys         & skeys         & mkeys\\
\hline
     &               &               & {\em mloop1}"zich uitstrekken"\\
     &               & {\em loop\_V} & {\em  mloop2}"z. te voet voortbewegen"\\
     & {\em loop\_V} &               & {\em mloop3}"geopend zijn waarbij er 
                                           vloeistof naar buiten komt"\\
loop &               &               & {\em  mloopaf1} "beginnen te rinkelen"\\
     &               & {\em loop\_af}& \\
     &               &               & {\em mloopaf2} "schuin naar beneden gelegen zijn"\\
     &               &               & {\em  mloopN1} "manier van lopen"\\
     & {\em  loop\_N}& {\em loop\_N} & \\
     &               &               & {\em  mloopN2} "van een geweer"\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{description}

In certain cases, however, two different stems are associated to the same fkey,
or two different skeys are associated to the same mkey(s). 

Two (or more) different stems associated to one fkey is useful in cases
of spelling variants ({\em cadeau-kado}, {\em democratie-demokratie},etc.).
Two (or more) skeys associated to the same mkey(s) occurs if a word has
two syntactic realization possibilities that cannot be expressed in one 
syntactic entry, without creating a difference in meaning.

Examples are the verbs {\em  helpen} `help' and {\em  leren} `teach' in Dutch.
Both verbs can occur with infinitival complements with or without {\em  te}.
If {\em  te} is present, {\em  verbraising} is optional; if {\em  te} is absent
verbraising is obligatory. This dependency between the presence of {\em  te}
and the optionality of {\em  verbraising} cannot be captured in one syntactic
entry in Rosetta. Therefore, there must be two syntactic entries for these
verbs.



\subsection{Assignment of syntactic categories}

In this section a number of cases are mentioned where the assignment of 
the syntactic category is unclear or controversial.

\begin{itemize}
  \item 
A first example of such a problematic case 
concerns the status of words such as 
{\em  aan, uit, in, op} etc. in sentences such as {\em  de lamp is aan,
de school is uit, joggen is in, de koffie is op}. Van Dale classifies
these as {\em  adjectives}. The Rosetta grammars can deal with them partially 
when classified as {\em adjectives}, but there is a clear preference to call
them {\em  (intransitive) prepositions}. Classifying them as prepositions
accounts immediately for the fact that they cannot be used 
attributively and do not form comparatives and superlatives, and
it will probably also be possible to find contexts where these words
can and adjectives cannot appear.
%makes it possible to account for the fact that these words can appear
%in the context {\em  hij had NP \underline{\hspace{1.5em}}}, 
%though words that clearly are
%adjectives cannot. (cf. {\em  hij had het werk af, de koffie op, de lamp aan}
%vs. {\em  *zij had haar man ziek, OKhet huis schoon }
% hij had NP -- -> `controllable' predicates (adjectives and prepositions)

\item 
Many (especially locative) prepositions can form a phrase either in combination with
a NP ({\em boven het huis}, {\em  buiten de fabriek}, {\em  binnen de muren})
or on their own ({\em  boven, buiten, binnen}). Traditionally, these words are
classified as prepositions when combined with a NP and as adverbs when used
on their own. Recent theories classify them as (pseudo-transitive) prepositions 
in both cases. The Rosetta grammars can
deal with such words used on their own 
both when they are classified as adverbs and when classified as 
pseudo-transitive prepositions.


\item
In Rosetta a categorial distinction is made between adjectives and adverbs for
such cases as {\em  het huis is mooi} (adjective) vs. {\em  hij zingt mooi}
(adverb). The adverb is derived by rules from the adjective whenever this
is possible. 
It is controversial whether this distinction should be made in this way.

If no such distinction is made, and both are called adjectives, then at least
there should be an attribute for adjectives that indicates how they can be used.

This attribute must be able to distinguish between attributive use, 
predicative use and adverbial use. There are words that can be used only
attributively (e.g. {\em  gouden, voormalig}), there are words that can be used
only predicatively (e.g. {\em  iets beu, iets zat}) and there are words
that can be used only adverbially (e.g. {\em vaak, graag })
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Special words}


Certain words get a very special treatment in Rosetta, either because they
behave in a peculiar way in syntax, or because they require this to be able
to translate correctly. In addition, certain words are related to other
words,  and this should be reflected in the dictionary. 
Some of these words are:

\begin{description}
  \item[R-pronouns] The so-called R-pronouns ({\em  er,hier,daar,waar,ergens,
nergens,overal}) 
  \item[Words expressing Modal meanings] This concerns verbs such as
{\em  moeten, kunnen, mogen, willen, zullen, hoeven,..}, adjectives such as
{\em  noodzakelijk, verplicht, toegestaan, ..} and adverbs such as 
{\em  misschien}
  \item[Negation] the word {\em  niet} en {\em  w\'{e}l}, and also such words
as {\em  nooit, nergens}
\item[Auxiliary Verbs] The auxiliary verbs {\em  hebben, zijn} and {\em  worden}
\item[Special Adverbs]  Adverbs such as {\em  graag, toevallig, liever} have
a special pattern partially motivated to get the correct translation of these
adverbs.
\item[`Voornaamwoordelijke Bijwoorden'] Van Dale has entered many words such as
        {\em  daarmee, daarheen, daarnaast, daarnaar, daaronder, hierbinnen, 
hierboven, hierdoor, eraf, eraan, erdoor, etc.} as separate entries. 
Furthermore one of the meanings (often the only one) they give for such 
words is simply a literal one, as if these would have been {\em  met dat, 
naast dat, door het}, etc. For Rosetta purposes these words should not be 
entered as entries in the dictionary, unless they satisfy the following two
conditions:
\begin{description}
  \item[Meaning] If the relevant word has an unpredictable meaning or an 
unpredictable translation , e.g.
{\em  daarboven} also means `in heaven', {\em  erna} should be translated as
{\em  afterwards} (in English).
  \item[Inseparability] If the two parts of these words can never be separated
under the intended meaning
\end{description}

The predictable meaning should never be given as a separate meaning.
Furthermore, all these words should be marked explicitly by some unique name
(e.g. {\em pronominal adverb}) that distinguishes them from all other words, so 
that these words can be found back as a set automatically

If the meaning is unpredictable but the two parts are separable, then the words
should be treated as real idioms.

\item[Separable verbs]

It is extremely important to have a clear criterion to be able to decide
whether potential candidates for separable verbs are to be considered as
one word or as two words.

Not only it is important to make a clear choice, it is also important to make a
good choice (i.e. a choice that makes it possible to write elegant and correct
grammars). Van Dale is rather inconsistent in certain respects, and where they
are consistent, they made a rather unfortunate choice (at least from the 
perspective of the Rosetta grammars).

Van Dale is inconsistent in their treatment of prepositions. For some verbs
that can be combined with a PP (hence also with {\em  er + PREP}) a separate
verb of the form {\em  PREP+V} is listed. Example: the verb 
{\em  achteraangaan}, as in {\em  ergens achteraangaan} is considered a 
transitive (!) verb by the Van Dale N-N (p. 53).

Van Dale is rather consistent in that they nearly always have an entry
of the form Postposition(or:second part of a circumposition)+Verb if the
verb can be combined with circumpositional or postpositional phrases.
(examples: {\em  afgaan} as in {\em  van het dak afgaan} , {\em  inlopen}
as in {\em  het bos inlopen}. 

Though Van Dale may be rather consistent in this respect, their choice is
rather unfortunate: These cases can be described in the most simple way
by considering them combinations of the normal verbs {\em  gaan} and 
{\em  lopen} combined with the circumpositional phrase {\em  van het dak af}
and the postpositional phrase {\em  het bos in}, where the normal and general
rules of Dutch orthography determine that the postposition or the second part
of a circumposition are to be written as one word. It would still not
be a problem to list these words in the dictionary, if at least it would have 
been indicated formally and unambiguously (and in a way that is
automatically recognizable )
that it actually involves two 
words that have to be written as one word. This, however, is not the case
in Van Dale. 
In particular, if such a verb also exists independently, this interpretation
of such a verb is given simply as a separate meaning.

The cases mentioned must be distinguished sharply from cases where we are 
really dealing with one word,  e.g. {\em  afgaan} as in {\em  de bommen zijn om 
3 uur afgegaan}, or {\em  inlopen} as in {\em  zij hebben hun schoenen 
ingelopen}. A number of criteria to distinguish these cases are listed in
section~\ref{prepandpart}. 

Similar problems might arise with verbs such as {\em  kapotscheuren}
({\em  kapot scheuren}?), etc.

\item[Irregular Comparatives] Words such as {\em  beter,best, liever,
liefst} etc. are irregular comparatives of adjectives and adverbs.
If these words are listed in the dictionary, then it should be indicated 
formally, and in a way that is recognizable automatically, that they are
comparative forms of other words, and it should be possible to distinguish
this information from real meaning descriptions for these words.  

\end{description}

\subsection{Category mismatches in translation}

It is sometimes necessary to translate a word from some syntactic category
into a word of a different syntactic category. E.g., it is sometimes necessary
to translate a verb of Dutch into an adjective of Spanish, or an adverb
of Dutch into a verb of Spanish, etc.. 

In the case of translations from a  verb to an adjective the copula that 
accompanies the adjective in such cases should not be part of the translation.
The copula is entered in Rosetta by grammar rules.

\subsection{How to exclude what}

In many cases there are several ways to account for certain phenomena. In
such cases a clear and motivated choice must be made how to deal with such 
cases. Some examples of such cases are:

\begin{description}
  \item[zullen] Must the fact that the past participle of {\em  zullen} 
does not exist in Dutch be ascribed to the morphology, or must it be ascribed
to the fact that the syntax guarantees that 
there is no sentence in Dutch where this participle can be
used (because it will always be changed into an infinitive)?
  \item[sneeuwen] Must the fact that impersonal 
verbs such as {\em  regenen, sneeuwen} etc. do not have a first and second 
person be accounted for in morphology, or must it be accounted for by the
syntax or semantics?
  \item[mass/noplural] Must the nonexistence of plural forms for mass nouns
be described by specifying by means of morphological attributes
that the word does not allow a plural, or should this follow from their
character as a mass-noun (hence be accounted for in syntax or semantics?
  \item[Predicative adjectives] Does an adjective such as {\em  beu} not
have an {\em  e}-form because of morphological reasons, or because it
can never be used attributively? 
\end{description}

For all such cases clear and motivated decisions must be made, preferably
before beginning the actual production of the dictionary.

As a general strategy, one should adopt the following approach: if there
are empirical arguments that make it possible to decide on one of the options,
then choose that option. If there are no clear empirical arguments, then
choose that option that 
makes all components of a grammar as simple as possible,
and do not make choices that make foreseeable rules or applications impossible.

In Rosetta we have made the following choices for the cases described above:

\begin{description}
  \item[zullen] The nonexistence of the participle of the verb {\em  zullen}
is NOT accounted for in the morphology, but in the syntax. This solution
simplifies the morphological component (it would require positing
a new `conjugation class') and it simplifies the the syntactic part of the
grammar.
If the participle form of {\em  zullen} would have been excluded in the 
morphology, then in the syntax special measures would have to be taken to
derive sentences such as {\em  hij had zullen komen}.
  \item[sneeuwen] For impersonal verbs first and second person
are not excluded in morphology, but in syntax. Excluding first and second
person for these verbs would complicate the morphology (a new class of
irregular verbs would be created) and it would not simplify the syntax
or semantics, since it is still necessary to guarantee that only {\em  het}
can occur as a subject to these verbs.
 \item[mass/noplural] This is a case where the choice within Rosetta was
not made consistently from the start. Many of us, however, hold the opinion
that 
for mass words the absence of a plural
must be accounted for by the syntax/semantics and not by the morphological
component. This makes it possible to be indifferent in the morphology
to the count/mass distinction, which is expecially fruitful because many
nouns have both a mass and a count meaning. In addition, there might be
productive morphological rules that turns  mass noun with meaning M
into a corresponding count noun with the meaning `instance of M' without
adding suffixes. The resulting count noun can form a plural, and must derive
the way to form the plural from the original mass noun. In this sense
it would be equivalent to the fact that for proper nouns it is specified that
plurals are possible in principle. When used as a real proper noun, this
plural will never occur (this is guaranteed by the syntax), but when
the productive rule to turn it into a common noun applies, the way
this common noun forms it plural is determined by the information on the
proper noun.
\item[Predicative Adjectives] No systematic choice has been made within Rosetta
for these cases, but this yielded no problems up to now.
\end{description}

\section{Syntactic Information: Argument Structure}
\label{synpat}

\subsection{Introduction}

The syntactic information concerning argument structure is dealt with in
a special section, because it is the most important, most difficult and
most voluminous  syntactic information in a Rosetta dictionary.

Subsection~\ref{gencons} supplies some general considerations concerning the
concept of argument as used in Rosetta. subsection~\ref{atts} specifies
the relevant attributes and their interpretation in detail. The rest
of the subsections deal with several tests to determine argumenthood or
to distinguish certain verbpatterns. In some of these sections knowledge
of the list of existing verbpatterns (supplied in appendix~\ref{vps}) is
presupposed. One will not find a flow diagram to determine verbpatterns. 
It is 
assumed that most verbpatterns can be determined rather easily by studying 
appendix~\ref{vps} and the examples given there. 
Only for some verbpatterns that are difficult to distinguish specific
tests have been listed.

Some subsections containing tests 
are written language-independently. For every test it will be 
indicated whether the test is valid for all languages dealt with (U=universal)
or whether it holds only for a specific subset of the languages dealt with
(D=Dutch, E=English, S=Spanish). For some tests also their strength
is indicated, i.e. whether the test is fully reliable (strong) or not
fully reliable (weak).

It should be emphasized that most of the tests
should be read as implications, not as equivalences, i.e. most tests take the
form {\em if X holds, then Y holds}. This means that if X does not hold nothing
can be said w.r.t. Y, and if Y holds, nothing can be said about X.

%The section on the treatment of {\em  met} contains suggestions on how they 
%should be dealt with in the future, and how the current treatment
%should be changed.


\subsection{General Considerations}
\label{gencons}

The notion of {\em  argument} plays a central r\^{o}le in the Rosetta grammars
and dictionaries.
It should be emphasized that the notion of {\em  argument} used in the
Rosetta syntax is a notion that corresponds directly to the semantic 
notion of {\em  argument}. It is important to emphasize this, because
often the notion {\em argument} is understood in a purely syntactic way.
We will call this the {\bf Semantic Arguments Condition}:

\begin{description}
  \item[Semantic Arguments Condition] The concept of {\em argument} 
 in Rosetta correponds directly to the {\bf semantic} notion of argument.
\end{description}

We will therefore make an explicit distinction between {\em  semantic 
arguments} and {\em  syntactic arguments}. The notion of {\em  semantic
argument} is the one that plays an important r\^{o}le  in the Rosetta system.
The concept of {\em  syntactic argument} actually plays no role in the system
itself, but it is convenient to have a cover term.

We will sometimes use the word {\em  argument} on its own, without any 
modification. In that case we invariably mean {\bf  semantic argument}.

To illustrate the difference between semantic and syntactic arguments, 
the boldface words or wordgroups in the following examples
are {\bf NOT} considered to be {\em semantic arguments} in Rosetta:

\begin{description}
  \item[] {\bf Het} regent 
  \item[] {\bf Er} wordt gedanst
  \item[] Ik schaam {\bf me} er niet voor
  \item[] Ik scheer {\bf me} iedere dag
  \item[] {\bf Er} wordt beweerd dat hij ziek is
  \item[] Hij betreurt {\bf het} dat hij ziek is
  \item[] Hij rekent {\bf er}op dat hij mag komen
  \item[] Hij kijkt {\bf naar het meisje}\footnote{In this, and the next two 
examples only the NP contained in the PP is an argument, but the PP as a whole
is not!}
  \item[] Zij werd {\bf door  hem} bekeken
  \item[] Zij schonk een fles wijn {\bf aan haar vriend}
  \item[] Hij gaf {\bf de pijp}  aan {\bf Maarten} (idiomatic reading)
\end{description}

A second important property of the concept of argument in Rosetta is the 
following:

\begin{description}
  \item[Arity Preservation Condition] The arity (i.e. the number of arguments a {\word} 
takes) of a {\word} and 
            its translation must be the same  
\end{description}

This is a very important property, that determines the structuring of the
lexicon to a high degree. It is also important to emphasize this property
because it does not hold in other frameworks (e.g. not in Eurotra).

There is a third important property w.r.t. arguments in Rosetta, which
might be called the {\em  Fixed Arity Condition}:

\begin{description}
  \item[Fixed Arity Condition] Every {\word} has a fixed arity 
\end{description}

This property is important for the treatment of optional arguments.
It is not possible to say in Rosetta that e.g. a verb such as {\em  eten}
takes one or two arguments (cf. {\em hij eet} vs. {\em  hij eet een boterham}),
for that would violate the {\em Fixed Arity Condition}. In such a case there are
two options: either the relevant word is associated to two different {\word}s,
and each of these {\word}s does satisfy the {\em Fixed Arity Condition}, or
the relevant word is associated to only one {\word}, its arity is taken to
be the maximum of the alternatives (in the case of {\em  eten}: 2), and it
is specified that one of the arguments of this word can be realized as
a special phrase, called {\em  EMPTY}. This special phrase is deleted
in the derivation, so that no overt argument appears in actual sentences.
\footnote{Apart from the special phrase {\em  EMPTY}, there are also some
other phrases that have no overt realization.}

The following information should be specified w.r.t argument structure:

\begin{itemize}
  \item How many arguments does a word take (its {\em arity}).
  \item Whether these arguments are optional or obligatory
  \item How these arguments are realized, i.e. what their categorial
        status can or must be, whether they must be accompanied by
        other words (e.g. certain prepositions), what grammatical relation
        they bear, etc. 
\end{itemize}

There are a number of attributes to encodes such information. These will be 
described in the next section.

In Rosetta a distinction is made between {\em  arguments}, {\em  adjuncts}
and {\em  adverbials}. They distinguish phrases as to the character of their
relation to the predicate word (e.g. a verb). {\em Arguments} have an
intimate connection with and are fully
dependent on the predicate word: both their presence and their form is 
completely
determined by the predicate word. As far as translation is concerned, the
syntactic realization of the translation of the argument (i.e., what grammatical
relation  it bears, whether other words should be present, etc.) is
fully dependent on the properties of  the translation of the predicate word.
As pointed out above, the arity must be preserved under translation.
Typical examples of {\em  arguments} are
direct objects, indirect objects and prepositional objects.

{\em Adjuncts}\footnote{The usage of this term in Rosetta deviates from the
standard usage of this term in English linguistic studies.} 
look like arguments in that the predicate
word determines whether they can appear or not. However, adjuncts are always
optional, their connection to the predicate word is looser,
and though they cannot be translated completely 
on their own, their translation
depends only on the possible adjuncts of the translation of the predicate 
word. In addition the translation of the predicate word need not take
adjuncts at all in certain cases, so the arity preservation condition
does not hold for adjuncts in general.
Typical examples of adjuncts are {\em  resultative 
constructions} (e.g. {\em  hij verfde de deur {\bf groen}}), and
{\em benefactives} (e.g. {\em  hij kocht een boek {\bf voor haar}}).

{\em  Adverbials} have no syntactic connection to the predicate word at all.
They are always optional, generally can appear with all predicate words
(though sometimes there are semantic restrictions), and in
 principle they can be iterated
(subject to semantic constraints). Typical examples of {\em adverbials}
are temporal and locative expressions.\footnote{Though locative
expressions can sometimes be arguments, e.g. to a verb such as {\em  wonen}.}


\subsection{Relevant attributes}
\label{atts}
There are basically three attributes to represent information on argument
structure. The first attribute (called {\bf thetavp}, for verbs, 
{\bf thetaadjp} for adjectives,etc.) indicates  how many arguments
a word takes, whether there is an {\em  external argument} 
(in the sense of Williams(1981)) or not, and which argument is the external 
argument, and it indicates in which order the other arguments must
be put in the structure. The maximum number of arguments allowed is 3.

The second attribute, the {\em  pattern} attribute, specifies for each
{\em internal argument} the following information:

\begin{itemize}
  \item what syntactic category it can be or must be of
  \item what grammatical relation it must bear
  \item whether additional words should be present (e.g. prepositions)
%  \item (partially) the semantic type of the argument if this has syntactic
%        reflexes.  
\end{itemize}


There is a unique pattern name for each possible realization of the arguments,
and the actual realization is performed by so-called pattern transformations.

The third attribute, called the {\em  adjunct} attribute, specifies
which adjuncts the word can take (verbs only).

There are some other attributes that play a role in encoding the argument
structure, i.e. the attributes {\em  prepkey1,prepkey2} and {\em  CaseAssigner
} for verbs and {\em  prepkey} and {\em  subcs} for adjectives,
 but these will be dealt with in the next section.

\subsubsection{Verbs}
\label{vatts}

\begin{attributes}
   \att[thetavp] The possible values of this attribute have the general 
format {\em  vp\#\#\#}, where each \# is replaced by a different value
from the set \{1,2,3\} or by {\em 0}.

It is assumed that there are three positions to realize an argument:
the subject position(for the external argument), 
and two positions inside VP (for the internal arguments). Each \# represents
such a position. The first \# is for the subject position, the second
\# is for the leftmost position inside VP, the third \# is for the 
rightmost position in the VP.

It is furthermore assumed that arguments (maximally three) come in a 
certain order. Let us represent arguments by the letters {\x},{\y} and {\z}.


A 0 in a position means that {\em no} argument is realized in this position.
A 1 in a position means that the {\em first} argument is realized in this position.
A 2 in a position means that the {\em second} argument is realized in this position.
A 3 in a position means that the {\em third} argument is realized in this position.

We will illustrate this with some examples:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Suppose a verb with thetavp=vp120 is combined with two arguments,
         {\x} and {\y}, in that order. Then the first argument ({\x}) is realized
        as a subject (external argument), because the digit 1 
        is the first digit in {\em vp120}.
        The second argument ({\y}) is realized as the leftmost and only argument
        inside VP. This is a typical value for transitive verbs.
  \item Suppose a verb with thetavp=vp010 is combined with one argument, {\x},
        then this argument is realized as the leftmost and only argument 
        inside VP, because the digit 1 is the second digit in {\em vp010}. 
        There are no other arguments, so there is no external argument.
         This is a typical value for so-called {\em  ergative verbs}.
\end{itemize}

The {\em Arity Preservation Condition} only requires 
that the number of arguments
is preserved under translation. It does not say anything about the realization
of the arguments. Hence, words with {\em thetavp=vp010} can be translated into
words with {\em thetavp=vp100} (e.g. ergative verbs into intransitive verbs),
words with {\em thetavp=vp120} can be translated into words with 
{\em thetavp=vp012}, etc.

From a monolingual point of view all values for thetavp can have
the digits in ascending order (except for 0), i.e. from a purely
monolingual point of view there is no need for values such as
{\em  vp210, vp132}, etc. However, this is necessary to deal adequately
with translation. In certain cases the conventions
for ordering the arguments (see below) yield different results in 
different languages.
In that case in one of the languages the value for thetavp must be
changed into a value with descending digits. 

Several examples of this phenomenon can be given.\footnote{See 
appendix~\ref{argswitch} for more examples.} In Dutch the verb
{\em  bedanken} takes a (human) subject, a (human) direct object and
and (nonanimate) prepositional object with the preposition {\em  voor} :
{\em iemand$_{1}$ bedankt iemand$_{2}$ voor iets}. 
The spanish verb {\em  agradecer}
takes a (human) subject, a (nonanimate) object and a (human) indirect
object preceded by {\em  a}: {\em alguien$_{1}$ agradece algo a alguien$_{2}$}.

From a monolingual point of view, both the Dutch verb and the Spanish verb
would be assigned thetavp=vp123. For translation purposes, however, that
would yield the wrong result: The Spanish sentence would be translated into
Dutch {\em  *iemand bedankt iets voor iemand}, which is absolute nonsense,
and the same would happen in the other direction. This problem is solved
in Rosetta by assigning thetavp=vp132 to one of the verbs.

In the preceding example the translation resulted in complete nonsense, but
in other examples the translation can represent a completely opposite meaning.
The verb {\em  mogen} from Dutch can be used with two human arguments:
{\em  iemand$_{1}$ mag iemand$_{2}$}. It should be translated into Spanish
{\em  gustar} (which happens to be ergative, but that is irrelevant here). 
If no special measures are taken a sentence such as {\em  Jan mag dat meisje
wel} which means `Jan likes that girl' will be translated into 
`Juan le gusta a esa chica' which means `That girl likes Jan'!

\lexic{It is not required that in the lexic project the same approach is 
adopted. There the switch of arguments might be performed by a `transfer'
rule switching the arguments.}

For this reason  values with descending digits 
({\em  vp210, vp213, vp132, vp231, vp021,
vp312, vp321}) exist next to the `normal' 
values:

\begin{values}
\val[vp000] Verb not taking any arguments, e.g. {\em  regenen, sneeuwen, 
ijzelen}
\val[vp010] Ergative Verbs taking one argument, e.g {\em  vallen, aankomen,
                sterven}; Raising Verbs taking one argument, e.g.
             {\em schijnen, blijken}; `Copular Verbs' such as  {\em  worden,
              raken}; impersonal transitive verbs such as {\em  regenen, 
hagelen} as in {\em  het regende verwijten; het hagelde kogels} 
\val[vp100] Intransitive Verbs, e.g. {\em  dansen, werken, slapen}.
\val[vp120] Dyadic verbs, a.o. transitive verbs, e.g. {\em  zien, bouwen,
             doden, proberen, geloven}
\val[vp012] Dyadic ergative verbs, e.g. {\em  opvallen, meevallen, ontgaan};
            dyadic raising verbs (e.g. {\em  lijken})

\val[vp123] Ditransitive verbs, e.g {\em  geven, verkopen, beschuldigen} 
\val[omegathetavp] a default value; of no significance for BVERB
\val[thetavpaux] a special value for auxiliary verbs.
\end{values}

An important issue is the determination of the order of arguments. For that
issue we have adopted a very simple criterion in Rosetta. Order the
arguments from left to right according to the grammatical relation
in the following manner:

\begin{description} 
  \item[Argument Ordering Convention] 
   subject $\prec$ direct object $\prec$ Sentential complement $\prec$ 
 prepositional object,    predicates, locatives, directionals $\prec$   
indirect object, {\em aan}-PP, 
{\em voor}-PP ({\em  para-}PP)
\end{description}

This ordering is determined on a monolingual basis. Where two orderings of
different languages yield a conflict, this is solved by the technique to deal 
with argument switch as described above. 

Notice that a distinction is made between direct objects and sentential 
complements. Also, it is assumed that indirect objects, {\em  aan}-PPs  and
{\em  voor}-PPs cannot occur unless there is a direct object, a sentential
complement or a small clause with an overt subject as well. 
We will illustrate the argument ordering convention
with a number of examples:

\begin{tabular}{|l|}
\hline
Dat$_{1}$ viel hem$_{2}$ op\\
hij$_{1}$ gaf Piet$_{3}$ een boek$_{2}$ \\
hij$_{1}$ gaf een boek$_{2}$  aan Piet$_{3}$\\
dat$_{1}$ irriteerde hem$_{2}$\\
ik$_{1}$ dwong hem$_{2}$ [dat te doen]$_{3}$\\
ik$_{1}$ beloofde hem$_{3}$ [dat te doen]$_{2}$\\
ik$_{1}$ beval hem$_{3}$ [dat te doen]$_{2}$\\
ik$_{1}$ beschuldigde haar$_{2}$ van diefstal$_{3}$\\
ik$_{1}$ verfde [de deur]$_{2}$ groen$_{3}$\\
ik$_{1}$ stuurde hem$_{2}$ [naar Amsterdam]$_{3}$\\
dit$_{1}$ kost ons$_{3}$ [drie gulden]$_{2}$\\
omdat mij$_{2}$ [dat mooi]$_{1}$ leek\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

Notice that the order of the argument need not correspond to the left-right
order in the syntactic structure (at any level of abstractness). Thus
indirect objects obligatory precede direct objects and {\em  aan}-PPs
can follow direct objects in Dutch, but in both cases they are the
third argument for a verb such as {\em  geven}.

Notice also that a case such as indirect objects vs. prepositional phrase
headed by {\em  aan} is NOT dealt with as a case of argument switch. Cases
of argument switch are only necessary because of translation and never
from a purely monolingual point of view. If there are cases where
arguments can occur in different orders in a syntactic structure in one
language, then the pattern transformations deal with these alternations.

   \att[synvps] This attribute takes a set of verbpatterns as value.
The number of possible verbpatterns is so
large, that a separate appendix has been devoted to it. See appendix~\ref{vps}

A {\em verbpattern} is an atomic value that specifies how arguments of a verb
should be realized syntactically, i.e. what their syntactic category should
be({\em  NP, PP, AdjP,etc.}), what grammatical relation ({\em  direct object, 
indirect object, predicate, etc.} they bear, whether accompanying words
should be present (e.g. certain prepositions, or certain repletive pronouns
(e.g. {\em  het}), etc. 

The verbpatterns of Rosetta only specify how the {\em  internal arguments}
are to be realized syntactically. Furthermore, also verbpatterns
that specify the syntactic realization of more than one argument 
are nevertheless atomic. 
This is one of the reasons why there are so many verbpatterns.
Another reason why there are so many verbpatterns can be found in the fact
that {\em  optional arguments} do not exist in Rosetta (cf. {\em  Fixed Arity
Condition}). Instead, it is assumed
that all arguments are obligatory, but that certain arguments can be realized
by the special category {\em  EMPTY}, which has no overt reflex in the 
surface structure. Whether an argument can be realized by the special 
category {\em  EMPTY} or not is indicated by means of separate verbpatterns.

If a verb has more than one possibility to realize its arguments syntactically,
then this is specified by a separate verbpattern for each different 
realization. For this reason the verbpattern attribute takes a set of
verbpatterns as its value. 

In traditional grammars, and also e.g. in the Van Dale dictionaries a large
number of verbs is subdivided into 
{\em  transitive} verbs (verbs that take
a direct object) and {\em  intransitive}
verbs (verbs that do not take a direct object). 
For the Rosetta grammars this distinction is on the one hand not
sufficiently refined, as will be illustrated below, and on the other hand
the whole distinction is irrelevant, since in Rosetta distinctions are
made that are orthogonal to the distinction {\em  transitive-intransitive}.

The distinction {\em  transitive-intransitive} is (for the 
the Rosetta grammars, and we think, for any good description of language)
a completely arbitrary, a far too rude and in fact a
completely irrelevant distinction. 

The distinction is not sufficiently refined because within the class of 
transitive verbs several subclasses must be distinguished.  So, there are
transitive verbs that allow an indirect object in addition to the direct object
({\em  geven, tonen, schenken}, etc.) and verbs that don't allow an 
indirect object
{\em  zien, beminnen, houden}, etc.) There are transitive verbs that allow 
a prepositional phrase headed by {\em  voor} 
(e.g. {\em  kopen, bouwen, breien}) and verbs that do not allow this
(e.g. {\em zien, bekijken, snappen}). There are verbs that allow
the presence of a prepositional object with some preposition (e.g. {\em  iemand 
van iets beschuldigen, iemand met iets uitrusten},etc.). 
These, and many more distinctions are 
simply not made if only the distinction {\em  transitive-intransitive} is made.

In the class of intransitive verbs there are verbs that cannot take any
complement (e.g. {\em telefoneren, dineren}), 
others that can take prepositional complements
(e.g. {\em  kijken (naar), letten (op), zorgen (voor)}, etc.), 
again others
that must take prepositional complements (e.g. {\em  rekenen} as in {\em  
rekenen op}, {\em houden} as in {\em  houden van}), again others that take
finite sentences introduced by {\em  dat} 
(e.g. {\em  zeggen, denken, vermoeden}), etc. etc.
However, all such finer distinctions must be made.

In addition, within Rosetta distinctions are made that are completely orthogonal
to the {\em  transitive-intransitive} distinction.\footnote{This does not make
the preceding objection worthless, since there we took only examples from
within one subset of the transitives and intransitives.} The traditional
class of intransitives must be subdivided in Rosetta into the class
of real intransitives ({\em  e.g. dansen, telefoneren, dineren}) which take
a subject argument and the class of ergatives (e.g. {\em gaan, komen, stijgen, 
zinken}) which take an object argument.

The traditional class of transitives must be separated into a class of
real transitives (e.g {\em  zien, kopen, bouwen}) that take a direct object
as their argument and a class of verbs that do not take a direct object
as one of their arguments but a `small clause' 
argument (the subject of which then appears to act as a direct 
object), in constructions such as {\em [iets goed] vinden}, {\em  [zijn 
schoenen kapot] dansen}, {\em  [de kinderen naar huis] rijden}, etc.\footnote{
The brackets indicate the `small clause' boundaries} 

So, we see that among the traditional class of intransitive verbs there are
verbs that take a direct object as an argument, and that among the traditional
class of transitive verbs there are verbs that do not take a direct object
as an argument, which renders the traditional distinction worthless.

\begin{values}
\val[ResAP] for resultative adjectival phrases, e.g.
              {\em  hij brak het {\bf kapot}}, {\em  hij braadde het vlees
               {\bf bruin}}, {\em  hij knipte het haar {\bf kort}}, {\em  hij 
                kookte de aardappels {\bf gaar}}, 
               {\em  hij verfde de deur {\bf groen}}.

\subsubsection{Adverbs}

It is assumed that all adverbs take at least one argument, viz. the
clause or VP that they modify. A distinction is made between adverbs that
take internal arguments or external arguments, but the distinction is somewhat
arbitrary, and mainly motivated by translation considerations. A monadic
 adverb that must be translated into a verb or adverb in some other 
language takes only an internal argument. The main reason for this is that
for such adverbs the category of the argument must be specified, which is
possible only for internal arguments.
For all other monadic
adverbs the internal-external argument distinction is irrelevant.
Certain adverbs take an additional
argument, either the surface subject of the sentence,
e.g. {\em  graag, liever}, or the argument that has control over the
event expressed, e.g. {\em  enthousiast, opzettelijk} (see also the 
discussion of the attribute {\em subc} below, section~\ref{advatt}).  
One can test whether
an adverb takes such an additional argument by checking whether it imposes
selectional restrictions upon some phrase. For diadic adverbs the sentence or
VP modified is always considered to be 
the internal argument, whereas the other argument is external.

The following attributes exist to indicate the argument structure of adverbs:
\begin{attributes}
   \att[thetaadv] The values of this attribute are:

\begin{values}
\val[thetaadv010] for monadic adverbs that take an internal argument, e.g.
{\em  toevallig}
\val[thetaadv100] for monadic arguments that take an external argument, e.g.
{\em  waarschijnlijk, hard, snel}
\val[thetaadv120] for diadic adverbs, e.g. {\em  enthousiast, graag, liever}
\val[omegathetaadv] default value, of no significance for the lexicon.
\end{values}

  \att[advpatterns] This attribute can take a set of verbpatterns as its value.
The only relevant patterns in such a set, however, are:
\begin{values}
\val[synCLAUSE] The adverb takes a clause as its argument. This pattern is 
intended for diadic adverbs that impose selectional restrictions on the 
controller of the event described (e.g. {\em  enthousiast, opzettelijk}).
\val[synOPENINFSENT] The adverb takes a sentence as its argument. This pattern
is intended for diadic adverbs that impose selectional restrictions on the 
surface subject (e.g. {\em  graag, liever}).
\val[synCLOSEDINFSENT] The adverb takes a sentence as its argument. This 
pattern is intended for monadic adverbs with an internal argument 
(e.g. {\em  toevallig}).
\val[synnovpargs] for monadic adverbs that take an external argument only
(e.g. {\em  snel, hard}).
\end{values}
\end{attributes}

\subsection{Arguments and Argumenthood}
\label{arghood}

A first requirement to determine the verbpattern of a certain verb is the
determination of the arguments of the verb. It is not possible to give an
operational definition of argument. However, the following tests can be used
to determine whether some phrase XP is an argument of some verb V:

\begin{description}
  \item[1. Obligatoriness test(U)]
If XP must be present in combination 
with V, then it is an argument of V. One should
also show that the obligatoriness is caused by V for this test to be valid. 
It is nevertheless not perfect, since in certain cases certain elements 
must be present though they are not arguments (e.g. {\em  het} in
{\em  het regent}). 

Related to the obligatoriness test is the {\em Minimal Sentence Test}.
The phrases that occur in a {\em minimal sentence} headed by V are
arguments of V. A {\em  minimal sentence} is a non-imperative 
sentence containing as
few elements as possible, e.g. taking pronouns such as  {\em someone, 
something, somewhere, etc.} or {\em  he, she, it, etc. } as phrases combined 
with V. 
Note that this test does not necessarily yield {\em all} arguments of 
V, e.g. optional arguments are not found in this way.

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[houden]  {\em *houdt}, {\em *Hij houdt}, {\em *houdt het}, 
{\em Hij houdt het}: {\em  hij} and {\em  het} are arguments of the verb 
{\em  houden}    
  \item[wonen] {\em  *woont}, {\em  *Hij woont}, {\em *woont ergens}, 
{\em  Hij woont ergens}:
{\em  hij} and {\em  ergens} are arguments of the verb {\em  wonen}
  \item[beschuldigen] {\em  *beschuldigde}, {\em  *hij beschuldigde },
{\em  *beschuldigde haar}, {\em  hij beschuldigde haar}, 
{\em  *beschuldigde van moord}, {\em  *beschuldigde haar van moord},
{\em  *hij beschuldigde van moord}, {\em  hij beschuldigde haar van moord}:\\
{\em  hij} and {\em  haar} are arguments of {\em  beschuldigen}, the status
of {\em  van moord} as argument of the verb {\em  beschuldigen} is unclear
(on the basis of this test).
\end{description}

  \item[2. Maximal Projection Test(U)] Arguments must be maximal projections, i.e.
NPs, SENTENCES, PREPPs, ADVPs, ADJPs etc. can be arguments, but prepositions,
single adverbs, single nouns, particles, conjunctions  etc. cannot be arguments.

Example:
\begin{description}
  \item[Aan] {\em  Aan} in {\em  hij kondigde hem aan} is not an argument.
 \end{description}


  \item[3. Reference Test(U)] Arguments must have reference. One can check
whether an NP has reference by replacing it by {\em  wie} or {\em  wat} and
check whether a well-formed sentence results. If it does, then the NP
has reference, if it does not, the NP has no reference. Similarly, adverb
phrases and PREPPs can be replaced by {\em  waar, wanneer, waarheen, hoe}, etc.
This excludes {\em  het}
in {\em  het regent} (cf. {\em  *wat regent (er)}), 
{\em  er} in such sentences as {\em  er werd gedanst} (cf.  {\em  *wat werd 
gedanst})\footnote{The sentence {\em  er werd gedanst} is ambiguous: {\em  er} 
is
either a meaningless element, or it has a locative meaning. In its locative 
meaning, {\em  er} can be replaced by {\em  waar}.}
etc. This test also correctly excludes parts of idioms as   semantic arguments.
It also excludes the PREPP {\em  van chocola} as in {\em  hij houdt van chocola}
(cf. {\em  *Waar houdt hij?}), but it does not exclude {\em  in Amsterdam} as
in {\em  hij woont in Amsterdam} (cf. {\em Waar woont hij}).


  \item[4. Selection Restriction Test(U)] A verb W can impose selectional 
restrictions only on arguments of W. This implies: if selectional 
restrictions are imposed by V on XP, then XP is an 
argument of V. If no selectional restrictions are imposed at all, then XP is 
not an argument of V.

Ways to determine whether selectional restrictions are imposed on some 
phrase XP by some verb V:

\begin{description}
  \item[a. Weather-it] Check whether XP can be replaced by {\em  weather-it}. If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then in most cases no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP
(if so, then all the other tests must yield the same result). In some cases, 
however, this latter conclusion cannot be drawn (example: {\em  ophouden}).

In general it is impossible to prove that some occurrence of {\em  het} is 
{\em  weather-it}, and not some other type of {\em  het}, unless the 
occurring {\em  het} serves a double function: it is the understood 
subject of some {\em  weather}-verb and the subject (or object) of V. 
This is possible only if V is a verb taking an infinitival sentential 
complement. 

Example:

\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert te zwemmen} vs. 
                   {\em  *het probeert te regenen}:  {\em  Jan} is an argument 
of {\em  proberen}
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em Jan blijkt te zwemmen } vs.
                  {\em  Het blijkt te regenen}: {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  blijken}. In this example it will turn out to be the case that {\em  Jan} 
is indeed {\em not} an argument of {\em  blijken} (see the other tests).
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan is opgehouden te zwemmen} vs.
           {\em  Het is opgehouden te regenen}:  {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  ophouden}. In this example, however, it will turn out to be the 
case that {\em  Jan} 
is in fact an argument of {\em  ophouden} (see the other tests).
\end{description}

  \item[b. Expletives] Check whether XP can be replaced by {Expletives} 
(e.g. {\em  er} (as in Dutch impersonal passives), {\em  there} in English)
If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP
(if so, then all the other tests must yield the same result).

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Er probeert gedanst te worden}: {\em  Jan} is an 
argument of {\em  proberen}.
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan blijkt te dansen} vs. 
{\em  Er blijkt gedanst te worden}: {\em  Jan} is not an argument of {\em  
blijken} 
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan hield op te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Er hield op gedanst te worden}: {\em  Jan} is an 
argument of {\em  ophouden}.

  \item[V=see, XP=John] {\em John saw a man} vs. {\em  * There saw a man}: 
{\em  John} 
is an argument of {\em  see}.
\end{description}


  \item[c. Extraposition-it]  Check whether XP can be replaced by 
{\em  Extraposition-it} (i.e. {\em  het}, {\em  it}, {\em  lo} or zero 
functioning as an
anticipatory {\em  subject, object} etc. for a sentential argument). 

If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then in most cases no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP
(if so, then all the other tests must yield the same result).

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen,XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert duidelijk te zijn} vs.
                   {\em  *het probeert duidelijk te zijn dat dit fout is}: 
{\em  Jan} is an argument of {\em  proberen}.
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan blijkt duidelijk te zijn} vs. 
{\em  Het blijkt duidelijk te zijn dat dit fout is}: {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  blijken}.
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan hield op duidelijk te zijn} vs.
                   {\em  ?Het is opgehouden duidelijk te zijn dat dit fout is}:
{\em  Jan} is an argument of {\em  ophouden}

\end{description}


  \item[d. Idiom Chunks]   Check whether XP can be replaced by 
an {\em  Idiom Chunk} (i.e. some part of an idiomatic phrase).

If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP.

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen,XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Het bijltje probeert er bij neer gegooid te zijn}: 
{\em  Jan} is an argument of {\em  proberen}.
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan blijkt te dansen} vs. 
{\em  Het bijltje blijkt er bij neer gegooid te zijn}: {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  blijken}.
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan hield op te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Het bijltje is opgehouden er bij 
neergegooid te zijn}: {\em  Jan } is an argument of {\em  ophouden}

\end{description}


  \item[e. Other Type] replace XP by some 
other phrase with the same syntax but
with a different type, e.g. replace a {\em human} XP by a {\em nonhuman} XP',
etc. (Examples of types; {\em  animate -nonanimate}; 
{\em  concrete-abstract}; {\em  masculine-feminine}; 
{\em  food-nonfood}; {\em  institution-noninstitution} etc. ).

If there are cases where this yields semantically ill-formed sentences
(or only metaphorical readings), then
XP is an argument, and if V is the only word
present imposing selection restrictions, then XP is an argument of V.
 
Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=verven, XP=de deur] {\em  Hij verft de deur }, cf. {\em  *hij 
verft de muziek }: {\em  de deur} is an argument of {\em  verven} 
  \item[V=beloven, XP=de jongen] {\em  hij beloofde de jongen het boek } vs.
{\em  *hij beloofde de eerlijkheid het boek }: {\em  de jongen} is an
argument of {\em  beloven}.
  \item[V=surprise, XP=the boy] {\em  her honesty surprised the boy} vs.
{\em  *her honesty surprised his shame}: {\em  the boy} is an argument of
{\em  surprise}.
\end{description}


\end{description}
 

\item[5. Category Restriction Test(U)] If V restricts the category of XP in any way,
then XP is an argument of V. It is important to note that one must really show 
that the categorial restriction is imposed by V, and not by other factors.

Example:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=schijnen, XP=ziek te zijn] {\em  Hij schijnt ziek te zijn} vs.
{\em  *Hij schijnt ziek}. The phrase {\em  ziek te zijn} is an argument of
{\em  schijnen} since {\em  schijnen} imposes the restriction that the phrase
functioning as its argument can be a SENTENCE, but not a AdjP.
\end{description}


%\item[Oblique Case Test(U)] If XP bears an oblique (=nonnominative 
%nonaccusative) case assigned by V, then XP is an argument of V. 

%This test is not very useful in the languages dealt with in Rosetta3. But
%all dative NPs are arguments of the verb (adjective) that assigns this dative 
%case.

\item[6. XP Integrity Test(U)] V cannot determine the form of the head X or of
complements of X if X is a member of the open (lexical)
categories (=N,Adj,Adv,Prep,V).

 
Take care: 

\begin{itemize}
  \item Many form aspects that appear to be internal to XP are in fact 
(also) features on XP: these {\em can} be restricted by V! 
(example: case )
  \item The form of elements from the closed  (nonlexical) categories 
(e.g. subordinate conjunctions,
`inflectional' words such as {\em  te} in Dutch or {\em  to} in English) 
can be determined by a verb.

\item In many cases XP-internal it may appear to be the case that
form aspects are imposed by V, though in
fact they are the (indirect) result of semantic restrictions imposed by the 
semantics of V onto the semantics of XP. 

\end{itemize}

This test correctly excludes parts of idioms as semantic arguments because
the form  of a part of an idiom is prescribed exactly by the idiom description.

It also correctly includes parts of semi-idioms as semantic arguments: 
in these cases not the form, but the meaning of the head of a phrase is 
uniquely specified.


\begin{description}
  \item[V=rekenen, XP=op de overwinning] {\em  Hij rekende op de overwinning}.
The phrase {\em  op de overwinning} is not (as a whole)  an argument of {\em  
rekenen} since the presence of the preposition {\em  op} inside this PREPP
is determined by 
the verb  {\em  rekenen}. In fact, the NP {\em  de overwinning} is here an 
argument (though this cannot be concluded on the basis of this test). 
\end{description}

\end{description}

\subsection{Ergativity}

\subsubsection{Ergativity of Verbs}
This subsection contains criteria to distinguish ergative from nonergative 
verbs. 

Each criterion has been marked as being {\em  weak} or {\em strong}, indicating
the importance  of the criterion to determine ergativity. Violations of
weak criteria do not make it impossible to assign a given verb to a certain 
class. Violations of strong criteria, however, do make this impossible, unless
independent reasons can be found and motivated to account for the deviancy.

Ergative verbs are verbs with thetavp=vp010 or thetavp=vp012, with an NP
argument.
The criteria hold for verbs with these thetavps and a sentential 
or closedxpprop argument
(e.g. raising verbs) as well.

\begin{description}
  \item[auxiliary(weak)(D)] If a verb is conjugated with {\em  zijn}, then it is
ergative. If it is conjugated with {\em  hebben}, it is not ergative.
  \item[Passivization(strong)(U)] If a verb can be passivized, then it is not
ergative.
  \item[Dyadic verbs(strong)(D)] If the indirect object can precede the other
argument, then the verb is ergative.
Example
 \begin{description}
  \item[] ..omdat {\em  de man} {\bf het boek} beviel 
\end{description}

The indirect object argument {\em de man} can precede
the other argument {\bf het boek} in this example, hence the verb 
{\em  bevallen} is ergatief. 

\item[Thematic role(weak)(U)] 
The argument of ergative verbs bears the thematic role
{\em Theme} or {\em Patiens}. This implies that it undergoes `movement' 
(in a very abstract and vague sense).

\item[Thematic role(strong, but vague)(U)] 
If an argument of a verb bears the thematic role {\em Agent}, then the verb is
not ergative. 

\item[Participle used attributively (strong)(U)] 

If the passive participle of a one-place verb can occur as an attributive 
modifier of some noun N, N being a noun that can occur normally as the
subject of the verb, then the verb is ergative. If this is not possible, then
the verb is not ergative. 

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em De jongen valt}, {\em De gevallen jongen},   hence {\em vallen}  
              is ergative
  \item[] {\em  De boek zijn aangekomen}, {\em  De aangekomen boeken}, hence
              {\em aankomen} is ergative
  \item[] {\em  De jongens dansen}, {\em *De gedanste jongens}, hence {\em  
dansen} is not ergative
   \item[] {\em  De mannen werken}, {\em  *de gewerkte mannen}, hence
{\em  werken} is not ergative.
\end{description}

A similar test can be used for dyadic ergative verbs. In Dutch the contexts are 
then: {\em  de indobj V$_{part}$ N}. 

Examples:

\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em Het boek valt mij op}, {\em  het mij opgevallen boek}, hence
{\em opvallen} is ergative.
  \item[] {\em  De resultaten vallen mij mee}, {\em  de mij meegevallen 
resultaten}, hence {\em  meevallen} is ergative
  \item[] {\em De man koopt het boek}, {\em  * De het boek gekocht(e) man}, 
hence
{\em  kopen} is not ergative.
       
\end{description}


\item[{\em -er/-aar}-formation(strong)(D)] 
If a word ending in {\em  -er/-aar} can be derived from V 
with the meaning {\em  someone who Vs (by profession)}, then V is not an 
ergative verb.  If it is not possible, then the verb probably is ergative.

Examples
\begin{description}
  \item[vallen] {\em *valler}, hence {\em  vallen} probably is ergative. 
  \item[aankomen] {\em  *aankomer}, hence {\em  aankomen}  probably is ergative. 
  \item[dansen] {\em  danser}, hence {\em  dansen} is not ergative
  \item[werken] {\em  werker}, hence {\em  werken} is not ergative
\end{description}

\item[Reflexives(weak)(D,?S,?E)] Reflexives (and reciprocals)
cannot appear very easily as an 
argument of an ergative verb if they are bound by the subject, cf.
{\em  ?Hij beviel zichzelf, ?Hij viel zichzelf op, ?Hij viel zichzelf mee,
*Hij schoot zichzelf te binnen, *Hij leek zichzelf ziek, *zij lijken elkaar 
ziek}. With nonergative verbs there are no problems whatsoever:
{\em  hij zag zichzelf, hij hoorde zichzelf, de katten roken elkaar} etc.
For some verbs reflexives are difficult for semantic reasons, e.g. {\em  ?hij 
ontmoette zichzelf}, but notice that with the ergative examples given above
there is no semantic reason whatsoever for their strangeness.

\end{description}

\subsection{The treatment of {\em  het, er} and extraposed sentences}

Sentential arguments of verbs can occur in principle as `subject arguments',
as `complement' arguments or as `prepositional object arguments'. They
are usually realized syntactically in a special way. This section describes
the treatment of these phenomena in the Rosetta3 Grammar of Dutch.

\subsubsection{Sentential subject arguments}

Sentential subject arguments cannot be realized as subjects in the 
grammar of Dutch. A sentential argument that must function as a subject
can be realized in one of the following two ways:
\begin{description}
\item[leftdislocrel] (main clauses only) The sentence is put in a special 
relation called {\em leftdislocrel} (in the beginning of the sentence)
and the subject position 
is either empty or occupied by the DEMPRO {\em  dat}:
\begin{description}
  \item leftdislocrel/[Dat hij vervelend is](, subjrel/dat) verbaast mij  
  \item leftdislocrel/[dat hij ziek is]( subjrel/dat) impliceert dat hij niet 
aanwezig kan zijn.
\end{description}
Such sentences are impossible or very marginal in subordinate clauses:

\begin{description}
  \item[] *hij zei dat dat hij vervelend is(, dat) mij verbaast
  \item[] ?hij zegt dat dat hij ziek is(, dat) impliceert dat hij niet aanwezig kan 
zijn.
\end{description}
 
 
\item[extraposrel] The sentence is extraposed (to the end of the sentence)
and bears the relation {\em  extraposrel}.
In this case the subject position is occupied by the pronoun {\em  het}:

\begin{description}
  \item[] subjrel/Het verbaast mij extraposrel/dat hij vervelend is  
\end{description}

\end{description}

Notice that sentential subject arguments can occur only with verbs that have
thetavp set to  vp100, vp120, or vp123.
Typical examples: {\em  verbazen, ergeren, interesseren, boeien}.

The same type of behavior is found with sentential subject arguments of
adjectives.

\subsubsection{Sentential complement arguments}

Sentential complement arguments are  always extraposed to the position 
bearing the relation {\em  extraposrel} (unless they are subject to 
Verb-raising). Depending on the verbpattern the pronoun 
{\em het} occurs as an
object. If the verbpattern of the verb contains the specification `HET'
(as in syn{\em HET}THATSENT, syn{\em HET}OPENOMTESENT) {\em  het} occurs,
if the verbpattern does not contain such a specification no {\em  het}
occurs. {\bf Notice that this object {\em  het} is realized as a subject
of verbs with thetavp=vp010 or vp012!}. If no {\em het} occurs for
verbs with thetavp =vp010, vp012, the `subject position' will contain 
{\em  er} in the surface.

Some examples:

\begin{description}
  \item[vp120, het] Hij betreurt objrel/{\em  het}  extraposrel/[dat hij ziek is] 
  \item[vp120, no het] Hij vindt extraposrel/[dat hij gelijk heeft]
  \item[vp012, het] subjrel/{\em Het } lijkt mij extraposrel/[dat hij gelijk 
heeft]
  \item[vp010, no het] {\em Er} is gebleken extraposrel/[dat hij ziek was]
  \item[vp010, het] {\em  Het} schijnt dat hij gelijk heeft
\end{description}


\subsubsection{Sentential Prepositional Object Arguments}

Sentential prepositional arguments are always extraposed. They leave {\em  het}
as an object of the preposition behind. This {\em het} will be turned into
{\em  er} by the normal rules. Examples:

\begin{description}
  \item[] Hij rekent {\em  er}op extraposrel/[dat hij mag komen]
  \item[] Hij gaat {\em  er}van uit extraposrel/[dat dit mogelijk is]
\end{description}

\subsection{The treatment of {\em  met}}
\label{met}

Prepositional phrases headed by {\em  met} do not have a uniform treatment,
but must be partitioned into a number of  different classes. This
section is intended to clarify this area somewhat. 

For the following verbs it has been indicated in the current dictionaries
of Rosetta that they can (or, in certain cases, must) be used in combination
with a prepositional phrase headed by {\em  met}. 

They should not all be treated uniformly. 
They can be classified into five groups:


\begin{description}
  \item[instrumental, optional] {\em  antwoorden met, iets betalen met, zwaaien met}
  \item[instrumental, subcategorized] {\em  ?beginnen met, ?eindigen met, besluiten met,
                    openen met}
  \item[subject oriented comitative] {\em   met iemand afwisselen, iets met iemand bespreken,  
met iemand corresponderen , met iemand onderhandelen, iets met iemand
overeenkomen, overeenkomen met iets, met iemand twisten, met iemand spreken} 
  \item[object oriented comitative] {\em  iemand verbinden met iemand, iets combineren met, 
iets met iets verenigen, iets met iets vergelijken}
  \item[prepositional objects] {\em  zich amuseren met, zich bemoeien met, zich 
bezighouden met, iemand feliciteren met, iemand helpen met, instemmen met,
ophouden met, zich vleien met, beginnen met, eindigen met} and perhaps
{\em ?zich met iemand meten, zich met iemand onderhouden, ?zich met 
iemand verstaan}.
\end{description}


These groups can be characterized as follows:

\begin{description}
  \item[instrumental, optional] 
The cases involved are instrumental modification that are optional. 
The 
prepositional phrase can be headed by other prepositions indicating manner,
e.g. {\em  op de volgende manier} or by adverbial phrases such as {\em  zo}:
{\em  Hij antwoordde (met de volgende woorden/zo/op de volgende manier)}.
The object of the preposition must be an object that can be used as an 
instrument. As a consequence, the fact that these verbs can be used
with such prepositional phrases should not be indicated in the verbpatterns.
  \item[instrumental, subcategorized] These cases involve instrumental 
modifications
that the verb is subcategorized for. They must occur in the structure. 
(cf. {\em  *Hij besloot}, {\em  Hij besloot met de volgende woorden},
{\em  *Hij opende}, {\em  Hij opende met de volgende woorden}. The 
prepositional phrase can be replaced by other prepositional phrases
(cf. {\em Hij besloot op de volgende manier}) and by adverbial phrases
({\em  Hij opende zo}). 
At the moment it is not possible yet to specify that an instrumental adverb
or prepositional phrase must be present in a verbpattern, but there
is no problem in adding such as pattern.\footnote{The status of verbs such as
{\em  beginnen met, eindigen met} is not fully clear. Is the 
{\em met}-phrase obligatorily present? Must a distinction between
instrumental adverb and prepositional object really be made? 
Notice that with these verbs 
the intended use here is as illustrated in: {\em  beginnen met deze woorden}, 
not as in: {\em 
beginnen met het werk}.} 
  \item[subject oriented comitative] These are cases where the {\em  met}-
phrase can be absent. The semantic type of the object of the preposition must
be compatible with the type of the subject. In certain cases
a singular (non collective non mass) 
subject can occur only if this prepositional phrase is present.
(cf. {\em ?Jan kwam dit overeen, Jan kwam dit overeen met 
Piet, Jan en Piet kwamen dit overeen}). It involves co-arguments of the 
subject.

A criterion to distinguish this class is that it must be possible to
paraphrase a sentence of the form
{\em A V X met B  Y} (X,Y arbitrary strings; A,B NPs)  as

{\em A en B V X Y} (with the number of the verb adapted appropriately).


In addition, a local translation of the {\em met}-phrase must be possible
(i.e. the translation of {\em  met} must be independent of the relevant verb).
These cases should be dealt with as `subject-oriented comitative phrases',
not as prepositional objects. (see the attribute {\em  adjuncts}).

  \item[object oriented comitative] 
The same as above, but the relation is now between the {\em  met}-phrase
and
the direct object. A sentence of the form 

{\em W V X A Y met B Z} (W,X,Y,Z  arbitrary strings; A direct object NP)
should be paraphrasable as {\em W V X A en B Y Z}    

These cases should be dealt with as `object-oriented comitative phrases',
not as prepositional objects. (see the attribute {\em  adjuncts}).


  \item[prepositional objects] These are cases of prepositional objects
headed by {\em  met}.

The status of {\em  zich met iemand meten, zich met iemand onderhouden, 
zich met iemand verstaan} is not fully clear. They appear to involve 
co-arguments, but the prepositional phrases cannot be left out, and the
paraphrase suggested to identify co-argument does not yield well-formed
results ({\em  *Jan en Piet meetten zich}, {\em  ??Jan en Piet 
onderhielden zich}, {\em  *Jan en Piet
verstonden zich}). Maybe they should be analyzed as co-arguments with 
additional restrictions, perhaps imposed by the presence of {\em  zich}.
 

\end{description}



\subsection{Other cases}

\begin{description} 
\item[1. DONP\_OPENTOSENT vs. CLOSEDTOSENT]
The following tests can be used to distinguish these two patterns

\begin{description}
  \item[1.1 Passive Test(U)] (Chomsky 1965:22) Given a structure of the form 
NP1 V1 NP2 {\em to} V2 NP3. Determine whether the verbpattern of V1 is
synDONP\_OPENTOSENT or synCLOSEDTOSENT.
Replace
V2 by a verb that can be passivized (if V2 can be passivized, it can be 
replaced by itself). Form structures of the form:
\begin{description}
  \item[] NP1 V1 NP3 to be V2-{\em ed} by NP2 
\end{description}

If this structure is well-formed and has 
the same truth-values  as the original
sentence (for any NP2, NP3), then V1 has pattern synCLOSEDTOSENT; otherwise
it has pattern synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.


Example:
\begin{description}
  \item[expect] I expected the doctor to examine the boy \to\\
                 I expected the boy to be examined by the doctor (OK)\\
                 I expected the boy to buy the  ball \to
                 I expected the ball to be bought by the boy (OK):
{\em Expect} has verbpattern {\em synCLOSEDTOSENT}

  \item[persuade] I persuaded the doctor to examine the boy \to
                  I persuaded the boy to be examined by the doctor (OK, 
different meaning)\\
                 I persuaded the boy to buy the  ball \to
                 *I persuaded the ball to be bought by the boy:\\
{\em persuade} has verbpattern {\em synDONP\_OPENTOSENT}
\end{description}

 
  \item[1.2 Selection Restriction Test(U)] Given a structure of the form 
NP1 V1 NP2 {\em to} V2 NP3. Check whether V1 imposes selectional restrictions
upon NP2 by using the Selection Restriction Test from section~\ref{arghood}. If it 
does, then NP2 is an argument of V1, hence V1 has pattern synDONP\_OPENTOSENT;
if it does not, then NP2 is not an argument of V1, hence V1 has pattern
synDONPCLOSEDTOSENT.

Examples. 
Consider the sentences {\em  I believe the doctor to examine the boy} and
{\em  I persuaded the doctor to examine the boy} (V1=believe, persuade;
NP2 = the doctor)
\begin{description}
  \item[Weather-It]  {\em  I believe it to rain}: OK, hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
                      {\em  I persuaded it to rain}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT
  \item[Expletives] {\em  I believed there to be a problem}:  OK, hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
                    {\em  I persuaded there to be a problem}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT
  \item[Extraposition-it] {\em  I believe it to be clear that this is correct}: 
OK, hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
{\em  I persuaded it to be clear that this is correct}: *, 
hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT
  \item[Idiom Chunks] {\em  I believed the cat to have Mary's tongue}: OK,
hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
{\em  I persuaded the cat to have Mary's tongue}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.
  \item[Other type] {\em  I believed the music to get louder}: OK, hence
synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
{\em  I persuaded the music to get louder}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.
\end{description}

\item[1.3 Replacement test(U)] Given a structure of the form 
NP1 V1 NP2 {\em to} V2 NP3. Check whether the part NP2 {\em  to} V2 NP3
can be replaced by finite sentence of the form 
{\em that} NP2 V2-{\em  s} NP3 (with preservation of grammaticality, and meaning). 
Check furthermore, if the part {\em  to} V2 NP3 can be replaced by 
a finite sentences of the form {\em  that he/she/it (should)} V2 NP3 or
{\em P the fact} {\em  that he/she/it (should)} V2 NP3, P some (simple) 
preposition
(with 
preservation of grammaticality and meaning). This can yield the following 
results:
\begin{description}
\item[] \mbox{}\\
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|l|}
\hline
Replacement 1 & Replacement 2 & Comments\\
\hline
possible      & impossible    & synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
impossible    & possible      & synDONP\_OPENTOSENT\\
impossible    & impossible    & nothing can be said\\
possible      & possible      & cannot occur. \\
\hline
\end{tabular} 
\end{description}

If the last situation does occur, it probably implies that V1 is in fact 
ambiguous.

Examples.
\begin{description}
  \item[V1=believe] {\em  I believe John to be ill},
{\em  I believe that John is ill}, {\em  *I believe John (of the fact) that he is ill} : synCLOSEDTOSENT
  \item[V1=persuade] {\em  I persuade John to come}, {\em *I persuade (of the 
fact) that John comes}, {\em  I persuade John (of the fact) that he should 
come }: synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.
\end{description}

For other languages, the replacement sentences must of course 
be adapted to the specific 
language.
\end{description}

\item[2. DONP\_OPENADJPPROP vs. CLOSEDADJPPROP]
The following tests can be used (see also section~\ref{sc}):

\begin{description}
  \item[2.1 Meaning(U)] In sentences of the form {\em  NP1 V NP2 AP} the 
interpretation of the AP is {\em resultative}  if V has pattern DONP\_
OPENADJPPROP, the interpretation is stative or resultative if V has pattern
CLOSEDADJPPROP. The resultative interpretation 
can be paraphrased as {\em  NP1 V NP2 so that/until
NP2 became AP} for synDONP\_OPENADJPPROP.  For synCLOSEDADJPPROP the 
resultative interpretation can be paraphrased as {\em  NP1 V so that/until
NP2 became AP}.
The stative interpretation can be paraphrased as
{\em  NP1 V that NP2 is AP}. Cf. {\em He painted the door green} \to
{\em  He painted the door, so that it(the door) became green} vs.
{\em  He considers her smart} \to {\em  he considers that she is smart}.
For both examples the other paraphrase is completely out:
{\em  *He painted that the door was green} and {\em  *He considers (her) so that
she is smart}.

\item[2.2 Replacement by THAT-sent(U)] If NP2 AP (in NP1 V NP2 AP)
can be replaced by a finite sentence
introduced by  {\em  that}({\em  dat, que}), then V has pattern 
synCLOSEDADJPPROP. 

\item[2.3 Selection Restrictions] A verb V can impose selectional restrictions
upon NP2 (in a structure {\em NP1 V NP2 AP}) if the pattern is 
synDONP\_OPENADJPPROP, but not if the pattern is synCLOSEDADJPPROP. 
\end{description}



\item[3. Locative/Directional Argument vs. Prepositional Object] The following 
tests can be used:
\begin{description}
  \item[3.1 Replacement by ADVP(U)] A locative or directional PREPP argument can be
replaced by locative adverbial phrases such as {\em  thuis}, {\em  hier},
{\em  ergens}, {\em  here}, {\em  zuidwaarts}, {\em  home}, etc. Prepositional
objects cannot. 
  \item[3.2 Replacement by other Ps(U)] In a locative or directional 
PREPP the head PREP
can be replaced freely by all other locative or directional PREPs. In a 
prepositional object these replacement possibilities are absent or severely
restricted.
  \item[3.3 Postverbal Position(D)] Prepositional objects can occur in
a postverbal position in Dutch, locative and directional arguments cannot.
cf. {\em  *hij heeft lange tijd gewoond in Amsterdam} vs. {\em  ..omdat hij
keek naar het televisieprogramma}. Locative and directional PPs in a postverbal
position, however, tend to get better if they are `heavier', or more complex,
e.g. {\em  ?..omdat hij lange tijd gewoond heeft in de stad die beroemd is om 
haar kazen}, so use short PPs to test this property.
\end{description}

\item[4. Locative/Directional Argument vs. Locative Adverbial] The following tests
can be used:
\begin{description}
  \item[4.1 Postverbal PREPPs(D)] Locative (and directional)
arguments cannot appear to the right of
the verb. Locative adverbials, however, can.
Examples:
The sentence {\em  Hij is in de sloot gevallen} is ambiguous. In its most 
natural reading it means that he fell from the bank of some ditch into this 
ditch. Under a second reading it means that he fell while being in a ditch.
Under the first reading the PREPP {\em  in de sloot} is a directional argument 
of the verb {\em  vallen}, in the second example it is a locative adverbial.
When the PREPP is put to the right of the verb {\em  gevallen}, the first 
reading disappears: {\em  hij is gevallen in de sloot} can only mean that he
fell while being in the ditch. Cf. also {\em  *Hij heeft lange tijd 
gewoond in Amsterdam,
*Hij zou verblijven in Amsterdam, *Hij was gegaan naar Amsterdam}

  \item[4.2 Neutral Sentential Accent(D)] The neutral sentential accent falls on the
PREPP/ADVP if this PREPP/ADVP is a locative or directional argument. 
It falls on the verb if  the PREPP is a locative adverbial. The example used 
above shows this directly: {\em  hij is in de SLOOT gevallen} (directional 
argument) vs. {\em  hij is in de sloot GEVALLEN} (locative adverbial).

 \item[4.3 Obligatoriness(U)] If some locative or directional PREPP is 
obligatorily present if some verb V is present, then it is an argument of V.
Cf. {\em  Hij verbleef lange tijd *(in Amsterdam), hij is *(naar huis) gefietst}

  \item[4.4 Directional PPs and ADVPs(U)] are always arguments or adjuncts
(except in idioms).
\end{description}

\item[5. synPREPNP vs. synPREPOPENNPPROP(D)]
(similarly: synDONP\_PREPNP vs. synDONP\_PREPOPENNPPROP). These can be 
distinguished in the following way:
\begin{description}
  \item[5.1 Absence of article(D)] If the NP can consist of such words as 
{\em  directeur}, {\em boekhouder}, {\em professor} etc. 
(which normally require the presence of an article in singular)
without there being an article, and such bare Noun NPs can occur as
predicative NPs in such sentences as {\em  hij/zij/het is NP} as well,
then the pattern is synPREPOPENNPPROP
(synDONP\_PREPOPENNPPROP). 
Otherwise it is  synPREPNP (synDONP\_PREPNP).

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[benoemen] {\em Ik benoem hem tot directeur}, {\em  Hij is directeur}: 
OK, hence 
synDONP\_PREPOPENNPPROP
  \item[kijken] {\em  *Ik kijk naar directeur}, hence synPREPNP.
\end{description}


\end{description}
\item[6. synMEASUREPHRASE vs. synNP] The following tests can be used:
\begin{description}
  \item[6.1 Measure test] If NP2 in NP1 V (NP3) NP2 can be replaced by some NP that
does not express a measure, then the pattern of the verb is not 
syn(IONP\_)MEASUREphrase,
but syn(IONP\_DO)NP. Cf. {\em  Hij betaalde (hem) drie gulden} and 
{\em  Hij betaalde (hem) het geld}, hence   pattern= synIONP\_DONP.
{\em  De biefstuk kost drie gulden} and {\em  *De biefstuk kost het geld},
hence pattern = synMEASUREPHRASE.
Cf. also the verb {\em  wegen}, which is ambiguous between a 
pattern synMEASUREPHRASE (as in {\em De os weegt drie kilo}) and the pattern
synDONP (cf. {\em  De boer weegt de os}).
  \item[6.2 Passivization] If a structure of the form NP1 V NP2 can be passivized,
then the pattern of the verb is not synMEASUREPHRASE.
cf. {\em  Er werd drie gulden betaald} vs. {\em  *Er werd drie gulden gekost}.
Cf. also {\em  Er werd drie kilo gewogen} (* in the measurephrase 
interpretation) vs. {\em  De os werd gewogen}.
  \item[6.3 Thematic role] The thematic role of the subject of a verb with pattern
= synMEASUREPHRASE usually is  not {\em Agent}. If 
it is, then the verb probably does not have pattern 
synMEASUREPHRASE. 
  \item[6.4 Progressive Test] Verbs with pattern= synMEASUREPHRASE are usually 
stative and cannot appear in the {\em  progressive} (E) or in the 
{\em  aan het}-construction (D), cf. {\em  *he is costing three pounds}, 
{\em  *hij is drie gulden aan het kosten}, {\em  hij is drie kilo aan het wegen}
(unambiguous), vs. {\em  Hij is drie gulden aan het betalen}, {\em  Hij is drie 
ossen aan het wegen}.
\end{description}

\item[7. synOPENOMTESENT vs. synOPENTESENT] (similarly, 
{\em synDONP\_OPENOMTESENT} vs. {\em  synDONP\_OPENTESENT}, etc.).

The pattern is {\em  synOPENOMTESENT} if the conjunction {\em  om} can be
present but need not be present. The pattern is {\em  synOPENTESENT} if the 
conjunction {\em  om} cannot be present. If the conjunction {\em  om} is
present obligatorily, then either the relevant sentence is not an argument
(use other tests to determine this), 
or the verb has pattern {\em  synOPENOMTESENTPROOBJ} (in that case some 
additional argument or adverbial inside the infinitive must be nonovert). 

Summarized in a table:

\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
can {\em  om be present}? & can {\em  om} be absent & pattern & example verb \\
\hline
\hline
yes & yes & synOPENOMTESENT & {\em proberen}\\
yes & no  & synOPENOMTESENTPROOBJ & {\em  dienen}\\
no  & yes & synOPENTESENT & {\em  beweren}\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

In general there is a preference to leave out {\em  om} if the infinitival
complement is adjacent to a finite verb form (or at least if the main clause
is rather short). Therefore use perfect tenses to test whether {\em  om}
can be present or not, wherever this is possible.

Example sentences that illustrate the behavior of the relevant verbs:

\begin{description}
  \item[proberen] {\em  Hij heeft geprobeerd {\bf om} dat te doen},
                  {\em  Hij heeft geprobeerd dat te doen} 
  \item[dienen] {\em  De kurketrekker dient {\bf om} flessen mee te openen},
                {\em  *De kurketrekker dient flessen mee te openen},
                {\em  *De kurketrekker dient} (infinitival clause is 
obligatorily present, hence it is an argument)
  \item[beweren] {\em  *Hij heeft beweerd {\bf  om} dit gedaan te hebben},
                 {\em  Hij heeft beweerd dit gedaan te hebben}
\end{description}

\end{description}



\subsection{Small Clauses}
\label{sc}


This section supplies some tests to distinguish between three kinds of 
small clauses. I will call them (rather arbitrarily)
here the A-type, the B-type and the
C-type. The following examples illustrate these three different
types:

\begin{description}
  \item[A] hij at zijn bord leeg
  \item[B] hij verfde de deur groen (Hij scheerde de man glad)
  \item[C] hij vond haar aardig
\end{description}

The following differences can be pointed out:
\begin{description}
  \item[A] Properties of type A: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item 
        The verb {\em eten} does not impose selectional restrictions upon {\em zijn bord}. 
  \item The predication has a `dynamic' (resultative) interpretation, 
        which can be 
        paraphrased as {\em hij at zodat/totdat zijn bord leeg raakte}
  \item In the paraphrase given above the direct object from the original
        sentence cannot be a direct object in the paraphrase:
        {\em  *hij at zijn bord zodat/totdat het leeg raakte}
  \item It appears to be the case that this kind of modification can 
appear with all non-ergative intransitive activity verbs.
  \item Instead of {\em het bord} a reflexive pronoun can occur, but 
        only in the form {\em zich}, not in the form {\em zichzelf}, cf.
        {\em  Hij at zich(*zelf) vol}, {\em hij rende zich(*zelf) dood}
  \item One cannot question the adjective with the adverb {\em  hoe}:
        {\em *A: Hoe at hij zijn bord? B: Leeg.}
  \item Omissability: Only leaving out both the `direct object' and the 
predication yields a well-formed result:
        {\em *Hij at zijn bord, * Hij at leeg, Hij at}. 
  \item For this construction the following implication does not hold:
        {\em Hij at zijn bord leeg} \implies {\em Hij at zijn bord}.
        In many cases it is not even possible to form a well-formed sentence
        by omitting the predication, cf. e.g. 
        {\em Hij danste zijn longen leeg} vs. {\em * Hij danste zijn longen.}
\end{itemize}

  \item[B] Properties of type B: 
\begin{itemize} 
  \item The verb imposes selectional restrictions upon the object NP
        (cf. {\em *Hij scheert de tafel glad})
  \item The predication has a `dynamic' (resultative) interpretation, 
paraphrasable as {\em hij verfde de deur zodat/totdat deze groen werd}
  \item The direct object from the original
        sentence can remain a direct object in the paraphrase given above:
        {\em  hij verfde de deur zodat/totdat deze groen werd}
  \item This construction is possible for verbs that can take a direct object as 
their only argument (apart from the subject) and satisfy certain other 
conditions. The nature of these other conditions is not fully clear
(`affectedness' of the direct object has sometimes been suggested)
In any case, it has to be specified for each individual verb whether or not
it can take a resultative adjunct as described here, cf. 
 {\em * hij kocht het boek groen} (in the intended interpretation),
{\em *hij zag de man kaal}).

Many transitive verbs can also be used in combination with a type A 
construction, provided that the verb expresses an activity. The direct object
is left out(or can the relevant verbs be used intransitively as well?) 
and replaced by a type A small clause,e.g. {\em  Hij kocht de winkel leeg}.

  \item The direct object can be replaced by the reflexive pronoun 
        {\em zichzelf}: {\em Hij verfde zichzelf groen}
  \item Questioning of the adjective by the adverb {\em hoe} is possible:
        {\em A: Hoe verfde hij de deur? B: Groen.}
  \item Omissability: the predication can be omitted on its own:
        {\em Hij verfde de deur}. The direct object cannot be omitted on its 
own: {\em *Hij verfde groen}.  The predication and the direct object cannot 
both be omitted at the same time. One cannot show this when using the verb
{\em  verven}, because this verb can be used intransitively as well, but it can 
be shown by taking a verb such as {\em  scheren} as in
        {\em Hij scheerde de man glad} vs. {\em hij scheerde de man} vs. 
         {\em *Hij scheerde glad} vs {\em *hij scheerde}. 
  \item For this construction the following implication holds:
        {\em  Hij scheerde de man glad} \implies {\em  Hij scheerde de man}
\end{itemize}

  \item[C] Properties of type C: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item No selection restrictions are imposed by the verb on the
direct object  (cf. {\em  Hij vindt het interessant dat dat gebeurt},
{\em Hij vindt het koud}).  
  \item The predication has a `static' interpretation. (cf. the 
        paraphrase {\em  Hij vindt dat zij aardig is}).

  \item The direct object from the original
sentence cannot remain a direct object in the paraphrase given above:
{\em  *Hij vindt haar dat zij aardig is}

  \item The construction appears to be limited to a small number of verbs
  \item The direct object can be replaced by the reflexive pronoun {\em  
zichzelf}
  \item Questioning of the predicate by the adverb {\em  hoe} is possible:
         {\em  Hoe vind je haar?}.  
  \item Omissability: 
Neither the direct object, nor the predication, nor both can be omitted:
{\em *Hij vindt haar} (* under the intended interpretation), 
{\em *Hij vindt aardig, *Hij vindt}.
   \item The implication {\em  Hij vindt haar aardig} \implies {\em  Hij vindt 
haar} does not hold, and cannot even be constructed in many cases.
\end{itemize}

\end{description}



In Rosetta these constructions are treated in the following manner:

\begin{description}
  \item[A] The direct object and the precication together form a complex
       adjunct (a small clause adjunct with an overt subject) 
        that can appear with activity verbs 
  \item[B] The direct object is an argument of the verb. The predication 
          is an adjunct that can appear with the verb if the verb is specified
so as to allow it.
  \item[C] The direct object and the predication together form a complex
argument (a small clause argument with an overt subject) of the verb
{\em  vinden}
\end{description}


By adopting these analyses the properties of the constructions mentioned above 
are accounted for.

Apart from the types mentioned one can have the so-called {\em bepaling van 
gesteldheid} that can be added freely and that can 
modify either the subject or the
direct object
(e.g.. {\em Hij at het vlees naakt} (i.e.
{\em hij at het vlees terwijl hij naakt was})
and {\em Hij at het vlees rauw} (with the interpretation: 
{\em hij at het vlees toen het rauw was})). 

These can be added freely and there are no specifications relating to these
constructions on verbs.


\subsection{Postpositions en Particles and how to distinguish them}
\label{prepandpart}

In structures of the form {\em ...Prep NP P V...} or {\em ...NP P V...} it 
must be determined whether P is particle or a postposition/second part
of a circumposition.

This section lists some criteria to determine this. Most of these criteria have 
been derived from van Riemsdijk (1973,1978) and Huybregts (1979).

\subsubsection{Structures of the type ...Prep NP P V...}


The following are typical examples. Under 1 examples have been listed where
P must be analyzed as a particle. Under 2 the examples where P must be
analyzed as the second part of a circumposition have been listed.

\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
\# & Prep  & NP        & P    & V\\
\hline
\hline
  & voor  & een boeman   & door & gaan\\
1 & tegen & de reis      & op   & zien\\
  & op    & z'n intuitie & af   & gaan\\
  & tegen & de jeugd     & uit  & varen\\
\hline
  & naar  & Amsterdam & toe  & gaan\\
  & onder & de brug   & door & lopen\\
2 & tegen & de berg   & op   & klimmen\\
  & op    & de kust   & af   & gaan\\
  & tegen & de kade   & aan  & varen\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

The following criteria can be used to determine whether P is a particle
or the second part of a circumposition:

\begin{description}
  \item[Circumposition(strong)] If the combination Prep...P is not a 
circumposition of Dutch, then P is a particle. This is a very hard criterion,
but it is hardly ever useful.
  \item[Path PP(weak)] If P is the second part of a circumposition, then the
combination Prep NP P must be a `path'-PP (directional). 
Usually the NP must indicate a potential location, which implies often that
it must be concrete, or that it must be concrete/abstract depending on the 
concreteness/abstractness of the object to be located.
The verb must express `movement'.
However, this is not an absolute requirement: it will fail in particular if 
idiomatization 
has occurred.
  \item[PPoverV(strong)] If the order P V Prep NP is possible, then P is 
a particle. If this order is not possible, then P is the second part of a
circumposition.

Examples: {\em  *dat hij doorging onder de brug; *dat hij toeging 
naar Amsterdam; *dat hij opklom tegen de berg} vs. {\em  dat hij afging op 
z'n intuitie; dat hij uitvoer tegen de jeugd; dat hij opzag tegen de reis}

\item[Waar Prep(strong)] If questions of the form {\em Waar Prep ... P V} 
are possible, then P is a particle. If this is not possible, then P is
the second part of a circumposition.

Examples {\em  *waaronder is hij doorgegaan; *waarnaar is hij toegegaan;
*waartegen is hij opgeklommen; *waartegen is hij aangevaren} vs. 
{\em  waartegen is hij uitgevaren; waartegen heeft hij opgezien}. 
Also notice that the question {\em  Waarop is hij afgegaan} 
can be answered by {\em  op z'n intuitie}, but not by {\em  op de kust}.

\item[auxiliary] If P is the second part of a circumposition, and the verb
is diadic, then it uses the auxiliary verb {\em  zijn}, not {\em  hebben}
to form perfect tenses. So, if the verb is diadic and it is conjugated
with the verb {\em  hebben}, then P is a particle.

Examples: (No minimal pair found yet to show this)

%{\em  Hij heeft de vrachtauto ingereden} (particle) vs. 
%{\em  hij is de vrachtauto ingereden} (postposition)

\item[Contrast(strong)] If a sentence of the type
{\em hij V er Prep P, niet Prep P} can be constructed, then P is
the second part of a circumposition
Cf. {\em  Hij klom ertegen op, niet  van af} (circumposition) vs.
{\em  *Hij zag ertegen op, niet van af} (particle)
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Structures of the form...NP P V...}

The following are typical examples. Under 1 examples have been listed where
P must be analyzed as a particle. Under 2 the examples where P must be
analyzed as a postposition have been listed.\footnote{The example {\em  de 
marathon uitlopen} is perhaps ambiguous. In this section the interpretation
`run the whole distance until the end' is intended, not the opposite 
interpretation `step out of the race before you have reached the finish'.}

\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
\#  & rest   & NP          & P    & V\\
\hline
\hline
 1 &        & de man      & op   & bellen\\
   &        & de marathon & uit & lopen\\
\hline
   &        & de winkel   & uit  & lopen\\
 2 &        & de berg     & op   & klimmen\\
   & iemand & de winkel   & uit  & werken\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

The following criteria can be used:

\begin{description}
  \item[Path PP(weak)] If P is a postposition, then the combination
NP P must be a `path'-PP (directional). 
Usually the NP must indicate a potential location, which implies often that
it must be concrete, or that it must be concrete/abstract depending on the 
concreteness/abstractness of the object to be located.
The verb must express `movement'.
However, this is not an absolute requirement: it will fail in particular if idiomatization 
has occurred.
  \item[R-relativization(strong)] If the NP can be relativized using
the relative pronoun {\em  waar}, then P is a postposition.
If this NP can only be relativized using {\em  die} or {\em  dat}, then P
is a particle.
Examples: {\em  *de man waar hij opbelde; *de marathon waar hij
uitliep} vs. {\em  de winkel waar hij uitliep; de berg waar hij opklom;
de winkel waar hij hem uit werkte}

Similarly, if the NP can be replaced by {\em  er}, then P is a postposition.
Cf. {\em Piet liep de winkel in, maar Karel liep {\bf er} uit}.

\item[auxiliary(strong)] If P is a postposition, and the verb is
diadic, then it is conjugated with {\em  zijn}, not with {\em  hebben}.

So, if the verb is diadic, and it is conjugated with {\em  hebben}, then
P is a particle.
Examples: {\em  Hij {\bf heeft} de marathon uitgelopen} (particle) vs.
{\em  Hij {\bf is  } de winkel uitgelopen} (postposition)

\item[`Double' objects(strong)] If structures of the form
NP$_{1}$ NP$_{2}$ P V can occur with triadic verbs, where
the first NP is a direct object, then P is a postposition
Example: {\em De directeur heeft 
de man(NP$_{1}$) de winkelNP$_{2}$ uit (P) gewerkt(V)}. 

One can test whether NP$_{1}$ is a direct object or not by checking whether
{\em  hij} can occur as a subject under passivization. If this is possible,
then this NP is a direct object, otherwise it is not.
Example: {\em  Hij werd (door de directeur) de winkel uitgewerkt}. 
In a sentence such as {\em  De directeur gaf de man het touw aan},
{\em  de man} is not a direct 
object, as can be shown by the fact that 
{\em  *Hij werd (door de 
directeur) het touw aangegeven} is ungrammatical.
(and {\em  aan} is a particle here). 

\item[Topicalisation] If the combination NP P can be preposed as a whole,
then P is a postposition. Examples: {\em  De winkel uit loop je niet zo
gemakkelijk} vs {\em  *de marathon uit loop je niet zo gemakkelijk}. 
This criterion is very strong, but the results of topicalization
are usually not fully grammatical, so  that a correct judgement can
be very difficult to give.
If the combination NP P cannot be preposed, nothing can be concluded.

\item[Prenominal participles(strong)] If the P plus the past participle of V
can occur as modifiers of a noun(of the same semantic type as NP), 
then P is a particle. If this is not
possible, then, strictly spoken, nothing can be concluded, but in most cases
P will then be a postposition.

Examples: {\em  de opgebelde man, de uitgelopen marathon, de ingelopen 
schoenen} vs.
{\em  *de uitgelopen winkel, *de opgeklommen berg, *het ingelopen bos}

\end{description}

\section{Other Syntactic Information}
\label{synt}

The following subsections contain rather detailed information about the
other syntactic properties that must be specified for each lexical entry.

\subsection{Verbs}

The following information on the syntax of verbs must be specified:



\begin{attributes}
      \att[req, env] These attributes are used to indicate the 
         {\em  polarity} properties of the verb. The attribute is a
set attribute, but only for historical reasons. Sensible values are
\begin{values}
\val[{[pospol, negpol, omegapol]}] The default value, indicating that no
relevant polarity properties hold.
\val[{[pospol]}] The verb requires (req) or creates (env) a positively polar
environment
\val[{[negpol]}] The verb requires (req) or creates (env) a negatively polar
environment
\end{values} 

The following tests can be used to determine polarity properties.

If a verb can replace V in the following sentences, then env={\em [negpol]}:

\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em Hij V een hand uit (te) steken} (idiomatic reading!!)
  \item[] {\em Hij V ook maar iets (te) doen }
\end{description}

   \att[controller] This attribute indicates what the controller of the subject
of an embedded infinitival complement is for this verb.
The values are:

      \begin{values}
	\val[subject] The subject is the controller
	\val[object] The object is the controller
        \val[indobj] The indirect object, or the NP in a prepositional phrase
headed by {\em aan}  or {\em voor} is the controller
        \val[prepobj] The object of the prepositional object PP is 
           the controller
        \val[none] No controller restriction is specified
      \end{values}

The relation mentioned is the relation before application of object-to-subject
transformations have applied (in passives, ergatives).

Tests to determine controllerhood: If a verb can replace V in 

\begin{description} 
  \item[] {\em hij V mij zich te gedragen}
  \item[] {\em hij V mij zich te scheren}
  \item[] {\em hij V mij z'n geduld te bewaren}
\end{description}

\noindent
then controller=subject.

If a verb can replace V in 

\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em ik V hem zich te gedragen}
  \item[] {\em ik V hem zich te scheren}
  \item[] {\em ik V hem z'n geduld te bewaren}
\end{description}
 
then controller = object, or indobj, depending on the relation of {\em  hem}

Similar tests can be constructed for controllers in prepositional objects.

In certain cases the determination of the controller is not so simple,
because the subject of the embedded infinitive
can be interpreted as referring to a group 
containing the controller. Sometimes, in addition, an interpretation
is possible where the referent of some other argument in the
clause belongs to this group, making the determination of the controller even 
more difficult.

These additional interpretations, however should not lead to additional
distinctions in the dictionaries for the attribute {\em controller}. 
The additional interpretations are dealt with in the Rosetta grammars.

In certain cases a verb can have either the indirect object or the subject
as controller, and this distinction correlates with a distinction in meaning.
An example is the verb {\em  zeggen}. When used in  a sentence such as
{\em Ik zei hem dit te willen doen} the subject is the controller and
the verb {\em  zeggen} must be interpreted as `mededelen'; however, when used
in a sentence such as {\em  Ik zei hem naar huis te gaan} the indirect object
is the controller and the verb {\em  zeggen} must be interpreted as
`opdragen'. In such cases a splitting up of this verb syntactically 
is necessary.

There are, however, also some verbs where either the object/indirect object
or the subject can be interpreted as  the controller without leading
to a different meaning of the verb. An example is the verb {\em  vragen}.
In a sentence such as {\em  Ik vroeg hem dit te doen} the indirect object is 
the controller, and in a sentence such as {\em  Ik vroeg hem dit te mogen doen}
the subject is the controller, but there is no (or at least a not very easily
discernible) difference in meaning between the two occurrences of {\em  vragen}.
 For such cases it might be convenient to allow for an additional value
for the attribute {\em  controller}, e.g. the value {\em  both}, which 
is to be interpreted in such a way that both arguments other than 
the infinitival clause argument can serve as a controller without leading
to a difference in meaning.

   \att[verbraiser] 
Used to indicate whether a verb allows, 
requires or disallows Verb Raising.

The values are:
\begin{values}
  \val[optionalVR] The verb allows Verb Raising
  \val[obligatoryVR] The verb requires Verb Raising
  \val[noVR] The verb disallows Verb Raising
\end{values}


A test to determine verbraiserhood (for verbs with thetavp=vp120, and taking
open sentential complements): 
If a (finite) verb can be inserted 
instead of V in

\begin{description}
\item[S1] {\em ..omdat hij het boek V (te) lezen}
\end{description}
\noindent
then this verb is either 

{\em optionalVR} or
{\em obligatoryVR}. If in addition the verb requires {\em  te} and 
can be inserted instead of V in
\begin{description}
\item[S2]  {\em ..omdat hij V het boek te lezen}
\end{description}
\noindent
it is {\em optionalVR}, otherwise 
{\em obligatoryVR}. If it cannot appear in the first context sketched it is 
{\em NoVR}.

Verbs taking closed sentential complements are obligatory Verbraisers.

For verbs with {\em  thetavp=vp123} the following contexts can be used 
analogously:

\begin{description}
\item[S1] {\em ..omdat hij de jongen het boek V (te) lezen}
\item[S2] {\em ..omdat hij de jongen V het boek te lezen}
\end{description}

For verbs with thetavp=vp012 the following contexts hold:
\begin{description}
\item[S1] {\em ..omdat hem het boek V (te) lezen}
\item[S2] {\em ..omdat hem V het boek te lezen}
\end{description}

If a verb allows {\em  te}, but does not (always) require it, then you must
usually split it up into two different verbs.


Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[thetavp=vp120, open S-compl]\mbox{}\\
\begin{description}
  \item[Proberen] {\em ..omdat hij het boek probeerde te lezen} and
                  {\em ..omdat hij probeerde het boek te lezen} are both 
correct, hence {\em optionalVR}
   \item[willen] {\em ..omdat hij het boek wilde lezen} correct, the verb does 
not allow a complement with {\em  te}, hence {\em obligatoryVR}
   \item[afspreken] {\em {\star} ..omdat hij het boek afsprak te lezen}, hence
{\em noVR}. 
\end{description}
\item[thetavp=vp123]\mbox{}\\
\begin{description} 
  \item[leren, no {\em  te}]  {\em ..omdat hij de jongen het boek leerde lezen},
is correct. The verb does not allow {\em  te} (cf. {\em {\star}..omdat hij de 
jongen het boek leerde te lezen}), hence {\em  obligatoryVR}
  \item[leren, with {\em  te}]
{\em {\star}..omdat hij de jongen het boek leerde  te lezen}, hence {\em noVR}
  \item[opdragen] {{\star}..omdat hij de jongen het boek opdroeg te lezen},
hence {\em noVR}
\end{description}
  \item[thetavp=vp012]\mbox{}\\
\begin{description}
  \item[meevallen] {\em  {\star}..omdat hem het boek meeviel te lezen}, hence
{\em noVR} (cf. {\em ..omdat het hem meeviel het boek te lezen})
\end{description}
\end{description}

Summarizing, in all tests two sentences are used, called S1 and S2.

\begin{description}
  \item[]\mbox{}\\ 
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
\hline
can occur in S1 & can occur in S2 & verbraiser\\
\hline
no              &   irrelevant    & NoVR\\
yes             &   yes           & OptionalVR\\
yes             &   no            & ObligatoryVR\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{description}

The so-called {\em  Third Construction} is not distinguished in Rosetta.
(It was considered ungrammatical). This construction shows a mix
of the properties of extraposed complements and complements that have
undergone Verb-Raising.  (See Den Besten \& Rutten, 1989)
It is generally analyzed as involving extraposition
plus preposing of arguments from the extraposed clause. The construction
can be recognized by the combination of the following properties:

\begin{itemize}
  \item one of the arguments  of the embedded  infinitive is to the left
          of the embedding verb
  \item the embedding verb does not show the IPP-effect (see below)
\end{itemize}

For some (ad-hoc) measures to deal with them in Rosetta, see the next 
attribute.

\subsubsection{Dependencies between attributes}

Certain attributes can have a significant value only if certain other 
attributes have been filled in a certain way. The following scheme
expresses such dependencies. In Rosetta these dependies have been used to
formulate a strategy of filling the dictionary. 

Filling the dictionary can be done along two dimensions. One can fill
all attributes of a lexical entry, and when this has been done, go  on
to the next entry, or one can fill one attribute\footnote{or a small number of 
related attributes} for a large number of
lexical entries, and then fill the next attribute for these lexical entries, 
etc.

It often was easier, or made more sense to adopt the second strategy. 
For these cases it was necessary to choose some order of filling the 
attributes. The scheme of dependencies has been used to supply such an order.

The scheme contains a number of dependencies that hold between attributes.
Each dependency is numbered. The dependency relation is indicated
by the symbol $\prec$. These dependencies should be read as follows:
 
\begin{description}
  \item[] $\alpha$ $\prec$ $\beta$ means that the meaningful values
for the attribute  $\beta$ depend to a large extent on the
value assigned to the attribute $\alpha$, so that it makes sense
to first fill the attribute $\alpha$, and only then, for the relevant
cases, the values for the attribute $\beta$.

\end{description}

The scheme (including the attributes {\em  bridge, tedeletion} 
that are not included yet in Rosetta):

\begin{description}
\item[1] thetavp $\prec$ synvps 
\item[2] thetavp $\prec$  CaseAssigner;  reflexivity
\item[3] synvps $\prec$ prepkey1; prepkey2; possvoices; Controller; Verbraiser
\item[4] Verbraiser $\prec$ IPP
\item[5] synvps $\prec$ Te-deletion; bridge

\end{description}

\subsubsection{Consistency Checks}

It is often the case that specifying some value for one attribute makes
certain values of some other attribute impossible, or very implausible.

In Rosetta it is possible to specify such constraints, and whenever the 
dictionary is updated, it is checked whether it satisfies these
constraints. If not, a warning (or error) message is given.

It is possible to specify two kinds of constraints. One kind of constraint
is intended for completely valid constraints. An other kind of constraint
is intended for constraints that are perhaps not fully true, but that are
in true in most cases. A list of the current constraints for the syntactic
categories BNOUN, BADJ and BADV in Dutch is given in appendix~\ref{constraints}.

Certain constraints are logically true, though others are just empirically true.
An example of a logically true constraint is the constraint that says
that the verbpatterns of a verb with thetavp=vp120 must be taken from
the set of verbpatterns compatible with thetavp=vp120.
An example of an empirically true constraint is the constraint that says
that verbs that take particles do not allow or require verbraising. There
is no logical reason why this should be so, it just happens to be true
in Dutch.\footnote{This of course does not exclude the possibility
that there might be
a principled linguistic explanation for this fact.}

\section{Semantic Information}
\label{sem}

The section on semantic information will not be as extensive as the sections
dealing with syntactic and morphological information.
As pointed out above several times, many attributes that are currently 
syntactic in Rosetta might better be considered semantic attributes. We
will repeat these here, for convenience, in section~\ref{sematt}.
Section~\ref{types} describes some properties that a system of sortal
restrictions (types, selection restrictions) must satisfy.
Section~\ref{readingdist} gives some general observations concerning 
reading distinctions.


\subsection{Attributes}
\label{sematt}

We will first list the attributes which have been mentioned in the sections
on syntax already and which can better be considered semantic:

\begin{description}
  \item[Verbs] {\em classes} 
  \item[Nouns/Propernouns] {\em class, deixis, aspect, retro, temporal  }
  \item[Adjectives] {\em class, deixis, aspect, retro, temporal }
  \item[Adverbs] {\em  class, deixis, aspect, retro, temporal}
\end{description}

Other features that one might perhaps want to shift to semantics, though
it is not very clear whether this is possible
are the attributes {\em  req,env} and perhaps {\em  posscomas}.

The attributes {\em  animate} and {\em  human} as used currently in the 
Rosetta syntax for nouns and propernouns are purely syntactic attributes.
The attribute {\bf  human} accounts for the proper distribution of relative
pronouns ({\em aan wie} vs. {\em  waaraan} etc.), and the attribute
animate is used to exclude inanimate indirect objects (though this is not fully
consistent with the test described above for this attribute). 

Of course, also in semantics {\em  human} entities must be distinguished
from {\em  nonhuman} entities, and {\em  animate} entities from 
{\em inanimates},
but these distinction do not necessarily coincide with the syntactic attributes
that (perhaps unfortunately) happen to bear the same name.\footnote{As an 
example, one might like to consider a word such as {\em  directie} as {\em  
human} from a semantic point of view. As far as the distribution of relative
pronouns is concerned, however, this word should count as {\em  nonhuman}
(cf. {\em  *de directie, aan wie...}).} 



\subsection{Selectional restrictions}
\label{types}

We do not have very much to say about how to incorporate information to
account for selectional restrictions, since we just started ourselves
dealing with this phenomenon. The following remarks, however, can be
be made:

\begin{description}
  \item[Ontology] If some (partial) ontology is assumed (e.g.  in the form of
a set of semantic features with some hierarchy defined over it), then
each distinction made in this ontology should have a linguistic motivation,
e.g. examples must be given where this kind of information leads to
disambiguation or a distinction between semantically well-formed and 
semantically ill-formed sentences. It should NOT be just an 
arbitrary ontology, since there are infinitely many ways to classify
entities, and only a classification that has linguistic significance
is relevant.
It is absolutely crucial to look at what argument taking functions require of 
their arguments to be able to establish a set of semantic features.
E.g., if one simply looks at entities that exist and tries to classify
them, then one will not necessarily come to a partitioning in 
{\em collective} and {\em  noncollective} entities. However, if one checks
wat requirements are imposed upon an argument by functions, then one will
find that this distinction is a relevant one. (cf. verbs such as
{\em  splitsen, samenstromen} etc.)
  \item[Testing] Tests must be designed to establish whether a meaning
should be classified under some semantic feature or not. These tests should
be as concrete as possible, e.g. in the form of testing sentences with open
slots where applying simple substitution operations and giving 
grammaticality judgements lead to an answer.
  \item[Metonymy] Cases of productive, cross-linguistic metonymy 
(e.g. a location can be
used for the persons present/working on  that location) should be carefully
distinguished, and for such cases no separate meaning should be included,
but perhaps only, that a certain meaning can be used with a special kind
of metonymy rule. For cases of metonymy that are not cross-linguistic it
would be useful to indicate what type of metonymy has been used in the 
case at hand.
For this purpose a list of productive rules of metonymy
should be gathered from the literature, and restrictions on their use 
established.
\item[Hierarchies] In Rosetta we intend to define a number of semantic
features. Over these semantic features several hierarchies are defined.
Every semantic feature occurs only in one hierarchy. These several
hierarchies make it possible to combine hierarchy with cross-classification:
\item[Cross-Classification] The system of semantic features should allow
for cross-classification. This is absolutely necessary to get a natural
system of semantic features. An examples of cases where cross classification
is necessary is e.g.
{\em human} vs. {\em  natural gender}: there are humans that are
         masculine, and humans that are not masculine; and there are masculines
         that are human, and masculines that are not human.        
In Rosetta we intend to classify meanings of nouns 
with a number of semantic features,
e.g the meaning of {\em  man} will be characterized as $<$human masculine$>$
indicating that this meaning is both {\em  human} and {\em  masculine}.
In general, every meaning of a noun should be characterized by all 
semantic features on a hierarchy that hold of it. If a more specific
feature holds of some meaning, then the less specific semantic features
need not be specified. 

\item[Alternation/negation] Meanings that are argument-taking functions will be
specified for the types of arguments that they require. E.g. the (or at least: 
one) meaning
for the verb {\em to eat} might get a specification of the form 
\mbox{$<$ animate-food$>$}, indicating that the first argument must be 
{\em  animate}, and that the second argument must be {\em  food}. In
experimenting with such descriptions, we found, that it is sometimes
very useful to have the option of alternation, i.e. the possibility to
specify that some argument must either be of one type {\bf or} of some other
type. Also negation of types was found to be useful, e.g. 
the verb {\em  instorten} can mean either `to have a nervous breakdown'
(the argument must be {\em  human}), or simply `collapse' ( the argument
must be {\em concrete} (or perhaps simply an {\em  entity}) but {\bf not}
{\em  human}. 

\end{description}



\subsection{Reading Distinction}
\label{readingdist}

We have little systematic to say about how and when to distinguish
two or more readings. The system to account for argument structure, however,
forces one to certain choices, and can be used as a guide in cases where
argument structure is involved.
In the Rosetta system we adopted the reading distinctions as supplied by the
Van Dale tapes. However, these distinctions turned out to be very unsystematic
in many cases and  the relation with  syntactic properties is almost never
made. For this reason we had to change the Van Dale distinctions in many cases.

Some examples of such cases are:

\begin{description}
  \item[Choice of Auxiliary] There is a connection between the choice
of auxiliary verb and the argument structure of movement verbs such as
{\em  lopen, rijden, zwemmen, schaatsen}, etc. If the verb is conjugated
with {\em  hebben}, then the verb is monadic (cf. {\em  hij heeft gerend,
*Hij heeft naar Amsterdam gerend}). If the verb is conjugated
with {\em  zijn}, then the verb is diadic (cf. {\em  Hij is *(naar Amsterdam) 
gerend}). There are also many other cases where the choice of
auxiliary correlates to other syntactic or semantic properties.
Van Dale does not make such distinctions at all.
  \item[Count/mass] Van Dale does not make a distinction between 
count and mass. This
requires a splitting up of the relevant lexical entries.
  \item[human/nonhuman] Van Dale does not make a distinction between 
human and nonhuman. This
requires a splitting up of the relevant lexical entries.
  \item[Idioms] Van Dale lists a separate meaning for the use of a word in
some idiomatic expression. 
  \item[resultative clauses] Van Dale very often lists a separate meaning for
the use of some verb in combination with a resultative or directional small
clause, even if the use is completely regular and the meaning completely
predictable. (examples: Van Dale N-N 
{\em  eten$^{2}$ III, 0.1}; {\em  persen I, 0.5}). This meaning is listed
under the transitive use of the relevant verb, whereas for Rosetta purposes
there should be no separate meaning at all.
  \item[Participles] When a word occurs only in a participle form, then
the word is listed not separately, but as the form of a non-existing verb
without indicating that this verb does not exist. (example: {\em  beterende},
as used in the expression {\em  aan de beterende hand zijn} is listed under
the intransitive verb {\em  beteren}, 
a verb which cannot be used in any other way.)
  \item[Strange Meanings] Van Dale contains many strange meanings, many
meanings that noone (!!) knows, and the order in which these meanings are 
presented is, to the extent that we can determine this, completely arbitrary. 
\end{description}

\section{Consistency Checks}
\label{constraints}

This appendix contains the consistency checks for the categories
BVERB, BNOUN, BADV and BADJ that currently hold in the Rosetta system.

The constraints are grouped per category. Each constraint is of the following
form:

\begin{description} 
  \item[] ':' ('IMPLIES' $\mid$ 'IFF') '(' $<$boolean expression$>$ ',' $<$boolean 
expression$>$ ')' '"' $<$error message$>$ '"'
\end{description}

The boolean expressions must be PASCAL boolean expressions.
The expressions {\em  IMPLIES} and {\em  IFF} have their normal meaning from 
logic.
If these predicates yield false upon evaluation, then the error
message is written. Constraints that are not completely valid have the
symbol `\$P' before the error message, and they point out {\em  peculiar}
(i.e. unlikely, though possibly correct) attribute fillings.

of these sets begin with the prefix {\em  LSAUXDOM\_}, e.g. the set
{\em  LSAUXDOM\_vp100vps} is the set of verbpatterns compatible with 
{\em  thetavp=vp100}.
