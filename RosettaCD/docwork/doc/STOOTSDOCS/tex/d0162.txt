\section{Introduction}

In this note a number of remaining topics are discussed.
 
\section{Existential and Locational Verbs}

\subsection{zijn,be,ser,estar}

Up to now it has been assumed that all occurrences of the verb {\em zijn}
(English: {\em be }, Spanish: {\em ser, estar}) are introduced
syncategorematically. 

I propose to change this.

I propose to consider these verbs when they indicate a location at a
certain position as basic expressions.

Thus, e.g. {\em is } as in {\em hij is in de tuin }
is a dyadic verb (thetavp=vp120) with as synvps: [synLOCOPENPREPPPROP].

It is in this respect now similar to such expressions as {\em zich bevinden,
gelegen zijn, wonen, woonachtig zijn } etc. We might even consider it
a synonym of {\em zich bevinden}

\subsection{Existential verbs}

It is now important to distinguish sharply between the use of {\em zijn } as
an existential verb (e.g. {\em Er zijn mensen die dit geloven }) and the
locational use of this verb as in {\em De mensen zijn in de tuin }). In 
particular, it should be clear what use is represented in
such sentences as {\em Er is iemand in de tuin }. My suggestion is, that in
such a sentence there is no ambiguity: If a locative adverbial is present,
then we are talking about the use of {\em zijn } as a locative verb.
Existential verbs cannot be combined with locational adverbials. The 
rules that introduces locative adverbials should be made sensitive to this.

A weaker version would allow locational adverbs, so that in certain cases
an ambiguity will arise. 
\subsubsection{Dutch}


Verb = {\em zijn}. {\em Er} is obligatory. The argument must be indefinite
It looks as if the NP must be modified. Ex. {\em Er zijn mensen die dit niet
geloven }. (cf. {\em ?Er zijn mensen}, only correct in the meaning {\em there
are people over there}, i.e. with an ER$_{loc})$. 

\subsubsection{English}
 The verb is {\em be}.  {\em There} is obligatory.
The argument must be indefinite. Ex. {\em There are people who do not believe
this }.


\subsubsection{ Spanish}
The relevant verb is {\em Hay}. The argument must be indefinite, unless it is
one of the the clitics {\em los,las,lo,la }, cf. {\em no los hay}.
There is a translation problem here.  An
abstract pronominal subject is inserted, cf.  D/E {\em er/there } 
Ex. {\em hay gente que no lo creen}


\subsection{Locatives}

The proposal up to now faced the following two problems:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item It is impossible to derive {\em Hij is in Rome} and {\em Hij bevindt
zich in Rome} isomorphically, hence they cannot be translated into each other 
   \item The choice between {\em  zitten, liggen, staan, etc.} that (I assume)
must be made by means of a type system must be made in the syntax, in contrast
to other cases of typing (in the IL, or in transfer).
\end{enumerate}

The new proposal solves both problems.

\subsubsection{Dutch} 

Example verbs:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] {\em zijn}, {\em liggen}, {\em staan}, {\em zitten}, {\em hangen}
\end{enumerate}

Example Sentences:
\begin{enumerate}
   \item[] De man {\em is} in de tuin
   \item[] Er {\em is} een man in de tuin
   \item[] het boek {\em ligt} in de kast
   \item[] Er {\em ligt} een boek in de kast
   \item[] De vogel {\em zit} op de tafel
   \item[] Er {\em zit} een vogel op de tafel
   \item[] Het schilderij {\em hangt} aan de muur
   \item[] Er {\em hangt} een schilderij aan de muur
\end{enumerate}


The verbs {\em zitten, liggen, staan, hangen } are ambiguous. In the meanings
{\em  to be in a sitting/lying/standing/hanging position } it has
thetavp=vp100, and synvps=[]. Locative phrases can be present as adverbials.
The verbs can be passivized, cf. {\em Er werd door de mensen
gezeten/ gestaan/ gelegen} etc. The verbs require {\em  animate} subjects.
(Perhaps even a subclass of animates). Their equivalents in English are
{\em to sit, stand, lie, hang }.

In a second meaning ({\em being located in some position }) they are
dyadic: thetavp = vp120, and have synvps=[LOCOPENPREPPPROP]. Passivization is
not possible (cf. e.g. {\em *Er werd door de handdoek in de tuin gelegen}).
In this meaning
they are synonymous to {\em zijn}. The choice between {\em zijn, zitten, 
liggen, staan, hangen } must be made on the basis of a (sorted) type system.
The choice depends on the sort of the subject (cf. e.g. {\em 
Er ligt/*zit/*staat een handdoek in de kast}) and on the nature of the locative
phrase (cf. e.g. {\em *Er ligt een handdoek aan de kapstok }) 

Their equivalent in English is the verb {\em to be }.

The type system required to make the appropriate choice must be quite 
sophisticated, and all sorts of very subtle meaning differences exist here.
The distinctions to be made  here will also be necessary to disambiguate
English {\em put} into resp. {\em zetten, leggen, stoppen, doen } (The 
`causative' correlates of {\em zitten, liggen, staan }).

Some considerations:
\begin{description}
  \item[zijn] no type restrictions
  \item[liggen] subject must be positionable horizontally, (perhaps: and this
must be the normal positioning of this object), cf. {\em  ok: boek, paal,
*lucht, *gas}. All(?) animates are horizontally positionable \\
   OR:\\
subject is a fluid: {\em  er ligt koffie/benzine op de grond}\\
but: {\em *Hij legde de koffie/benzine op de grond}
 
   \item[staan] subject must be positionable vertically: {\em 
ok: boek, *handdoek, *broek}.  Certain animates are vertically positionable.
(cf. {\em but what about ?vis} and  cf. {\em er staat een vogel in de tuin vs.
Er zit een vogel in de tuin } and {\em ?Er staat een vogel in de boom  vs. Er
zit een vogel in de boom} 

   \item[zitten] subject must be able to sit (=animate?, but {\em  vis})
or it cannot be positioned horizontally or vertically, either inherently
(cf. e.g. {\em Er zit lucht in de tank }(?), but cf. {\em *op de tank })
or because the location does not allow for such a positioning, e.g.
{\em Er zit een boek in de zak }.
Other relevant examples: {\em  er zit een man in de tuin; er zit een vogel  op
het dak} Also it is used to indicate that something is sticking to something
else (e.g. by glue or by magnetic force), cf. {\em er zit een sticker op/aan de
deur}. Notice the subtle difference in meaning between 
{\em  er zit een vlieg op het raam}  and {\em Er zit een vlieg aan het raam }
The second must mean that the fly is sticking to the glass, implying e.g. that
it has been beaten to death.
 
Cf. also {\em  Hij heeft een button op/aan zijn vest}

Notice furthermore, with pictures {\em  Er staat een boom op het bord,
Er staat een paard op het bord, ?Er ligt een paard op de tekening vs.
Er staat een paard op de tekening }
  \item[hangen] Not fully clear to me
\end{description}

A special investigation might be appropriate. (Perhaps by Natalia, or some
other stageair)

\subsubsection{ English}

The verb is {\em to be}, perhaps also {\em to hang }:

Example sentences:
\begin{enumerate}
   \item[] The man {\em is} in the garden
   \item[] There {\em is} a man in the garden
   \item[] The book {\em is} in the cupboard
   \item[] There {\em is} a book in the cupboard
   \item[] The bird {\em is} on the table
   \item[] There {\em is} a bird on the table
   \item[] The painting {\em  is} {\em ?hanging} at the wall
   \item[] There {\em is} a painting hanging at the wall
\end{enumerate}


Problem: Suppose the Dutch sentence {\em er hangt een boek in de kast}
(which describes a strange situation, but it is a perfect sentence) must be
translated into English. The most appropriate translation is not
{\em  There is a book in the cupboard}, but {\em There is a book hanging in the
cupboard}. How can we achieve this effect? It seems to imply that
the type restrictions of
the verbs {\em liggen, zitten, staan, hangen }  cannot, or less easily , be
violated in the second meaning, but they can, at least more easily,
in the first meaning. 
 
\subsubsection{Spanish}

The verb is {\em estar} if the subject is definite, {\em hay} otherwise
(Can locational {\em estar } never occur with indefinite subjects?).
\begin{enumerate}
   \item[] El hombre {\em est\'{a}} en el jard\'{i}n
   \item[] {\em Hay} un hombre en el  jard\'{i}n
   \item[] El libro {\em est\'{a}} en la caja
   \item[] {\em Hay} un libro en la caja
\end{enumerate}

{\em  hangen} must be translated as  {\em colgar}


\section{Some remarks on the structure of Spanish sentences}

 I wil assume that the basic sentential structure of Spanish can be
represented in the following way.

 At the deepest level, Spanish (as any decent language) has the following
structure: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item [] SENTENCE [ subj/ NP, pred/VP ]
\end{enumerate}

 Adverbs modifying the verb are left-most in VP or right before VP. (i.e.,
outside the Rosetta-framework, Chomsky-adjoined to VP on the left).

 Auxiliary verbs (e.g. {\em haber} for perfect tenses) precede the VP (and
the adverbs mentioned).

 In all cases the main verb in VP must move to an aux-position: Either
simply becoming aux (as in (1)), or adjoining to an auxiliary verb forming
one new complex verb (as in (2)): 
\begin{enumerate}
   \item subj/Juan aux/compra pred/VP [ obj/ un libro ]
   \item subj/Juan aux/VERB [ aux/ha adjoin/comprado ] pred/VP [ obj/ un libro ]
\end{enumerate}

 The result is that the typical surface word order is

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] subj/ .. auxiliary/.. headverb/.. adverb/.. objectsofverb/..
\end{enumerate}


(cf. e.g. {\em  Podremos tomar bien el sol} from Bienvenido pp 121:16)

 The subject can be subject to the rule of free inversion. It achieves a
similar effect as the postsubjformation rule in Dutch, but there is no
restriction on the definiteness of the subject, and the subject ends up on the
righthand side of the VP:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] ha comprado el libro {\em el hombre de Espa\~{n}a que he visto ayer}
\end{enumerate}

 (Maybe there restrictions of another kind, e.g. w.r.t relative heaviness, or
ability to function as focus) 

If the verb is ergative, its argument starts out as an object, and can, but 
need not, remain there, getting nominative case:

\begin{enumerate}
   \item[] Ha llegado {\em Juan} en Espa\~{n}a
   \item[]{\em Juan} ha llegado en Espa\~{n}a
\end{enumerate}

In yes-no-questions the highest node functioning as aux moves to the left and
gets the relation inversionrel/.. (not {\em conjrel} as in Dutch,English): 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] inversionrel/compra subj/Juan pred/VP [ obj/un libro ]?
\end{enumerate}

 or
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] inversionrel/ VERB [ aux/ha adjoin/comprado] subj/Juan pred/VP
[obj/un libro ]? 
\end{enumerate}

 Similarly, in wh-questions the aux-node starts to function as 
inversionrel/..,
and moves to the left:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] shift/Para qui\'{e}n conj/ha comprado subj/Juan obj/un libro?
\end{enumerate}

 When certain (all(?), what about relativization) other elements are shifted
(topicalization) aux also becomes inversionrel/... .
See Torrego LI.

 This rule aux/.. $\rightarrow$ inversionrel/.. accounts for all cases of
subject-verb inversion Of course, aux$\rightarrow$inversionrel and free
inversion do not exclude each other, so we can also derive: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] shift/Para qui\'{e}n inversionrel/ha comprado un libro postsubj/este hombre de  
Espa\~{n}a? 
\end{enumerate}

 A certain class of verbs that takes infinitives as complements (e.g.
{\em ir a}, {\em salir de}, {\em deber}, {\em poder}, etc.) causes (optionally)
a reanalysis of the structure: When the infinitive has been inserted, the
infinitive is adjoined to the governing verb from the higher clause and it
starts to form a unit with it: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] [ subj/ \'{e}l,  aux/va 
  \item[] \ \ pred/VP [ compl/ [SENT subj/PRO aux/a comprar pred/VP[obj/eso] ] ]$\rightarrow$
  \item[] [ subj/ \'{e}l,  aux/ VERB [ aux/va adjoin/ a comprar ] 
  \item[] \ \ pred/VP [ compl/ [SENT subj/PRO pred/VP[obj/eso] ] ]
\end{enumerate}

 After this, the deeper sentence-node is pruned, and the whole starts to act as
one clause: clitics move to the verb-complex, instead of to their governing
infinitive etc.
The question what must happpen in such cases to such preposition-like material
as {\em  a} (cf. {\em va {\bf a} comprar}) is still open.
 In questions the whole complex verbs goes to inversionrel/..:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] {\em Van a ir} los empleados de huelga?
\end{enumerate}

It seems, however, that at least, {\em usted} can get in between, even if
clitic climbing has taken place, cf. (taken from Ramsey, p 98): 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] Qu\'{e} {\em me va} V. {\em a dar}?
\end{enumerate}

This seems to be quite archaic, however, and we can ignore it.
 
 The negative particle {\em no} is part of the verb or verbcomplex functioning
as aux, and so are the clitic pronouns, when they precede. When they follow
they are part of the nonfinite form preceding them. 

 Nonfinite forms usually do not allow overt subjects. An exception to this is
that overt nominative subjects can appear when the verbal form bears the
relation inversionrel/.. , and has moved to the left:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] La encontr\'{e} {\bf volviendo} {\em yo} de la caza
   \item[] Tem\'{i} que mi hermano, no {\bf estando} {\em yo} presente, cometiera
alg\'{u}n disparate 
\end{enumerate}

(Ramsey:p. 361).

 Apparently this is true for infinitives as well, if at least we consider a
sentence such as:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] As\'{i} aprovecho para {\bf comprar} {\em yo} tambien algunas cosas
\end{enumerate}
(taken from  Bienvenido p. 128:3-4), 
where either {\em aprovecho para comprar} forms a complex verb (improbable,
given the semantics of this verb, and given the fact that clitic climbing seems
to be impossible), or the infinitive bears the relation inversionrel/.. and can,
preceding the subj/.. assign nominative case to this subject. If this is the
correct analysis, then it will be possible only after a restricted class of
verbs. This phenomenon occurs in Italian as well (cf. Rizzi). 

Ivonne Bordelois(1986: Formal Parameters, p. 2-3) gives adverbial infinitival
examples: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] Admiramos el poema al {\bf leerlo} {\em el autor}
  \item[] We admire the poem while the author (was) reading it
  \item[] me fui despu\'{e}s de {\bf haber firmado} {\em Juan} el art\'{i}culo
  \item[] I left after John having signed the article
\end{enumerate}

\section{Some remarks on modals in Rosetta3}

I propose to treat modals in Rosetta3 as much as possible in the same way 
as in Rosetta2 (for pragmatic, not principled reasons). The following proposal
is such a proposal. 

I assume the facts as they have been assumed in R2, see appendix.
If these facts are not fully correct, then appropriate adaptations must be made.

One of the basic problems concerning modals in Rosetta is that the choice
which verb actually must appear cannot be made in the startrules, but only
after the assignment of time.

I assume the following:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item There are 6 abstract BVERBs:
        \begin{description}
          \item[permission] thetavp=vp120 
          \item[volition] thetavp=vp120 
          \item[obligation] thetavp=vp120 
          \item[ability] thetavp=vp120 
          \item[possibility] thetavp=vp010
          \item[necessity] thetavp=vp010
        \end{description}
       and there are five relevant BADJs (in English):
	 \begin{description}
          \item[allowed] thetavp=vp120
          \item[obliged] thetavp=vp120 
          \item[able] thetavp=vp120 
          \item[possible] thetavp=vp010
          \item[necessary] thetavp=vp010
	 \end{description}
     
In R2 (to the extent that I can recover it) there was one abstract verb 
{\em abstract}. The distinction made above was made by an external parameter
in the vpmodal rules. This option is not available in R3, since vp-rules are
transformations in R3. The adjectives {\em allowed, obliged, able } were 
treated as particles of the verbs {\em  be}.

The approach adopted here creates an ambiguity in certain cases, in English.
The ambiguity might be avoided partially by retaining information on
time attributes up to the start rules, and parametrizing these.

  \item These abstract verbs run through the VERBPPROP subgrammar. The BADJs
through the ADJPPPROP subgrammar.
  \item Directly after the Time rules the abstract verbs, now marked for tense,
are spelled out, in the following way:
\begin{tabbing}
aaaaaaaaaaaaaaa \= aaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaa \= aaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaa \= \kill
Input      \> Dutch                        \> English\\
permission \> mogen                        \> can, be allowed to\\
volition   \> willen                       \> want to, would like to\\
obligation \> moeten                  \> must, to have to, be obliged to(?)\\
           \> hoeven                       \> need (to)\\
ability    \> kunnen                       \> can, be able to\\
possibility\> kunnen, misschien?           \> may, might, possible to\\
necessity  \> moeten                       \> must, necessary to\\
\end{tabbing}

The spelling out of {\em hoeven} as obligation is perhaps not necessary.
We can translate it locally (as was done in R2). The polarity requirements will
guarantee that it occurs in the correct environment. 

The synvps of the abstract verbs equal the unions of the synvps of all verbs
they are spelled out by.

\item After the spelling rules the PROPOK rules follow. Sentences
containing syntactic modals in English are subject to the following changes:
(A somewhat simplified representation of the facts is given)

A structure of the form

   \begin{verbatim}
     CLAUSE{CLAUSErec1}
             [ subjrel/T1, mu1, auxrel/(*do*), mu2,
                              pred/VERBP{VERBPrec1}
                                  [ head/VERB{VERBrec1},
                                    compl/SENTENCE{SENTENCErec1}
                                         [ subjrel/T2,
                                           pred/VERBP{VERBPrec2}
                                         ]
                                  ]
             ] 
   \end{verbatim}

where
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] VERBrec1.key in SYNTMODALS
  \item[] VERBrec1.thetavp = vp120
  \item[] SENTENCErec1.infsort= inf
  \item[] index of T1 = index of T2, and T1 and T2 are fully identical
\end{enumerate}

is turned into
 
\begin{verbatim}
CLAUSE{CLAUSErec1}[ subjrel/T1,
                    mu1,
                    auxrel/VERB{VERBrec1},
                    mu2,         
                    predrel/VERBP{VERBrec2}
                  ]
\end{verbatim}

A similar change must be applied when the theatvp = vp010: in that case
the subject of the infinitive sentence becomes the subject of the 
higher clause.

In the embedded sentence adverbs( and NEG!) and some further auxiliaries 
(have, be) can be present. In a definitive formulation of the rule
these should be taken into account.

In this transformation the SENTENCErec1 disappears. 
Of course, in analysis, this record must be created again, so that it must
be quite clear how this should be done. Furthermore, part of the information 
in this record is still necessary later on in the derivation(now
speaking generatively again), so this information should be copied to other
records here somehow. Jan Odijk is working on a specific proposal concerning
these matters, combining them with similar operations that are necessary in 
Dutch.

  \item We can adopt the same method as in R2 to get {\em  we can do it}
as a translation of {\em  wij kunnen het}: expand {\em  het} to {\em  het doen}
already in Dutch (speaking analytically).

  \item The case {\em we hadden het kunnen doen } is translated into
{\em  we could have done it} in R2.  Assuming that this is correct, we may
adopt the same method as in R2( with a special parameter(??), or we can see the
Dutch sentence as a transform of {\em  we zouden het gedaan kunnen hebben},
where {\em  zouden kunnen} is translated 'locally' into {\em could}, and {\em  gedaan
hebben } is as well (or are these two approaches the same?). 

  \item If we guarantee a blocking of E. {\em * it can, it must}, which
can be achieved relatively simply, since we must also block {\em  *I wonder it}
, and if we allow for a translation into the adjectives {\em  possible, 
necessary } etc., then we may get the translation {\em  it is possible} for
D. {\em het kan } etc. (Not dealt with, but noticed  in R2)

   \item Not accounted for in R2 are cases where modals are embedded under
modals, e.g. {\em Dit moet kunnen} or {\em Dit kan moeten }. If {\em  kunnen}
{\em moeten} can be translated into {\em possible, necessary}, then the 
result will be {\em This must be possible, This can be necessary }. Cases such 
as {\em Dit moet vertaald kunnen worden, Dit kan vertaald moeten worden }
(to be translated into {\em It must be possible to translate this, It can
be necessary to translate this }) are possible only  if we allow that active
voice is translated into passive voice, at least in certain cases. 
A simpler approach would assume that the relevant translations are
{\em It is necessary that that this can be translated, It is possible that
this must be translated}. The following will be yielded then as well:
{\em It is necessary that it is possible that this is translated,
It must be possible that this is translated; It is possible that it is 
necessary that this is translated, It can be necessary that this is translated}
(Or are subjunctive required here?)
\end{enumerate}
