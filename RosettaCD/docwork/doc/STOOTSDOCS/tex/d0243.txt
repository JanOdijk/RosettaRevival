\section{Introduction}

This document is the documentation of the Dutch surface rules for sentences,
i.e. for the surface rules {\em  Vcomplex, VP } and {\em  SENTENCE}. 
These rules can be found in the file {\em dutch:surfrules1.sur}.
This document
presupposes that it is known how the surface rules operate, and what their
notation is. 


\section{General remarks}

In many cases it was necessary to send values of attributes upward via a record
that did not have an attribute suitable  for this task. In these cases often
new attributes have been added. The addition of attributes is no problem in 
itself, but their presence does complicate M-grammar. It would be desirable if
it were possible to define attributes in a record that are only visible in the
surface Rules and can be used only there. Appendix~\ref{surfatt} contains a
list of attributes that have been introduced solely for the purposes of the
surface parser (for the records of VERB, VERBP and SENTENCE).

A general observation is that often very complex boolean expressions must be
used. The readability of boolean expressions decreases very fast, especially
in so primitive a language as PASCAL. Often
auxiliary boolean functions have been introduced to be able to break down a 
complex boolean expression into several parts, and some general boolean
functions that simulate the behavior of boolean operators have been defined.
(in particular: the function IMPLIES).

It is desirable to take special measures for such phenomena in a more
sophisticated surface rule notation than the one used now.  

Originally it was planned to have VP's with and without head-VERBS. However,
it turned out to be very difficult to make VP's without a head-VERB present,
basically because the only way to reduce the number of clearly incorrect VP's
later on would require sending up extremely much information via the record
VERBP upward (almost all boolean parameters would have to be sent upward). 
For this
reason we decided, already in a rather early stage, to require that a VERBP 
always contains a head-VERB. The consequence of this decision, however, was
that a major part of the rule for VERBP had to be copied to the SENTENCE rule
as well. This is clearly an undesirable situation. One way of solving this
problem is allowing for many attributes that are visible only in the surface  
parser and can be used only there.

A further general observation is that after having written a first version
of the surface parser, extremely much wrong structures are yielded. In general
most of these can be eliminated very simply by adding further restrictions,
but these restrictions usually are no more than   arbitrary true statements
about surface structures that have no principled status whatsoever (in most
cases they do not coincide with a rule of the grammar as one would like to state
it and does so in the M-rules), though they play a very important function
in reducing the number of clearly incorrect structures, and hence in 
avoiding unnecessary ambiguities. This implies that their relevance  cannot be
foreseen by writers of (complex) surface rules in any way, and it also implies
that their presence is a (partially arbitrary) function of the sentences tested 
by the author of the rules. There is no guarantee, that all clearly incorrect
structures that can be eliminated easily are eliminated indeed. Furthermore,
it makes the prospects of generating an efficient surface parser 
automatically (given an M-grammar) even more dim than they are already, since
for such a function one would like to have a theory on the issue of which true
statements about surface structures are relevant  for reducing unnecessary
ambiguities. 

Errors made very often were putting global conditions under 
LOCAL CONDITION, and mistyping the category name after \$ (e.g. \$RADV instead
of \$RADVP). In these cases no errors were signalled due to the primitivities
of the (compiler for the) programming language used, but the behavior of
the parser was completely unpredictable. Such errors are very difficult to
discern (though solving them is very easy), and {\em it can still not be 
guaranteed that errors of this kind do not occur!}. It is very important to
build in checks on this kind of error in a more sophisticated surface rule
compiler. 

Another error that is very difficult to find is forgetting the specification
{\em VAR} with certain parameters of auxiliary procedures. The PASCAL
compiler does not give any warning when an assignation is made to a non-VAR
parameter.
Therefore it would be useful if a more sophisticated surface rule syntax
could prevent such errors, or warn for them.

\section{The surface syntax of verb clusters}

\subsection{Verb clusters}

In this section a short, informal description will be given on how to 
recognize verb clusters (for human beings), given a string.\footnote{
See also ANS, 8.6.1, 8.6.3 for a discussion of the notion {\em  groepsvormend}.
The notion {\em  verplicht/optioneel groepsvormend werkwoord} corresponds
partially to the notion of BVERB with the attribute {\att Verbraiser} set to
{\val obligatoryVR}/{\val\ optionalVR} ({\em  verbraisers}). The notion 
{\em groepsvormend werkwoord} in the
ANS is somewhat wider, since our notion {\em  verbraiser} is applicable only
to verbs that allow the order
{\em verb - infinitive}. 
If only the order {\em  infinitive - verb} is allowed, then {\em  verb} is not
a {\em  verbraiser}, though it can be a {\em  groepsvormend werkwoord}.}
  

I will call a group of verbs (possibly consisting of 1 verb) in a subordinate
clause, and a group of verbs that is clearly not in `second position' in
main clauses, a {\em  sentence final verb group}\footnote{This corresponds
to the notion {\em werkwoordelijke eindgroep } in the ANS (8.6.1), with the 
proviso's made in the preceding footnote }. Apart from verbs it can
contain the element {\em  te}, particles, certain postpositions 
 and the words {\em  aan} and {\em  het}
in this order. Examples:
\begin{enumerate}

   \item ...dat ik de boeken 
   \begin{enumerate}
     \item {\em kocht}
     \item {\em heb gekocht}
     \item {\em wou kopen}
     \item {\em heb kunnen kopen}       
     \item {\em probeerde te kopen}       
     \item {\em zou kunnen proberen te kopen}       
     \item {\em aan het kopen ben}
   \end{enumerate}
   \item ... dat ik de man
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item {\em opbelde}
      \item {\em heb opgebeld}
      \item {\em wou opbellen}
      \item {\em heb kunnen opbellen}       
      \item {\em probeerde op te bellen}       
      \item {\em zou kunnen proberen op te bellen}       
      \item {\em aan het opbellen ben}
    \end{enumerate}
   \item ... dat ik het bos
      \begin{enumerate} 
       \item  {\em  inreed}
       \item  {\em  wilde inrijden}
       \item  {\em  probeerde in te rijden}
       \item  {\em  ben ingereden}
      \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}

Certain sequences of verbs must be excluded from this definition.

Consider e.g. 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item ...dat ik me ervoor {\em schaamde} {\bf te moeten vertrekken}
  \item ...dat wij {\em afgesproken  hebben} {\bf te komen}
\end{enumerate}

In these examples the italic parts are sentence final verb groups of the finite
subordinate clause, and  
the boldface parts are sentence final verb groups of an embedded infinitival
sentence(a {\em  beknopte bijzin}, in traditional terminology, cf. also ANS
8.6.1, 20.3). 
The italic and boldface parts taken together, however, do not form
a sentence final verb group.

A test to check what elements belong to the same sentence final verb group is
the following. Replace the last verb in a candidate group by a transitive verb.
Put the direct object belonging to this verb before all preceding adjacent
verbs (particles, {\em te }). If this yields a well-formed result, then
in one analysis, the verb belongs to the same sentence final verb group that all
the preceding verbs belong to. If it does not yield a well-formed sentence, 
then the last verb and the first verb do not belong to the same sentence final
verb group. The maximal sentence final verb group can be found by shifting the
object one verb to the right, and applying the test again, etc.


In the examples mentioned this test yield ungrammatical results:

\begin{examples}
\example ...dat ik me (ervoor) schaamde deze boeken te moeten kopen
\example \star ...dat  ik me deze boeken (ervoor) schaamde te moeten kopen
\example ...dat wij afgesproken hebben deze boeken te kopen
\example \star ...dat wij deze boeken afgesproken hebben te kopen
\end{examples}

Other examples show more possibilities:

\begin{examples}
\example ...dat hij wou proberen te {\em zwemmen}
\example ...dat hij {\em  de boeken} wou proberen te {\em kopen}
\example \star ...dat hij wou {\em  de boeken} proberen te kopen
\example ...dat hij wou proberen {\em de boeken} te kopen
\end{examples}

A sentence final verb group consists maximally of three {\em  verb clusters}.
A verb cluster can consist of one verb, or of more verbs.
In the surface rules each of these three verb clusters form a constituent.
They bear the relations {\rel  head},  {\rel  progauxrel} and {\rel  auxrel}.
They occur always in the order given. (See the rule for VERBP, numbers
18, 19, 29, 20 and 30).

We will give some clues to find the partitioning of sentence final verb groups 
into verb clusters, followed by some examples:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A participle is always the last element of a verb cluster
  \item The combination {\em  aan het V} is a verb cluster
  \item All noninitial verbal elements in a verb cluster must be infinitives
         or participles, except in the case described immediately below
  \item The combination {\em  infinitive} + {\em  finite modal verb} is a verb 
cluster
  \item A particle can either precede all verb clusters of a sentence final 
verb group, or precede {\em  te + VERB} (where VERB is the verb that requires 
the particle) directly , or be written as one word in combination with a verb.
\end{itemize}


Some examples of the partitioning into verb clusters:

\begin{tabular}[t]{|c|c|c|c|}
\hline
other & head               & progauxrel & auxrel\\
\hline
      & gekocht               &            & heeft\\
      & aan het kopen         & is         &\\
      & wilde opbellen        &            &\\
      & opgebeld              &            & kan hebben\\
      & aan het opbellen      & kan zijn   &\\
      & zat te werken         &            &\\
      & aan het kopen         & geweest    & is\\
      & aan het kopen         & geweest    & kan zijn\\
      & heeft gekocht         &            &\\
  op  & wilde bellen          &            &\\
      & wou proberen te kopen &            &\\
      & moet kopen            &            &\\
      & kopen moet            &            &\\
      & gedood                &            & zou kunnen worden\\
      & ingewandeld           &            & kon zijn\\
      & aan het wandelen      & is geweest &\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

The surface rule {\em  Vcomplex} describes the form each of these verb clusters
can take.

\subsection{The rule Vcomplex}

\subsubsection{The Regular Expression}

The regular expression gives 11 alternative realizations of VERB. Some typical
structures they are supposed to account for are listed below. 
The numbers 
between brackets refer to the numbers after the slashes in the regular 
expressions. Each alternative consists of two elements (except the 
antepenultimate one and the 
penultimate one, which consist of three elements). In the examples a string
corresponding to this second element is printed in boldface: 

\begin{description}
  \item[1] (1,2) {\em  te} + VERB: {\em  te} {\bf lopen}, {\em te} {\bf kopen},
             {\em te}  {\bf werken } 
  \item[2] (3,4) PART + VERB consisting of {\em te } + VERB: {\em  op}
{\bf  te bellen} , {\em aan } {\bf te lopen} 
  \item[3] (5,6) VERB + VERB. The first VERB must be a {\em  bareV, teV}. The
second VERB can be any infinitival VERB, also a verb cluster:
\begin{itemize}
  \item {\em  wilde} {\bf lopen} , {\em wilde } {\bf opbellen} , {\em willen } 
{\bf opbellen}
  \item {\em probeerde} {\bf te lopen} , {\em probeerde } {\bf op te bellen} ,
 {\em proberen } {\bf op te bellen} 
  \item {\em  wilde} {\bf proberen te lopen} , {\em wilde } {\bf proberen op te 
bellen} , {\em willen } {\bf proberen op te bellen } 
  \item {\em te willen } {\bf lopen} , {\em te kunnen } {\bf lopen} 
  \item etc.
\end{itemize}
  \item[4] (7,8) auxiliary verb plus nonauxiliary verb: 
        {\em  heeft} {\bf gelopen} , {\em is } {\bf gelopen} , {\em werd } 
{\bf gedood} , {\em heeft } {\bf opgebeld} , {\em werd} {\bf opgebeld}, 
{\em te hebben } {\bf opgebeld} , {\em te zijn } {\bf gedood} 
   \item[5] (9,10) bare infinitive (or PART + infinitive) followed by a 
finite modal verb. A {\em  modal} verb is a verb with the attribute {\em  subc}
set to the value {\em  modalverb}. It consists of the following verbs: {\em 
kunnen, mogen, moeten, willen, zullen} (exhaustive list). Some examples:
{\em kopen} {\bf  wil}; {\em  opbellen} {\bf moet}.

   \item[6] (11,12). A verb with subc = progaux followed by (PART +) {\em  te}
+ infinitive: {\em  zat} {\bf te werken}, {\em  stond} {\bf te treuzelen}, {\em
 zitten} {\bf te werken  }, {\em  te zitten} {\bf te werken}, {\em  stond}
{\bf op te bellen}.

   \item[7] (13,14) An auxiliary verb followed by an infinitival verb cluster
(for cases of Infinitivum Pro Participio): {\em  heeft} {\bf zitten te werken},
{\em  is} {\bf komen zwemmen},  {\em  heeft} {\bf kunnen lezen}.

  \item[8] (15,16) The auxiliary verb {\em  zijn} followed by a participle of 
the auxiliary verb {\em  zijn}. This case could have been collapsed with case
4, but for historical reasons this has not been done. Example: {\em  is} 
{\bf geweest}, {\em  te zijn} {\bf geweest}.

  \item[9] (17,18,19) For the combination of {\em  aan}, {\em  het} and an 
infinitive: {\em  aan het kopen}, {\em  aan het opbellen}.

  \item[10](20,21,22) For the combination of certain directional postpositions
and verbs, which are written as one word (hence there is a GLUE): 
{\em  in}{\bf gewandeld}, {\em  uit}{\bf gewandeld}

  \item[11] (20, 23) For the combination of certain directional postpositions
and VERBs consisting of {\em  te} + infinitive: {\em  in} {\bf te wandelen},
{\em  uit} {\bf te wandelen}
\end{description}


\subsubsection{Parameters}

There is a parameter for each attribute of the record of the new topnode to be
created.

Furthermore there is a  parameter to record the key of the particle found.
There is an extra parameter to keep track of the polarity properties of 
other verbs than the head. (This has no principled status yet).

\subsubsection{Auxiliary functions and procedures}

There is one auxiliary function, which checks whether the combination of a verb
and an auxiliary is a correct combination.  

The check works as follows: If the verb does not allow passive, then the 
auxiliary present must be (one of ) the auxiliary(-ies) required by this verb.
If the verb does allow passive, then the auxiliary present must be 
(one of ) the  auxiliary(-ies) required by the verb, or one of the 
passive auxiliaries ({\em  zijn, worden}).

The function is called in 8.

\subsubsection{The projection of values of attributes}

We will call the process of sending values of attributes upward the 
{\em projection} of values of attributes.

The attributes of the category VERB can be divided into five groups, depending
on their behavior w.r.t. projection.

\begin{description}
  \item[I] `argument structure attributes' 
  \item[II] `morphological form attributes'
  \item[III] `the attribute {\att  status}'
  \item[IV] `deep attributes'
  \item[V] `surface attributes'
\end{description}

These groups can be characterized (informally) in the following way:

\begin{description} 
  \item[argument structure attributes] attributes that give information on the 
argument structure that can have a significant value in the case of VerbRaisers
  \item[morphological form attributes] attributes that supply information on 
the morphological form of the verb
  \item[the attribute {\att  status}] A special attribute to keep track of what
is below the current verb
  \item[deep attributes] attributes that give information on the 
argument structure that can {\em  not} have a significant value in the case of
VerbRaisers 
  \item[surface attributes] attributes assumed only for the purposes of the 
surface parser.
\end{description}


For more information on the difference between 
'deep attributes` and 'argument structure attributes', see below.

These groups contain the following members\footnote{The behavior of the 
attributes {\att subc, IPP}, and {\att  perfauxs} w.r.t. projection is not fully 
consistent yet. The following holds:
\begin{description}
  \item[subc] behaves like class I attributes in 8; like class II attributes in 
11. 
  \item[IPP] behaves like class I attributes in 7,8; like class II attributes 
in 13,14.
  \item[perfauxs] behaves like class I attributes in 7,8; like class II 
attributes in 13,14. 
\end{description}
This has not been made uniform yet, because it must be investigated whether 
this will have consequences. 
}:

\begin{description}
  \item[I argument structure attributes] possvoices, synvps, thetavp, 
         Caseassigner, controller, verbraiser, class, IPP, subc  
  \item[II morphological form attributes] conjclasses, number, persons, eORenForm,
         modus, tense, perfauxs 

  \item[III the attribute {\att  status}]
  \item[IV deep attributes] prepkey1, prepkey2, particle, reflexivity
  \item[V surface rule attributes] foundauxes, synvpefs2
\end{description}


The behavior of these groups w.r.t. projection differs only if two (or more)
verbs get dominated by one new verb. The behavior is dependent on the
relations of the two verbs combined. There are two relevant subcases:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item There is a relation {\rel head}, and a relation {\rel adjoinrel}
  \item There is a relation {\rel head}, and a relation {\rel auxrel}
\end{enumerate}

The attribute {\att status} never gets a value by projection. Its value is set
directly in the rule

The behavior w.r.t. projection can be described as follows:

\hline

                  &  II             & from {\rel head}\\

                  &  V              & from {\rel adjoinrel}\\
\hline
auxrel-head       &  I              & from {\rel head}\\
                  &  II             & from {\rel auxrel}\\

\hline
\end{tabular}

\paragraph{Deep Attributes}

We will first describe the structure of verb clusters, and propose some 
definitions, and then describe how `deep attributes' are used

A verb cluster has the following form (ignoring {\em te }, particles, and some
other elements that are irrelevant here, and the simple structure 
\mbox{V$_{0}$[adjoinrel/V$_{2}$, head/V$_{1}$]} where V$_{1}$ is a finite modal
verb:

\begin{tabular}[t]{cccccccccccc}
       &V$_{0}$\\
V$_{1_{1}}$&       &            &V$_{1_{2}}$\\
\\
\\
\\
        &       &            &       &   V${n_{1}}$  & V${n_{2}}$\\
\end{tabular} 

In such a structure we define:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The highest V$_{i_{1}}$ bearing the relation {\rel head} is called the
{\em highest} verb 
  \item V$_{n_{2}}$ bearing the relation {\rel adjoinrel} (if also a 
{\rel head} is present) or {\rel head} (if also an {\rel auxrel} is present)
is called the {\rel deepest} verb
  \item For all i : The verb V$_{i_{j}}$ that bears the relation {\rel head}
if also the relation {\rel adjoinrel} is present, is called a {\em VRinducer}.
\end{itemize}

In a verb cluster a VRinducer must be a {\att verb raiser}, i.e. a verb with 
the attribute {\att verbraiser} set to {\val optionalVR} or {\val obligatoryVR}.

Use is made of the following generalizations:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A verbraiser has no particle  (Evers 1975)
  \item A verbraiser is not reflexive (Evers 1975)
  \item A verbraiser, when it is a VRinducer, takes maximally one NP argument,
and an infinitival complement
\end{enumerate}

The last generalization has two specific consequences:

\begin{itemize}
  \item There are no prepositional complements
  \item If the relation of NP-arguments (subjrel, postsubjrel, objrel,
indobjrel) is made sufficiently vague (this has been done by calling these
{\rel argrel}), then no information on the verbpattern of the intermediate
verbs in  a verb cluster is required to have nonambiguous paths. 

Given this, it is known that the attributes {\att particle, reflexivity, 
prepkey1, prepkey2} of a VRinducer have no significant value. 
Therefore we can use these attributes for other purposes. We use them to 
project the values of these attributes of the {\em  deepest} verb in the 
cluster, where these attributes d\'{o} have a significant value.

Notice that the verbpattern of the {\em  deepest} verb is relevant to exclude 
wrong parsings w.r.t. categories bearing relations other than {\rel indobjrel,
objrel}, in particular categories bearing {\rel predrel} and non-NP categories.
It is projected by using the attribute {\att synvpefs2}.

 \end{itemize}
 



\subsubsection{Final Remarks}

It would have been better if the behavior w.r.t projection had been represented 
directly in the surface rule, e.g. by calling different procedures each 
dealing with a group of attributes that behave identically w.r.t projection.
However, at the moment of the  writing of the 
rule, auxiliary functions and procedures could not be written yet, so that this
has not been done directly, and it has not been done afterwards either.

There are two attributes in the VERB record that have been introduced solely
for the purposes of the surface parser.:

\begin{description}
  \item[foundauxes] It keeps track of the auxiliary verbs found, but only
 to the extent that they are an auxiliary of the verb that is the least
embedded one semantically.
  \item[synvpefs2] It is used to project the value of {\att synvps} of the 
deepest embedded verb in  a verb cluster. It is used crucially in the rule for
VP
\end{description}
 

It would be desirable if we could keep track of the fact that the deepest 
embedded verb is an auxiliary. This would make it possible to
eliminate some (incorrect) structures in the VP rule. See the section on VP.

\subsection{Special Topics}

\subsubsection{The combination of verb and particle}

Wrong combinations of a verb and a particle are filtered out in the surface 
rule. This is done in the following way:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use is made of the generalization that verbraisers and the 
        auxiliary verbs {\em  hebben, zijn, worden, zitten te} etc.
        do not take a particle. As a consequence maximally one particle 
        per  VERBP (and per SENTENCE) can occur.
  \item It is assumed that the particle expected by some verb is represented
        in the attribute {\att particle} of the VERB bearing the relation 
        {\rel head}. This value is assigned to the parameter {\bf expparticle}.
        If there is a complex verb cluster bearing the relation {\rel head}
        the value of the attribute {\att particle} is computed from
        information deeper in the cluster. The exact method is described below.
  \item If a particle is found under VERBP, then the {\att key} of this 
        particle is assigned to the parameter {\bf particlekey}
  \item A condition in the final condition of the VERBP surface rule checks
        whether {\bf expparticle} = {\bf particlekey}. 
\end{itemize}

Crucial use is made of the fact that in complex verb clusters the correct value
of the attribute {\att particle } of the topmost VERB is computed. The computation
of this attribute is done in the surface rule Vcomplex. The attribute 
{\bf particle} behaves as a so-called {\em  deep attribute} w.r.t. projection.
However, there are two special cases: 

\begin{itemize}
  \item If some node VERB requires a particle and it is combined 
        with a particle under a new node VERB, then the attribute 
        {\att particle } of this new VERB is set to 0.
  \item If some node VERB requires a particle, but it contains this particle
        already itself under the SUBVERB dominated by the VERB node (this
        can be seen by inspecting the attribute {\att status}), then
        the value of  the attribute {\att particle} is not projected in the
        rule Vcomplex, and the parameter {\bf expparticle} is not assigned a
        value in the rule %VP
\end{itemize}

In cases like {\em   opbellen wou} (particled infinitive plus modal
verb), the surface parser requires special conditions to guarantee that
the structure is not analyzed as {\em PART + VERB[ VERB VERB] }. This is
guaranteed in the rule {\em Vcomplex} (box no. 9), where it is required that
a particle appears if it is expected by the verb.

Finally, special measures must be taken to prevent an analysis of the form
VERBP[PART + VERB[ TE VERB] instead of VERBP[VERB[PART VERB[TE  VERB]]]. This
is prevented by the auxiliary function {\em  PARTinVcluster}. 

\subsubsection{The prepositions in prepositional objects}

The specification of which prepositions are required by some verb 
in prepositional objects is represented in the attributes {\att prepkey1} and
{\att prepkey2} of the VERB bearing the relation {\rel head}. These attributes
have received their value either directly from the SUBVERB that the VERB
immediately dominates, or (in a VERB cluster) from some deeper VERB by
projection of these {\em  deep attributes}. 

The values of the attributes
{\att prepkey1} and {\att prepkey2} are assigned to the parameters 
{\att verbprepkeyvar1} and {\att verbprepkeyvar2} respectively. In the 
auxiliary function {\em  prepobjok} the values of these parameters are compared
to the values of the parameters that record the values of the keys of
the PREPs heading prepositional objects found
 ({\att prepkeyvar1, prepkeyvar2}).
 

\subsubsection{Stranded Prepositions}

{\em  Stranded prepositions} are prepositions the complement of which has been 
removed by some process. In Dutch only the RADVs {\em  er, hier, daar, ergens, 
nergens, overal, waar} can be removed from a PREPP.

Stranding of prepositions in Dutch is severely restricted. Basically only 
argument PREPPs may contain stranded prepositions. But there are further 
restrictions. 

The treatment of stranded prepositions is based on the facts enumerated
in appendix~\ref{strandingsents}. The following conclusions can be drawn on the
basis of these sentences:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A stranded preposition immediately precedes the verb or a PREPP 
followed by a VERB. (violated by 7\ref{7a}, 12\ref{12a}, 13\ref{13a} 
(For 13\ref{13c} see below). The VERB must be in a final position. If the
there is no VERB in final position (e.g. in main clauses where the verb is
in second position), still the adjacency requirement mentioned holds, but
now there must be adjacency to the position where the VERB would have been
if it were not in `second position'.

  \item A stranded preposition from a {\em  preprepobjrel} PREPP can occur
behind another PREPP only if this PREPP does not contain a stranded
preposition. 
 ( violated by 5\ref{5d}, 6\ref{6Ad}, 6\ref{6Ae}, 6\ref{6Be}, 8\ref{8d}).
  \item Two stranded prepositions can occur only if one of them is directional
     (violated by 5\ref{5c}, 5\ref{5d}, 8\ref{8c}, 8\ref{8d}).
  \item The postposition of a directional PREPP forms a unit with the verb. It
           behaves in certain respects as a particle (cf. 6\ref{6Ba}, 6\ref{6Bb},
6\ref{6Bc}).
\end{itemize}

Furthermore the following seems to be true as well:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Bare resultative adjectives show particle like behavior 
(cf. 13\ref{13c})
        (This fact is not accounted for in any way, neither 
         in the surface rules, nor in the M-rules. In such cases it must be 
assumed that the particle and the verb are two parts of one lexical item:
        a particle-verb combination)
         
  \item Rform plus pure postposition exclude each other
  \item Certain circumpositions allow deletion of the second preposition if an
   NP-complement is present
\end{itemize}

The treatment of stranded prepositions differs in a number of respects from
the treatment of stranded prepositions in Rosetta2. One major difference
is
that it is assumed that stranded PREP's are normally inside a PREPP. The 
reasons
for assuming this are the following:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Certain stranded prepositions can be modified, e.g.
        {\em  Hij heeft er gisteren {\bf vlak voor} gestaan}
  \item The combination of a preposition and a postposition ({\em  splitprep}s)
        can be stranded: {\em  Hij is er gisteren {\bf naar toe } gegaan}
\end{enumerate}


However, cases of stranded prepositions corresponding to PREPP's bearing one
of the relations {\rel preprepobjrel, vpadvrel, byobjrel} are treated 
(somewhatr arbitrarily) as bare PREP's (directly under VERBP).

Not dealt with (yet) are the cases where stranding of a PREPP has taken place
from an ADJP or from an NP. Stranding of PREP's inside ADJP's is possible,
and must be accounted for in the surface rules dealing with ADJP's. As far
as NP's are concerned, it seems that stranding of PREPP's in NP's is not 
possible at all. In cases where this appears to be the case, one can usually
argue that the PREPP is not inside the NP. (See also Koster 
1987)\footnote{Koster, Jan (1987) {\bf Domains and Dynasties. The Radical
Autonomy of Syntax.} Dordrecht:Foris Publications}

\subsection{Final Condition/Action}

The final Condition consists of a number of boolean expressions. Most of these
are function calls, and have been discussed above.


The Final action consists of a verbpattern reduction part, 
and three parts in which verbp's are made:

In the {\bf Reduction part} the parameters recording the verbpatterns
are reduced as much as possible.

The other three parts are:

\begin{description}
  \item[Passive Part] In this part passive VERBP's are made.
         The attribute {\att Vmatrixhead}, which records whether the
verb found in the VERBP is also the main verb of the whole SENTENCE that is
going to be parsed, is set to {\val true}, since in passive VERBP's the head of
VERBP is always also the main verb of the whole SENTENCE. 
  \item[Active Part, Vmatrixhead=true] In this part active VERBP's are made.
where the head of the VERBP is also the main verb of the SENTENCE.  

  \item[Active Part, Vmatrixhead=false] In this part active VERBP's are made.
where the head of the VERBP is {\em not} the main verb of the SENTENCE.  
\end{description}

  \typing \to BOOLEAN

\subsection{The Boxes}
\label{sboxsection}


In Hinit the parameters are given a default value. Special care must be taken
in the case of the parameter {\em v2subcvar}. Its default value is
{\em mainverb}. The problem with this specification is that it might yield
incorrect results if the parameter is used without checking whether a verb
in second position has been found.

\begin{boxes}

\begin{boxitem}
\boxcategory SENTENCE
\boxrelation leftdislocrel
\boxintention `subject sentences'
\boxremarks Only finite sentences and infinitival sentences without {\em  te}
are allowed as `subject sentences'. Other options are certainly excluded
(cf. e.g. {\em  *{\bf (om) te zwemmen} is leuk}. It is not clear that
constructions such as {\em  boeken lezen} as in {\em  boeken lezen is leuk}
which are analyzed here as infinitival sentences, are infinitival sentences
indeed. They might very well be dominated by an NP node, cf.  the fact
that they can appear in subordinate clauses rather easily ({\em hij zei 
dat boeken lezen leuk was}) in contrast to finite `subject sentences' (cf.
{\em  *Hij zei dat dat hij dit boek las leuk was}), See also Koster(198?).
\end{boxitem}

\boxremarks \noremarks

\subsection{Final Condition/Action}

The final condition and action check whether the structure postulated up to
now is compatible with certain conditions, and if so it creates a new
topnode of category SENTENCE and computes the values of the attributes
of the record of the new topnode.


\subsubsection{Conditions}

In the final condition many conditions must be satisfied. All these
conditions check the consistency between two or more independent
values of parameters.

The following conditions must hold:
\begin{itemize} 
  \item {\em ((NOT leftdislocpuncfound) OR (shiftrelfound ))}: to prevent sentences 
such as
as {\em  dat hij ziek is, verbaast mij} and {\em  boeken kopen, is leuk}.
   \item  {\em VPandV2ok}: to check the consistence of the VERBP, 
                         if any and the verb in second position, if any.
   \item  {\em conjandverbok}: to check the consistency of the CONJ found, if 
any and parameter {\para finitenessvar}.        
   \item  {\em allpreadvsok}: to check whether {\em  preadvrel}s are allowed if
they occur
   \item  {\em SVAOK}: to check subject-verb agreement                       
   \item  {\em noofargsok}: to check whether there are not too many arguments
   \item  {\em reflexivesok}: to check whether the reflexive pronouns, if 
present, are allowed
   \item  {\em ( (NOT(shiftrelcases = [Nominative])) OR\\
            ((NOT subjrelfound) AND (NOT postsubjrelfound))\\
          )                                                 }: If the
NP bearing relation {\em  shiftrel} has only {\em Nominative} as an element
in the set value of the attribute {\att cases}, then it must be the subject,
so that neither {\rel  subjrel} nor {\rel  postsubjrel} can occur in the 
structure.
   \item  {\em (particlekey = expparticle)}: To check  whether the
particle found is the particle expected.                  
   \item  {\em prepobjok}: to check whether the prepositional objects
found are allowed to appear.
   \item  {\em infok} to impose some additional conditions if the sentence is
infinitival.
   \item  {\em dirargnpok }   to prevent wrong parses of NP as 
{\em  dirargrel}.
   \item  {\em IMPLIES(conjsentinm1found, (postvok OR verbpfound))}: to
avoid a double parsing of adverbial sentences such as in 
{\em  Hij ging weg zonder om te kijken}, to avoid parsing of the adverbial
sentence as a sentence-internal adverbial clause (with the sentence-final
part being empty).
\end{itemize}

In the actions part there are first reductions of the pattern variables, and then
there is a condition to check whether the pattern variables are still nonempty.

\subsubsection{Subject Verb Agreement}
\label{sva}

In this section we explain how subject-verb agreement is accounted for in the
surface parser.

First it will be indicated which relevant configurations must be taken into 
consideration. The treatment of subject-verb-agreement is based on the
assumption that all relevant configurations are enumerated here. If some
configuration relevant  to subject verb agreement is not taken into 
consideration here, subject verb agreement might operate incorrectly.

The traditional notion {\em  subject} can be realized in several different
ways in Rosetta3. The following table lists these:

\begin{description}
  \item[subjrel] e.g. {\em  Zwemt {\bf hij}?}, i.e. in nondeclarative main clauses,
in declarative main clauses with some nonsubject topicalized and in subordinate
clauses. In the case of main clauses special care must be taken to deal with 
cases of {\em  inversion}, i.e. the inflected present tense finite verb 
precedes a second person subject. Furthermore, special care must be
taken of the NPs headed by {\em  het, dit, dat}.
  \item[postsubjrel] e.g. {\em  er zwemt {\bf iemand}}. In this case only 
indefinite NPs, hence only third person NPs can appear.
  \item[objrel] e.g. {\em  er komt {\bf iemand}}, or as in 
{\em ..dat hem {\bf het boek}  gegeven werd}. In the first subcase only 
indefinite NPs, hence only third person NPs can appear. In the second subcase
both definite and indefinite NPs can appear, but personal pronouns cannot
(cf. {\em  *..dat hem {\bf ik/mij/zij/haar} voorgesteld werd}), so that 
also here only third person NPs can appear.
  \item[shiftrel] Here three subcases can be distinguished:
\begin{description}
  \item[in the same clause, not {\em er}] e.g. {\em  {\bf Ik} zwem}, i.e. in declarative 
main clauses and in wh-interrogative and relative clauses.
  \item[in the same clause, {\em  er}] e.g. {\em  Er werd gedanst}.
In this case the inflected verb must be in third person. Special care must be
taken of the NPs headed by {\em  het, dit, dat}.
  \item[in some higher clause] e.g. {\em  {\bf wie} denk je dat dit zal doen}, i.e.
if the subject is  wh-shifted, topicalized or relativized into a superordinate
clause. In
this case first and second person pronouns cannot occur (cf. e.g. {\em 
*{\bf Ik} geloof jij niet dat dat zal doen}). Use  is made of this fact below.
\end{description}
  
  \item[erposrel] e.g. {\em  Wordt er gedanst}. In thsi case the inflected verb 
must be in third person.
  \item[subject is absent] e.g. {\em  In de tuin werd gedanst}, {\em  daar werd niet naar 
gekeken}. In these cases the inflected verb must be in third person. 
\end{description}



Subject Verb Agreement is accounted for by the function {\em  SVAOK} which is
called in the final condition.
This function overaccepts, i.e. it accepts certain configurations of 
subject-verb agreement that are incorrect in Dutch, e.g. it accepts
{\em  Is dit de problemen} and {\em  zijn dit het probleem?}. Of course, such
cases will be rejected by the M-GRAMMAR, but the surface parser is (currently)
too primitive to be able to reject these.
The main task of SVAok is to reduce the number of incorrect parses. 
It performs this task rather succesfully. It is succesful in this task
because of the following reasons:

\begin{description} 
  \item[] The function SVAok can always determine correctly what the value
of the attribute {\att  persons} of the inflected verb should be.
  \item[] There are many false parses due to differences in the attribute
{\att persons} of the inflected verb (if SVAOK would not exist)
  \item[] The function SVAOK cannot always determine what the value of the
attribute {\att number} of the inflected verb should be.
  \item[] There are hardly any false parses due to differences in the attribute
{\att number} of the inflected verb (if SVAOK would not exist)
\end{description}


The function {\em SVAOK} itself calls several functions, each dealing with
a specific configuration. These functions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item SVAinvok
  \item SVAnormsubjok
  \item SVAspecsubjok
  \item SVApostsubjok
  \item SVAshiftok. This function calls the function {\em shiftetcetera},
        which in its turn calls the following functions:
           \begin{enumerate}
             \item SVANomshiftok
             \item SVAspecNomshiftok
             \item SVAerok
             \item SVArelprook
            \end{enumerate}
  \item SVAelseok
\end{enumerate}

The function SVAOK partitions the structures it applies to in three classes.
For each of these classes separate conditions hold. This is represented
in the following table:

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
description           & SVAOK :=\\
\hline
\hline
infinitival sentences & true\\
\hline
imperative sentences  & there is no subject, or\\
                      &  the {\em  person} of\\
                      &  the subject = 2 or 4\\
\hline
all other sentences   & see below\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

The remaining cases to be dealt with are the finite non-imperative sentences.
For these it is first determined whether the configuration contains 
{\em  inversion}, i.e. whether the present tense verb form precedes
a second person singular subject. If so, the boolean {\em  inversionfound}
is set to {\em  true}.


The relevant structures can be partitioned further by determining the 
realization of the subject of the sentence. This partitioning has been
indicated in the following table, where it is also indicated which function
takes care of the relevant configuration:

\begin{description}
  \item[Configuration with Inversion] SVAinvok (e.g.{\em  Kun jij komen?})
  \item[Configuration with normal subject] SVAnormsubjok (e.g.
Heeft hij dit gekocht?)
  \item[Configuration with special subject] SVAspecsubjok (e.g. {\em  Zijn dit
de problemen?})
  \item[Configuration with a postsubject] SVApostsubjok (e.g. {\em  Er zwemt 
iemand in de tuin})
  \item[Configuration with subject in {\em shiftrel}] SVAshiftok (e.g.
{\em  Hij zwemt})
  \item[Other configurations] SVAelseok (e.g. {\em Komt er iemand?}) 
\end{description}

Each of these cases will be discussed now separately:

\begin{description}
  \item[SVAinvok] If we are dealing with a configuration of inversion,
and there is a subject, then
the attribute {\att number} must be {\val  singular} and the value
{\val  0} must be in the attribute {\att verbpersons }.
  \item[SVAnormsubjok] The configuration with a normal subject holds if
there is a subject, there is no inversion and the subject is not a special
subject (i.e. it is not an NP headed by {\em  het, dit} or {\em  dat}).
If this configuration holds, the person and number of the subject must be
compatible with the corresponding attributes on the verb. 
  \item[SVAspecsubjok] The configuration with special subject holds if
there is a subject, there is no inversion and the subject is a special
subject (i.e. it is an NP headed by {\em  het, dit} or {\em  dat}). If this
configuration holds nothing can be said about the attribute {\att number}
 of the verb
(cf. {\em  Is dit het probleem} vs. {\em  Zijn dit de problemen}), but
the attribute {\att persons} of the verb must have the value {\val  3}.
  \item[SVApostsubjok ] The configuration with a postsubject holds if there
is a {\rel postsubjrel} but no {\rel subjrel}. In this configuration the 
inflected verb must be compatible with the attributes {\att person} and
{\att number} of the NP bearing the relation {\em postsubjrel}.
  \item[SVAshiftok] The configuration with subject in {\em shiftrel} holds
if there is no {\rel subjrel}and  no {\rel postsubjrel} and there is a
{\em  shiftrel}. If this configuration holds, the function {\bf Shiftetcetera}
is called.
This function distinguishes four configurations and their associated conditions:
\begin{description}
  \item[SVANomshiftok]  This configuration holds if the {\rel shiftrel}
is a normal NP (i.e. an NP not headed by {\em  dit,dat}, or {\em het})
and the attribute {\att cases} contains the value {\val Nominative} as its
only value. In this configuration the inflected verb must be compatible with
the values of attributes {\att  person} and {\att number} of this NP.
  \item[SVAspecNomshiftok] This configuration holds if the {\rel shiftrel}
is a special NP (i.e. an NP headed by {\em  dit,dat}, or {\em het})
and the attribute {\att cases} contains the value {\val Nominative} as its
only value. In this configuration nothing can be said about the attribute
{\att number} of the finite verb, but the attribute {\att persons} must contain 
the value {\val 3}.
  \item[SVAerok] This configuration holds if the {\rel shiftrel} is an 
RADVP  containing the RADV {\em  er}. in this configuration nothing
can be said about the attribute {\att  number}, but the attribute
{\att persons} of the verb must contain the value {\val  3}.
  \item[SVArelprook] This configuration holds if a relative pronoun bears the
relation {\rel  shiftrel}, and no subject is present. In this configuration
the value {\val  3} must be in the attribute {\att persons} of the inflected
verb.

This condition excludes sentences such as {\em  ik die groot ben, jij die
ziek bent} etc. If these are correct sentences, and if they can be dealt with 
by the M-GRAMMAR, the condition that {\em  3 IN verbpersonsvar} 
must be removed and set to {\em  true} (as it was in an earlier version
of the surface parser). The condition has been added because without it
many wrong parses are found, and because the M-rules cannot yet deal with 
cases such as {\em  ik die groot ben} etc.
\end{description}


  \item[SVAelseok] This function deals with all configurations not dealt with 
yet, i.e. if the following conditions are satisfied:
\begin{itemize}
  \item There is no subject                , and
  \item There is no postsubject            , and
  \item \mbox{}
\begin{itemize}
  \item there is no {\rel shiftrel}, or
  \item there is a {\em shiftrel} that does not 
         have Nominative as its only value in the attribute {\att cases }
         (if it has this attribute) or that is RELPRO
\end{itemize}

  
\end{itemize}

In these configurations nothing can be said about the attribute {\att number}
of the inflected verb, but the attribute {\att persons} must contain the
value {\val  3}.
\end{description}

\section{Traditional grammatical functions and Rosetta3 relations}
\label{gf}

The following list supplies a  mapping from traditional grammatical functions
onto Rosetta3 relations. The traditional grammatical functions have been taken
from the ANS (they are called {\em  zinsdelen} in ANS(cf. p. 801 ff)). 
An English translation of this Dutch terminology is provided. For each
traditional grammatical function the canonical realizations and
the noncanonical realizations are given. The canonical realizations are the
realizations as specified in the first M-rules that introduce the relevant
elements (e.g. pattern transformations (for arguments), Rpassive (for the
{\em  door}-bepaling, etc.). 

This list has never been used while constructing the surface parser, and for 
this reason it is a rather independent criterion of completeness of the 
(sentence part of the) surface parser. 
As is clear, there are still some gaps that should be filled 
in. These will be discused below. 

Though this list defines a mapping from ANS-{\em  zinsdelen} to Rosetta3 
relations, this does not imply that the grammatical analysis in Rosetta3 is the
same as in ANS for the examples for which a correspondence relation is given.


\begin{description}
  \item[gezegde] (predicate) The predicate can be partitoned into two classes:
     \begin{description}
        \item[werkwoordelijk gezegde](verbal predicate)  {\em  head}\\
Noncanonical realization: {\em auxrel, progauxrel, conjrel}/VERB.\\

In the ANS (and also
elsewhere, traditionally) only verbal forms are counted as predicates or as
parts of the verbal predicate. In Rosetta3 VERBP is considered the predicate,
verbal forms are considerd the {\em  head} of VERBP.

        \item[naamwoordelijk gezegde](nominal predicate) {\em  predrel}/VERBP
(headed by a copula).\\
The nominal predicate consists of two parts:
\begin{description}
  \item[verbal part] {\em  head}\\
Noncanonical realizations: {\em auxrel, progauxrel, conjrel}/VERB

The class of copular verbs has no independent status in Rosetta3. They are 
simply a class of main verbs with verbpatterns from a specific set. The only
copula that has a special status is the verb {\em  zijn}.
  \item[nominal part] {\em  predrel/}ADJP, NP, ADVP, PREPP\\
Not accounted for, but mentioned in the ANS:
\begin{description}
  \item[present participle] All examples mentioned in the ANS are arguably ADJPs
  \item[infinitive (construction)] All examples mentioned are arguably NPs.
  \item[ subordinate clauses] The first two examples mentioned in ANS are 
arguably ADJPs, the last example is an NP (with a free relative).
\end{description}

\end{description}
     \end{description}

  \item[onderwerp](subject) {\em  subjrel, objrel}/NP, 
{\em  leftdislocrel}/SENTENCE,
                              {\em  erposrel/RADVP}\\
The canonical realization is:
\begin{description}
  \item[subjrel] for impersonal verbs and for
nonergative intransitive verbs and transitive verbs 
  \item[objrel] for ergative verbs and passive verbs
  \item[leftdislocrel] for `subject-sentences' (main clauses only)
  \item[erposrel] if the subject is expletive {\em  er}
\end{description}

Noncanonical realizations:

\begin{description}
  \item[subjrel] for ergative verbs passive verbs
  \item[postsubjrel] for indefinite subjects of nonergative intransitive and
transitive verbs if expletive {\em  er} is (or can be) present.
  \item[shiftrel] in declarative main clauses, in wh-interrogative and relative
clauses.
  \item[supershiftrel] if the subject is topicalized, wh-shifted or
relativized into a superordinate clause. 
\end{description}


  \item[lijdend voorwerp](direct object) {\em  objrel/}NP, 
              {\em complrel}/SENTENCE.
Noncanonical realizations of {\em  objrel}: 
\begin{description}
  \item[preadvrel] if it precedes adverbial phrases, NEG or POS
  \item[hetclrel] if it is the pronoun {\em  het} or {\em  't}
  \item[firstclrel] if it is a pronoun (not {\em  het,'t})
  \item[secondclrel] if it is a pronoun and {\rel firstclrel} is occupied by
   some other pronoun
  \item[shiftrel] if it is topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted
\end{description}

Noncanonical realizations of {\em  complrel}: 
\begin{description}
  \item[extraposrel] if it is extraposed
  \item[leftdislocrel] if it is `topicalized'
  \item[absent] in the case of verbraising
\end{description}

Sentential complements are treated in ANS as direct objects. In Rosetta3 they
bear a special relation {\em  complrel}. One reason for doing this is the fact
that a direct object NP and a sentential complement can cooccur (as e.g.
in {\em  Hij dwingt mij weg te gaan}, cf. {\em Ik werd gedwongen weg te gaan}).
The reltree {\em  complrel/SENTENCE} never occurs in surface structures.

  \item[meewerkend voorwerp](indirect object) {\em  indobjrel}/NP,
{\em  aanobjrel}/PREPP, {\em  prepobjrel}/PREPP

In Rosetta3 the traditional notion {\em  indirect object} is split up into
three canonical realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[indobjrel] for NP indirect objects 
  \item[aanobjrel] for prepositional indirect objects headed by {\em  aan} when
                    they precede direct objects
  \item[prepobjrel] for prepositional indirect objects headed by {\em  aan} when
                    they follow direct objects
\end{description}

Noncanonical realizations: 
\begin{description}
  \item[firstclrel] for pronominal indirect objects 
  \item[preadvrel] for NP indirect objects that precede adverbs, NEG, POS, or 
other {\rel preadvrel}s.
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted indirect objects.
  \item[postvporel] for postverbal PP indirect objects
\end{description}

  \item[voorzetselvoorwerp](prepositional object) {\em  prepobjrel}/PREPP

Noncanonical Realizations:
\begin{description} 
  \item[postvporel] for postverbal prepositional objects
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted prepositional  
                 objects
  \item[erposrel] for prepositional object PREPPs containing {\em  er, hier, daar} or
{\em  waar}
\end{description}

  \item[ondervindend voorwerp](experiencer object) {\em  objrel}/NP, 
{\em  indobjrel}/NP

The experiencer object is realized canonically as an {\em objrel}/NP or as an
{\em indobjrel}/NP, depending on the verb it is an argument of. If this verb is
an ergative verb, it is an {\rel indobjrel}, otherwise it is an {\rel objrel}.
Examples of verbs taking an {\rel indobjrel}: {\em  bevallen, meevallen, 
tegenvallen, (ge)lukken, mislukken, overkomen, gebeuren, ontgaan}, etc.

Examples of verbs taking an {\rel objrel}: {\em  spijten, berouwen, 
beangstigen, behagen, mishagen, verheugen, bedrukken, passen, voegen, betamen,
bevreemden, verbazen, verbijsteren, amuseren, baten, hinderen, schaden,
overvallen, dwarszitten 
}, etc.

The cases {\em  waard zijn, zwaar vallen, moeilijk vallen} mentioned in ANS 
probably are idioms (taking {\em  indobjrel}s).

The noncanonical realizations are as usual with {\rel indobjrel}s and 
{\rel objrel}s.

There are good reasons to give the {\em  objrels} of the verbs mentioned above
a different relation (neither {\em  objrel} nor {\em  indobjrel}), e.g.
{\em  expobjrel}, but this has not been done yet in Rosetta3. 
See document R0??? on Verbpatterns.
  \item[oorzakelijk voorwerp](direct object) {\em  objrel}/NP
No distinction is made in Rosetta3 between {\em  lijdend voowerp} and {\em  
oorzakelijk voorwerp}. The only distinction ANS mentions is that {\em  lijdend 
voorwerp} occurs as a complement to  a verb, while {\em  oorzakelijk voorwerp}
occurs as a complement to an  adjective.

The noncanonical realizations are as usual for a {\em  objrel}.
  \item[{\em  door}-bepaling] ({\em by}-phrase) {\em  byobjrel}/PREPP
This PREPP occurs in Passive constructions, but also in so-called {\em  
Dooractive} Constructions (e.g. {\em  Zij liet zich {\bf door Piet} uitkleden}.
ANS does not mention this use.

Noncanonical Realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[erposrel] for {\rel byobjrel}s containing {\em  er, hier, daar, waar}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted {\rel byobjrel}s.
  \item[postvrel] for postverbal {\em by}-phrases.
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van plaats] (locative adjunct){\em  locargrel, locadvrel}/PREPP,ADVP
ANS does not distinguish locative arguments from locative adverbials. 
Nevertheless, there are several reasons for doing so. One example is e.g.
the sentence {\em  Hij heeft in Amsterdam gezeten}. This sentence is ambiguous,
i.e. it can mean he stayed some time in Amsterdam, or that he was in prison in
Amsterdam. The verb {\em  zitten} in the meaning `be in prison' takes no 
locative argument, the verb {\em  zitten} in the meaning `be, stay somewhere'
obligatorily takes a locative argument. The distinction is reflected in the
intonational patterns associated to the two reasons. Under normal intonation
the major sentential accent is on the constituent preceding the verb in VP,
if there is one, on the verb otherwise. The sentence mentioned, under the 
interpretation `stay somewhere' must be read with major sentential accent on 
the phrase {\em in Amsterdam} (which, being an argument, is inside VP), while
under the interpretation `be in prison' the sentence must be read with major
sentential accent on {\em gezeten} ({\em in Amsterdam}, a locative adverbial, 
is outside of VP). 

%Further difference between locative arguments and locative
%adverbials can be found w.r.t. stranding: In general stranding of prepositions
%is slightly better for locative arguments than for locative adverbials
%(cf. {\em  Waar heeft hij naast gewoond?} vs. {\em  ?Waar heeft hij naast 
%gewerkt}, though judgements are not crystal clear here.)

Locative adverbials can follow the verb, locative arguments cannot
(cf. {\em Hij heeft jarenlang gewerkt in Amsterdam} vs 
{\em  *Hij heeft jarenlang gewoond in Amsterdam}). The sentence 
{\em  Hij heeft jarenlang gezeten in Amsterdam} is in contrast to
the sentence {\em  Hij heeft jarenlang in Amsterdam gezeten} not ambiguous.
It can only mean that he was in prison in Amsterdam for several years, not that he
stayed in Amsterdam for several years.

Noncanonical realizations of locative arguments and adverbials are:
\begin{description}
  \item[erposrel] for the locative {\em  er} 
  \item[defRadvrel] for the locatives {\em  hier, daar, waar}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, wh-shifted or relativized locative
                  phrases
  \item[postvrel] for postverbial locative adverbials
\end{description}
 
  \item[richtingsbepaling] (directional adjunct) {\rel  dirargrel}/PREPP,ADVP

In Rosetta3 all directional adjuncts are considered to be arguments. There are
no directional adverbials. Prepositional phrases and ADVPs indicating a 
source (e.g. {\em vanwaar, uit welke stad} etc.) are also considered to be
directional arguments. A better term for directional argument would be
a {\em  path argument}.

Noncanonical realizations of {\rel dirargrel} are:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] only for directional PREPPs with a PREposition, and for
               directional adverbs. 
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van tijd] (time adjunct) {\em  tempadvrel}/PREPP, ADVP, NP,
SENTENCE; {\rel leftdislocrel}/SENTENCE; {\rel posttempadvrel}/SENTENCE

Noncanonical realizations of time adjuncts:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted time adjuncts 
  \item[postvrel] for postverbal PREPP time adjuncts
\end{description}
 

The treatment of temporal adverbial sentences is not fully consistent yet.
The surface  parser yields results that cannot be dealt with (yet) by the
M-grammar. The cause of this is the general discrepancy between the
fact that though M-rules should do syntax (though controlled by semantics),
M-rules dealing with Time do semantics, and hardly any syntax.  

  \item[bepaling van frequentie] (frequency adjunct) {\em  tempadvrel}/PREPP, ADVP, NP
The same comments apply as with time adjuncts.
  \item[bepaling van graad] (degree modifier) {\em vpadvrel}/ADVP,PREPP.
These are treated as verb modifying adverbs. Degree-adverbials
are not explicitly distinguished in the syntax. Maybe this should be done.

Noncanonical realizations are:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for wh-shifted degree modifiers
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van kwantiteit] (quantity adjunct) {\em predrel}/NP.
These are considered to be arguments of verbs. The cases ANS mentions
as nonobligatorily present qunaity adjuncts are not treated in Rosetta3
as quantity adjuncts. Not every phrase expressing a quantity (semantically)
is treated as a predrel/NP. Some are simply direct object NPs. Examples
of real quantity adjuncts: {\em  kost FL. 300,--; weegt een kilo; meet 3 bij 3 
meter}. Examples of direct objects: {\em  drie kilometer gelopen/gewandeld}.
One criterion is: passive is impossible if we are dealing with real quantity 
adjuncts. This analysis is perhaps controversial, and might be looked into 
somewhat more.
  \item[bepaling van causaliteit](causality adjunct) {\rel sentadvrel}/ADVP,PREPP; {\rel 
sentadvrel,leftdislocrel,postsentadvrel}/SENTENCE
SENTENCE
Adverbs and preposition can have the value {\em  caus} or {\em  causadv} in 
their subcs. They are treated as {\em  sentadvrel}s. The value {\em  according}
should be used here as well. Adverbial sentences are not distinguished in any
specific way, and there is no need to do so in syntax.

Noncanonical realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized {\rel sentadvrel}s 
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van gevolg] (consequence adjunct) {\em sentadvrel}/ADVP,PREPP; 
{\rel postsentadvrel/SENTENCE}

See the comments stated at the causality adjunct.
The sentential consequence adjuncts are introduced by the conjunction {\em  
zodat}, which is marked in such a way that it can appear only in a
postsentadvrel/SENTENCE. (attribute {\att distri}).

Noncanonical realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel]  for topicalized causality adjuncts
\end{description}


  \item[bepaling van middel] vpadvrel/PREPP
The PREPP is headed by a preposition that has  the value {\val  instrum} in 
its {\att subcs}.
  \item[bepaling van doel] {\em sentadvrel}/PREPP, {\em postsentadvrel}/SENTENCE
No specific treatment has been adopted, neither for PREPPs nor for SENTENCEs. 
An extension of the surface parser is required.
  \item[bepaling van belang] (benefactive) {\em  voorobjrel/PREPP, prepobjrel}/PREPP
These are treated as arguments of verbs. Only PREPPs headed by the preposition
{\em  voor} are accepted as such. Their treatment as arguments is quite 
problematic. An alternative treatment is proposed in the document on 
Verbpatterns, doc R0???. 

Noncanonical Realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted {\rel voorobjrel}s 
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van veronderstelling en voorwaarde] (adjunct of presupposition
and condition)
         {\em sentadvrel}/ADVP,PREPP, {\em  leftdislocrel, sentadvrel, 
postsentadvrel}/SENTENCE

These are not explicitly distinguished in Rosetta3. Advs and PREPs are dealt 
with if they have {\em  sent(adv)} in their attribute {\att subcs}. Adverbial
sentences introduced by a conjunction or a preposition are not distinguished as
such. Only adverbial sentence not introduced by a conjunction or a preposition,
but by a finite verb (e.g. {\em  Is hij ziek}) are distinguished as being
subordinate adverbial sentence with mood {\val Conditional}.

Noncanonical Realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted {\rel 
sentadvobjrel}/ADVP,PREPP
\end{description}


  \item[bepaling van toegeving]          {\em sentadvrel}/ADVP,PREPP, {\em  leftdislocrel, sentadvrel, 
postsentadvrel}/SENTENCE

These are not explicitly distinguished in Rosetta3.

Noncanonical Realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized, relativized or wh-shifted {\rel 
sentadvobjrel}/ADVP,PREPP
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van hoedanigheid] {\em vpadvrel}/ADVP, {\em agvpadvrel}/ADVP

ADVPs are dealt with adequately. PREPPs (e.g. {\em met plezier, zonder omwegen, in het geheim, bij hoog en bij laag, op
zo'n manier}) are not dealt with at all. The surface
parser should be extended accordingly. 

  \item[bepaling van beperking] sentadvrel/ADVP,PREPP

Adverbs and prepositions with the specification {\em  topic(adv)} are intended
for this use. These have not been dealt with systematically yet. 
  \item[bepaling van vergelijking] sentadvrel/PREPP ; ASP, THANP

The Prepositional phrases mentioned by ANS ({\em  in vergelijking met, 
vergeleken met} are not explicitly distinguished in Rosetta3. It is not clear 
whether they can be dealt with in Rosetta3.

The phrases head by {\em  als} or {\em  dan} are treated as ASP and as THANP.

Sentence introduced by {\em  alsof} are {\rel extraposrel/SENTENCE} if they are
arguments of a verb ({\em  doen alsof, het lijkt alsof}); they are
{\rel postsentadvrel/SENTENCE} otherwise. (e.g {\em  Hij ziet bleek, alsof hij 
ziek is}).
  \item[bepaling van verhouding] (adjunct of proportion)??.\\
Examples: {\em  (Naargelang hij ouder werd, ..; naar hun 
lengte, met de jaren, in vehouding tot de.., naarmate, hoe...des te}.
These are not dealt with at all. 
  \item[bepaling van omstandigheid] (circumstantial adjunct)??.\\
Examples: {\em  bij helder weer, in een wat landerige stemming, met open raam,
onder grote hilariteit, zonder noemenswaard resultaat}. 
These are not dealt with at all.

Adverbial sentences expressing circumstantial adjuncts are dealt with as 
any other adverbial sentence.

  \item[bepaling van modaliteit] (modal adjuncts) sentadvrel/ADVP ;
sentadvrel/PREPP

Adverbs must be marked as having {\val sentadv } in their attribute {\att  subcs}.
Prepositions must be marked as having the value {\val according} in their 
attribute {\att subcs}.

Noncanonical Realizations:
\begin{description}
  \item[shiftrel] for topicalized modal adjuncts.
\end{description}

  \item[bepaling van ontkenning] {\em  negrel}/NEG
  \item[bepaling van bevestiging]{\em  posrel}/POS
  \item[bepaling van gesteldheid] (predicative adjunct)

\begin{description}

Examples: {\em  Hij kwam {\bf naakt} binnen}, {\em  Hij at het vlees {\bf rauw} op}
  \item[volgens de handeling] predrel/ADJP {\em De koffie ruikt {\bf lekker}}

hekje {\bf groen}}

\section{Sentences with stranded prepositions}
\label{strandingsents}

\subsection{Single Stranded PREPs}

The following sentences are examples of stranded prepositions:

\begin{description}
  \item[prepobjrel] Hij heeft er gisteren {\str over} gesproken
  \item[dirargrel]  Hij is er gisteren {\str naar toe} gegaan
  \item[locargrel] Hij heeft er gisteren {\str in} gezeten 
  \item[vpadvrel] Hij heeft de man er gisteren {\str mee} geslagen
  \item[vpadvrel] Hij is er gisteren {\str voor} gekomen
  \item[byobjrel] Hij werd er gisteren {\str door} verrast
\end{description}

The stranded prepositions of {\rel vpadvrel} and {\rel byobjrel} 
must follow {\em  objrel}, though
when not stranded they can (in fact: must\footnote{A sentence such as {\em  Hij
heeft een man met de hamer geslagen} is of course grammatical, but in this
sentence the constituent {\em  een man} does not bear the relation {\em 
objrel}, but the relation {\em  preadvrel}} ) precede {\em  objrel}: 

\begin{itemize} 
  \item \star Hij heeft er gisteren {\str  mee} een man geslagen
  \item Hij heeft met de hamer een man geslagen 
  \item \star \star Er worden  {\str door} ballen afgeschoten
  \item Er worden door dit apparaat ballen afgeschoten
\end{itemize}


PREPPs bearing the relation {\em  aanobjrel, voorobjrel, preprepobjrel} cannot
contain stranded preposions:

\begin{description}
  \item[aanobjrel] \star Hij heeft er gisteren {\str aan} een boek gegeven 
  \item[voorobjrel] \star Hij heeft er gisteren {\str voor} een boek gekocht
  \item[preprepobjrel] \star Hij heeft er waarschijnlijk {\em  van} geen kaas 
gegeten
\end{description}

However, when such PREPP's bear the relation {\rel prepobjrel}, stranding is
possible:

\begin{description}
  \item[] Hij heeft er gisteren  een boek {\str aan} gegeven 
  \item[] Hij heeft er gisteren  een boek {\str voor} gekocht
  \item[] Hij heeft er waarschijnlijk geen kaas {\str  van} gegeten
\end{description}

This probably has to do with the fact that the prepositions in the first  set 
of examples are not adjacent to a verb, though they are in the second set.
