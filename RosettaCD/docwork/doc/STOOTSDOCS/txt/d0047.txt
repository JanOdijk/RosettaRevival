$C1 Introduction
$nl 1
$ This note deals with the description of a subdivision of intransitive verbs,
i.e. verbs that take one argument (and, as we shall see below, some verbs that
take two arguments)$ 
$nl 1
$ The distinction between two types of intransitive verbs has been made
recently in the linguistic literature, originally (to my knowledge) by D.
Perlmutter. In this note we will indicate some of the motivations to
distinguish ergative from nonergative verbs. Most of these motivations
immediately indicate the possibilities of using this distinction in the Rosetta
system.$ 
$nl 1
$ One might wonder what the notion 'ergative', that one generally associates
with rather exotic languages is doing in such 'normal' languages as Dutch or
English. In fact the notion 'ergative' here is a different notion.
Nevertheless, the terminology is not unfortunate. In the exotic languages
intended the notion 'ergative' indicates a certain division between the
function of Cases. In particular, in these languages the subject of an
intransitive verb bears the same Case as the object of a transitive verb (as
opposed to the subject of transitive verb). The terminology 'ergative' for the
more familiar languages such as Dutch, English, Italian or French is fortunate
in the sense that _ergative verbs are intransitive verbs the argument of which
behaves as a direct object_.$ 
$nl 1
$ Let us illustrate some differences between ergative verbs and unergative
verbs in Dutch.$

$C3 Thematic Roles
$nl 1
$ Although 'thematic roles' are vague notions, it is nevertheless useful to
give some indications concerning the relation between the distribution of
thematic roles and ergativity. Ergative verbs never have an 'Agentive'
argument, i.e. an ergative verb never assigns the thematic role 'Agent' to its
argument. Mostly, the argument of an ergative verb bears the thematic role
'Theme' or 'Patient'. In general the argument of an ergative verb undergoes
some sort of 'movement' in the broadest sense of the word movement. In cases
such as 'rijden' the nonergative verb assigns the thematic role 'agent' to its
argument, whereas the nonergative verbs assigns the thematic role 'theme' to
its argument. If we take an NP that does not normally allow the thematic role
'agent', and use it as an argument of the _unergative_ 'rijden', then the
sentence can only be interpreted metaphorically. This, however, is not the case
if we use the same NP as an argument of _ergative_ 'rijden':$
$nl 1

$hr 10,(4)a.
$ @De auto heeft naar Groningen gereden.$
$um
$hr 10,b.
$ ^De auto is naar Groningen gereden$
$um

$ The form of the auxiliary indicates which verb ('ergative' or not) has been
chosen.(_De auto heeft gereden_, is probably well-formed, but must be
interpreted as e.g. 'de auto heeft het gedaan')$

$C3 Nominative-Dative Inversion
$nl 1
$ Certain ergative verbs take two arguments. One of them bears the thematic
role 'theme', the other bears the thematic role 'goal' and usually the
grammatical function 'indirect object'. Some examples from Dutch are:$ 
$nl 1 
       (8) bevallen 'to please'     Dit bevalt hem.
           opvallen 'to strike'     Dit viel hem op.
$nl 1
$ In general it is the case that in Dutch the subject must precede the indirect
object (except in matrix clauses where Topicalization can change phrase order).
Cf:$
$nl 1
(9)a. Jan zei dat Karel(subj) Piet(io) een boek (do)had gegeven
    b.*Jan zei dat Piet(io) Karel(subj) een boek (do) had gegeven
    c. Jan zei dat Karel (subj) die mensen(io) een boek (do) had 
             gegeven
    d.*Jan zei dat die mensen (io) Karel (subj) een boek (do) had 
             gegeven
$nl 1
$ For a restricted set of verbal forms, however,the order can be reversed. In
particular, it is possible for _passive forms_ and for _ergative verbs_:$
$nl 1
       (10)( passives) 
           a. Jan zei dat die mensen dat boek werd gegeven
           b. Jan zei dat hem        dat boek werd gegeven
           c. Jan zei dat dat boek die mensen werd gegeven
           d. Jan zei dat dat boek hem        werd gegeven
$nl 1
       (11) (ergatives)
           a. Jan zei dat hem dat boek wel beviel
           b. Jan zei dat die mensen dat boek was opgevallen
           c. Jan zei dat dat boek hem wel beviel
           d. Jan zei dat dat boek die mensen was opgevallen
$nl 1

$nl 1

           b. Jan zei dat die mensen een boek het meest was bevallen

$nl 1

    c. Er werden Jan boeken gegeven -d. *Er werden boeken Jan gegeven

$C3 Deeply embedded postsubj's are obj's
$nl 1
$ One of the most interesting phenomena w.r.t ergative verbs is the fact that
in sentences such as (15), the NP _drie jongens_ can be the direct object of 
_komen_, so that in this sentence the verb _schijnen_ agrees with the direct 
object of the verb of the embedded infinitive:$
$nl 1
       (15) Er schijnen drie jongens te komen
$nl 1
$ One can see that the NP can be the direct object in such constructions by 
considering its position relative to the indirect object. As one can see
in (16), this NP can follow the indirect object:$
$nl 1
    (16) Er schijnen die man drie jongens te bevallen
         Er schijnen hem     drie huizen te bevallen
         Er schijnen die man drie jongens op te vallen
$nl 1
$ Similarly, and even more easily, this holds for passives:$
$nl 1
    (17) Er schijnen die man drie boeken gegeven te zijn
         Er schijnen aan die man drie boeken gegeven te zijn
$nl 1
$ In all cases, the 'direct object' of the embedded verb can appear before the 
indirect object as well.$
$nl 1
     (18) -- schijn-  [ -- hem drie huizen te bevallen ]
$nl 1
$ This distribution indicates that the relevant NP's here can be direct objects
of the embedded infinitive or postsubj's of the verb _schijnen_.(Postsubj of
the verb _bevallen_ is excluded as we have seen above; or subj of the embedded
verb?). In other languages such sentences have a similar structure. Cf. e.g.
English, where _there_ must appear with existential _be_, but appears with
_seem_ in  _There seem to be three men_, or in Italian, where the _ne_/0&/-test
(see below) unambiguously indicates that the relevant NP is in the direct
object position: _Ne sembrano venire molti_ 'Many of them seem to come' The
proper treatment of such cases depends on the proper treatment of verbs like
_seem_.$

$C3 Morphology
$nl 1
$ Certain derivational processes are sensitive to the ergative-nonergative 
distinction. In particular, e.g. the formation of nouns from verbs by the
suffix -er/-aar in Dutch, and a similar process in English that uses the suffix
-er requires that the relevant verb is not ergative, cf. *_overlijder_, 
*_valler_, *_ganger_ etc. vs. _loper_, _slaper_, _werker_. Furthermore,
one can form new (transitive) verbs from existing intransitive ones in Dutch 
by prefixing them with _be_-, provided that they are unergative:
cf. _fietsen_ ==> _befietsen_; _rijden_ ==> _berijden_, _discussieren_ ==>
_bediscussieren_, redeneren ==> _beredeneren_, but not _gaan_ ==> _begaan_,
_komen_ ==> _bekomen_, _vallen_ ==> _bevallen_, _sterven_ ==> _besterven_.
(In all these cases the derived forms do exist, but have a totally 
unpredictable meaning w.r.t. the simple verb). For some examples from English,
see LI 1985: ^^$

$C2 Other languages
$nl 1
$ In other languages similar phenomena can be found as described above for
Dutch. We will give two examples, but there are many relevant phenomena. One
phenomenon concerns Italian, and one concerns English.$ 
$nl 1
$C3 Italian
$nl 1
$ One of the most famous examples supplying evidence for the distinction
between ergatives and unergatives, is the behavior of the Italian form
comparable to Dutch _er_ 'of it,them'. In Italian the form for this word is
either _ne_ or zero. Whether _ne_ or the _zero_-form appears depends on the
position occupied by the argument this element appears in. When it appears in a
preverbal subject, then the zero-form must appear:$ 
$nl 1
       (19)a. Tre 0&/  sono  arrivati    'Three of them have arrived'
           b. Tre 0&/  hanno telefonato  'Three of them have phoned'
           c. *Tre ne sono  arrivati
           d. *Tre ne hanno telefonato
$nl 1
$ When it appears in a direct object, then _ne_ must appear:$
$nl 1
       (20)a. Ne ho visto tre 'I have seen three of them'
           b.*Ho visto tre 0&/
$nl 1
$ When it appears in a postverbal subject, then if the verb is ergative, then
'ne' must appear:$
$nl 1
       (21)a. Ne sono arrivati tre
           b.*Sono arrivati tre
$nl 1
$ If the verb is not ergative, then neither 'ne' nor the zero-form can appear:$
$nl 1
       (22)a.*Ne hanno telefonato tre
           b.*Hanno telefonato tre
$nl 1
$ And if the verb is passive, then _ne_ must appear:$
$nl 1
       (23)a. Ne sono presentati tre     'Three of them have been introduced'
           b.*Sono presentati tre
$nl 1
$ Again these facts show that the argument of an ergative verb behaves in the
same way as a direct object of a transitive verb under certain circumstances
(in this case: if it is postverbal).$
$nl 1
$C3 English
$nl 1
$ In English _there_ insertion is much more limited than in e.g. Dutch. In
Dutch basically any subject can become a postsubj provided the subject is
indefinite. In English this is possible only for a limited set of verbs. _Be_
is the most normal case, but such verbs as _arrive_, _come_, _go_ can be
subject to this rule as well. For other verbs this possibility is fully
excluded, however, in particular for transitive verbs, and for a set of
intransitive verbs (e.g. _sleep_, _work_, _talk_). The verbs that can be
subject to this rule are the ergative verbs and the existential verb _be_.$

$nl 1
$ The facts mentioned can all be accounted for adequately by assuming that
ergative verbs take an _object_-argument, whereas unergatives take a
subject-argument. Thus the strict subcategorization of the verb 'aankomen' is
as follows:$
$nl 1

$nl 1
       (25)                werken  : --

say that ergative verbs assign their thematic role to the object, whereas 
unergatives assign their thematic role to the subject.
In all cases either the relevant properties follow directly from this
assumption, or an interesting correlation can be shown to exist with other
facts, given the assumption, so that the specific properties mentioned here are
simply a subcase of a more general case.$

$C3 Choice of Auxialary
$nl 1
$ Ergative verbs take 'zijn', not 'hebben' as an auxiliary: This follows if it
is assumed that the auxiliary 'zijn' can appear iff there is a (0&--dependency)
relation between the object-position and the subject-position (most often: the
subject-position is filled and originates in, hence binds the empty
object-position). If no such relation holds, then the auxiliary 'hebben' must
appear.$
$ The relation between subject and object should be characterized in a more
precise way (in particular to account for the cases of non-movement of the
direct object NP), but this is not necessary for our purposes here ). The
important point is that structures containing ergatives look exactly like 
passives in the relevant respects, hence the ergatives will behave w.r.t. 
choice of auxiliary in the same way as passives do. In fact the following
situations occur (abstracting from nonmovement):$
$nl 1
-ergatives take 'zijn': the object is empty and bound by the 
 subject.
-unergatives take 'hebben': since there is no object, there 
 cannot be the relevant relation.
-transitives take 'hebben': there is an independent object.
-passives take 'zijn': the object is empty and bound by the 
 subject
$nl 1
$ There is one set of exceptions to the account sketched here. Certain
transitive verbs allow both _hebben_ and _zijn_ as an auxiliary. The relevant
cases are:$ 
$nl 1
$ -_Vergeten_ In the sense 'niet gedacht hebben aan, verzuimd hebben' it takes
 _hebben_ or _zijn_. In the sense 'zich niet meer herinneren' it takes _zijn_.
 Cf.$
$nl 1
    (26)a. _Hij heeft het gedicht vergeten_ 
            'He forgot to take along the poem'
         b. _Hij is het gedicht vergeten_
            'He forgot to take along the poem' or
            'He does not remember the poem' 
$nl 1
$ -_verliezen._ ('to lose something'). It takes _hebben_ or _zijn_. Ik heb/ben
 m'n portemonnee verloren. (_kwijtraken_ takes _zijn_ only)$
$nl 1
$ -_volgen_. In the sense 'nadoen, navolgen' it takes _hebben_. In the sense
 'achternagaan' it takes _hebben_ or _zijn_. 'Vondel heeft Vergilius gevolgd'
 vs. 'Men heeft/is de spion gevolgd'$
$nl 1
$ -_opvolgen_. In the sense 'gehoor geven aan' it takes _hebben_. In the sense
 'successeren' it takes _hebben_ or _zijn_. 'Hij heeft het bevel opgevolgd';
 'Willem V is/heeft Willem IV opgevolgd'$

$C3 Thematic Roles
$nl 1
$ Ergative verbs never take a agent-argument, because agent-arguments can
appear only in subject-position (but ergatives take no argument in
subject-postion) or (in passives) in a _by-phrase_ (which cannot appear here).$
$nl 1
$C3 Impersonal Passives (Er-passives)
$nl 1
$ An absolutely necessary (but not sufficient) requirement for a verb to be
able to passivize is that it selects an argument in the subject position. This
one can see if one takes a monadic verb like 'schijnen', that takes only an
argument in the object position (a sentence). As predicted by the condition
stated, _schijnen_ cannot passivize (*Er werd geschenen dat hij kwam; * er werd
door hem geschenen/schijnen te komen ). Similarly, verbs like _regenen_ or
_sneeuwen_, which take no argument (hence no argument in subject-position),
cannot be passivized (*Er werd geregend/gesneeuwd). Since ergative verbs do not
take an argument in subject position under the assumption proposed, they cannot
passivize.$ 
$nl 1
$C3 Past Participles as Noun-modifier
$nl 1
$ Apparently, in the clear cases, N has a 'direct object' relation to the past 
participle in the configuration NP[...PastPart N ...]. (where the clear cases 
are the cases where the past participle has been formed from a transitive 
verb). Under the assumption made, it follows automatically that the past 
participle can be formed from ergative intransitive verbs, but not from 
unergatives, because only in the former case we are dealing with a 'direct 
object' relation.$
$nl 1
$C3 Nominative Dative Inversion
$nl 1
$ Somehow, it must be accounted for that postsubj's get the correct Case 
(Nominative), and that the inflected verb agrees (perhaps indirectly via _er_)
with the postsubj. This mechanism can be extended easily to allow the direct 
object to fulfill the same function as the postsubj. If we do so, then the 
following simple statement accounts for all cases of Nominative- Dative 
inversion:$
$nl 1
(27)   The relation a postverbal NP (the 'postsubj') bears if the verb is 
       ergative or passive is _obj_ 
$nl 1
$ I.e. In the case of ergatives and passives, the element that bears what is
called the 'postsubj' relation in Rosetta2, bears in fact the relation 'obj',
and it behaves accordingly (to the right of an indirect object, like all 
direct objects). In the case of transitive verbs or unergative intransitives
such a postverbal NP bears the relation _postsubj_ (or whatever, but not
_obj_), and accordingly it does not behave like a direct object (it is 
positioned before the indirect object). In fact what we have here in the case 
of ergatives and passives is that the NP that is the direct-object argument
occupies the direct-object position even at the surface, although it normally
moves into the subject-position. The (dummy) subject position is filled with
_er_, is left empty (in subordinate clauses and in matrix clauses if the
COMP-node is filled with some adverb or PP),or (Den Besten 198?) the 'indirect
object' moves into the subject-position, hence _er_ need not (and in fact:
cannot) appear). Perhaps this accounts for the fact that there need not
necessarily be _er_ in the clause as a subject, and for the fact that even
definites can appear in the postverbal position.$

$C3 English
$nl 1
$ The distribution of postsubj's in English follows from the assumption made
plus the simple assumption that English does not have a rule that moves an
element from the subject-position to the postsubj-position, i.e. there are no
postsubj's in English. The elements that look like postsubj's are in fact
obj's. The only special case is existential _be_. (actually, I think, only

$C1 Application to Rosetta3/4
$nl 1
$ There are many ways in which we might use the distinction ergative/unergative
in the Rosetta system. Some suggestions are made here. Some are quite simple, 
others, however,  are more 'drastic' (as compared to Rosetta2).$
$nl 1
$ 1) Use it as a way to fill the dictionary w.r.t certain features, e.g. w.r.t.
the attributes _voltvormen_, _possvoices_, and an attribute determinining
whether the past participle of a V can appear as a modifier of N (not present
in Rosetta2, as far as I can see). Similarly, for attributes for morphological
properties (derivation ). In particular, if a verb is specified w.r.t. one of
the attributes in the existing dictionary, we can specify it w.r.t. the other
attributes mentioned here in a default way, so that only certain exceptions,
that are listed in grammars anyway(we hope), are to be specified w.r.t. these
attributes by hand.$
$nl 1
$ 2) Define  new verbpatterns for ergative verbs, so that these verbs really
take a direct object argument instead of a subject argument, and 
concomitantly (re)defining rules that turn a direct object into a subject.
See the appendix for a proposal in which this is worked out somewhat.

A potential problem, caused by the treatment of _bevallen_ as an ergative verb
is caused by the fact that _NP1 pleases NP2_ is also a correct translation of
_NP1 bevalt NP2_, but that _please_ does not have the verb-pattern _vp2erg_,
but is a normal transitive verb. This means that we must allow for translation
of verbpatternrules into other verbpatternrules, in particular it must be 
allowed that _RVP2ERG_ is translated into the verbpattern rule for transitive 
verbs (RVP2-rules). It seems to me, however, that there there are no 
conceptual problems with this, if we restrict this possibility to a translation
of verbpatternrules that deal with the same number of arguments. The verb 
pattern rules, after all, determine the mapping of arguments (represented by
variables) to grammatical functions. There is, however, perhaps a slightly 
different problem, i.e. in the case of more than one variable, how do we 
guarantee that the correct variable is associated with the correct grammatical
function, so that _Jan slaat Piet_ is not translated into _Piet hits Jan_.
(This suggests that we would need a characterization of argument roles, or
something that simulates such an effect. Distinguishing 3 different types of 
variables(e.g. by an index 1,2,3) seems to be sufficient, with default 1-->
subject, 2-->direct object, 3-->indirect object.

exceptions e.g. opvallenstrike:  (2,3)

where opvallen is regular: (2 -->obj; 3--> indobj) and
      strike             : (2 -->subj; 3--> obj)

e.g             bevallenplease:  (2,3):

     bevallen is regular: (2-->obj; 3--> indobj)
     please             : (2-->subj; 3-->obj)

and for that matter:
    bevallenlike:    (2,3)

    bevallen is regular: (2-->obj; 3-->indobj)
    like               : (2-->obj; 3-->subj)
  (or, as above, regular)
