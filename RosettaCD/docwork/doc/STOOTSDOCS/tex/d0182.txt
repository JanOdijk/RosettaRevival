  \item Probleem met generieke NP's:\\

boeken - los libros\\

\section{Linguistenbijeenkomst 9-3-'87}
\begin{itemize}
\item {\bf Probleem NPVARs}\\
Niet alle attributen van de NP zijn inherent. {\em Cases} krijgt bijvoorbeeld 
een waarde door de context. Deze wordt toegekend aan de NPVAR. Nu ontstaat er 
een probleem als er twee NPVARs in een zin staan met dezelfde index, maar 
verschillende waarden voor {\em cases}, en in de implementatie de records voor 
deze variabelen fysiek hetzelfde object zijn. De Softies willen dat omdat het
efficienter zou zijn (en het in Rosetta2 ook al het geval was).\\
Besluit:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Dit is geen goede implementatie. Het moeten verschillende objecten zijn.
\item We zullen nadenken over structurering van records, aangezien dit probleem 
veroorzaakt wordt door het feit dat er inherente en niet-inherente 
attributen zijn. (Wel pas voor Rosetta4!)
\end{enumerate}
\item {\bf dezelfde/hetzelfde}\\
Voor analoge behandeling van de volgende zinnnen is het noodzakelijk dat {\em 
zelfde} als een expressie met een variabele op te vatten en in de morfologie(?) 
regels te hebben die {\em de} + {\em zelfde} en {\em het} + {\em zelfde} aan 
elkaar plakken:
\begin{enumerate}
\item de even grote man als Piet zag
\item dezelfde man als Piet zag
\end{enumerate}
\item Volgende keer: vrijdag 13 maart a.s. 9.30 uur
\begin{enumerate}
\item Adverbia
\item Tijdsadverbials
\item Planning
\end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

\section{Two notes on M-rules.}

The two points are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A note on the pre-action
  \item A (linguistic) problem w.r.t identificational sentences
\end{enumerate}

The first point has been discussed with Carel Fellinger, and both problems
have been mentioned and discussed in the meeting of linguists dd. 23-02-87.

\subsection{ Pre-action}

In order to make the application of certain transformations (in particular 
shift rules ) more deterministic, it is desirable to have the pre-action 
differ per rule, or at least per group of rules.

Consider e.g. a rule that introduces relative clauses. Such a rule substitutes
a CN for a CNVAR that is located in a SENTENCE as a shiftrel:
\begin{description}
  \item[substituent] CN
  \item[substituee]  SENTENCE[ shiftrel/CNVAR, ... ]
\end{description}

The point is: how does the CNVAR get shifted at all?

It does not have any attribute values that can serve as a trigger for
the application of the shift rules. As a consequence, for every CNVAR it must
be attempted to shift, and not to shift. Similarly, if pronouns are 
relativized ({\em ik, iemand} etc.), then for all NPVARs a shift must be
applied, and not applied. 

We might avoid this ambiguity, by setting an (existing) attribute of the
relevant variables in the pre-action, on the basis of the Rule (not on the 
basis of the record of the substituent): e.g. the attribute {\em  mood} of the
variable is  set to {\em relative} in the pre-action of the rules that 
introduce relative clauses.

It is an extension of the use of the pre-action, as planned until now
(cf. doc. 0159  Jan Landsbergen - Variables and pre-actions, esp. Note 1).

By this extension, the pre-action is specific to a group of rules, or to
individual rules. 

\subsection{ Identificational sentences and agreement}

Consider the following sentences:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \ Dit {\em is} het probleem
  \item \ Dit {\em  zijn} de problem{\em en}
  \item *Dit {\em is } de problem{\em en }
  \item *Dit {\em zijn} het probleem
\end{enumerate}

From these sentences one can conclude that in these constructions agreement
takes place between the verb and the postverbal NP. 

Now consider :

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \ Dit schijn{\em t} het probleem te zijn
  \item \ Dit schijn{\em en } de problem{\em  en} te zijn
  \item *Dit schijn{\em  en} het probleem te zijn
  \item *Dit schijn{\em t} de problemen te zijn
\end{enumerate}

In these sentences, the verb of the matrix sentence agrees with the complement
of the embedded verb {\em zijn}.

This fact would make the agreement rule rather nonlocal. In order to 
avoid this, it seems reasonable to make (the NP dominating) the word {\em dit }
agree with the complement of {\em zijn} (a local rule), and to make (the NP
dominating ) {\em dit} agree with 
the verb that heads the sentence (the NP dominating) {\em  dit} is the subject
of (also a local rule). The nonlocal aspect is now removed from these rules,
and put into the transformation of NP-raising, which splits up the nonlocal 
part into a number of local steps. The rule of NP-raising is necessary anyway.
The analysis sketched gains in plausibility by the fact that in English  and
Spanish the corresponding words for {\em  dit} in these constructions overtly
show the number distinction, cf. in English

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \ {\em This} seems to be the problem 
  \item \ {\em These} seem to be the problems
  \item *{\em This} seem(s) to be the problems
  \item *{\em These } seem(s) to be the problem
\end{enumerate}


The change from singular to plural {\em dit} must be made in the rules that
form the construction. What should happen is that the 
variable that functions as the placeholder for the NP containing {\em  dit} 
gets the number required. At the application of the subject-verb agreement
rule the number attribute of this variable  is set to singular again, after
which substitution of NP can take place normally.

{\bf The consequence of this analysis is that the variable temporarily has 
other properties than its substituent !!}

This consequence was found not acceptable by me (partially intuitively, 
partially, because I didn't know how this would interact with the pre-action 
etc.) , and I have actually written rules that introduce the NP containing
{\em dit} directly (without an NPVAR). Now the record of the NP is changed,
a solution slightly more acceptable to me.

The following method appears to be a clear alternative to avoid all this:
Make {\em dit} ambiguous between singular and plural, so that one never has
to change  the NP records dominating these, but can simply check, whether they
have the appropriate values for the relevant attributes. 

This method, however,
is not available, since it would be necessary to block plural dit-NPs in 
all other contexts, cf.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \ Dit bewijst niets
  \item *Dit bewijz{\em en} niets
\end{enumerate}

This probably can be done, but it requires very peculiar conditions on 
start rules, or verbpattern rules, which clearly show that something has been
missed. Basically, it captures a certain generalization (plural {\em dit}
occurs only in identificational constructions (under specific conditions))  by
stating its complement in several different rules (in every rule that starts a
nonidentificational construction plural {\em  dit} is excluded ). 

Such a solution is not acceptable to me either.

All suggestions for other solutions, or for new viewpoints on the solutions
sketched, are welcome.
