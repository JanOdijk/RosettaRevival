\section{Introduction}

This document contains a description of the Rule and Transformation classes
referred to in the docs. 149,150,151 that are common to the three languages
or that have not been split out fully per language.

For each rule and transformation class the sets HEADCATS, IMPORTCATS and
EXPORTCATS will be specified.

Filter classes are not, or hardly discussed, since they usually have no other
function than guaranteeing the application of a transformation in the
cases required. 


\newpage
\section{ TC: ADJpatternrules}

\begin{description}
  \item[HEADCATS] ADJPPROP
  \item[IMPORTCATS] --
  \item[EXPORTCATS] ADJPPROP
\end{description}

If the adjective takes no argument, then a dummy pronoun appears as a
subject.

In Dutch this is the dummy pronoun {\em het}, in English {\em it}, and in
Spanish it is an abstract reduced nominative pronoun (e.g. {\em ello} reduced).
It will have to be deleted in Spanish later in the derivation. See
TC:subjpronoundeletion in doc. 154.

It is assumed that the adjpatternrules assign Dative Case to indirect object
complements of the adjective.

\newpage
\section{ TC: Advcontrolrules}


\begin{description}
  \item[HEADCATS] CLAUSE
  \item[IMPORTCATS] --
  \item[EXPORTCATS] CLAUSE
\end{description}

This transformation class does similar things as the TC: Controlrules,
but it applies only to adverbial sentences. The subject variable of 
adverbial sentences is obligatorily interpreted as the subject of the main
clause, and this is independent of inherent properties of the main verb.
In certain expressions, the subject is interpreted
as referring to e.g. the author, cf.  
e.g.  {\em  so to speak, to use some other terminology}. The issue when which
interpretation is valid depends (at least among other things) on whether
there is a comma separating it from the main clause.
When it refers to the author, the adverbial sentence should contain an
abstract pronoun as a subject of category BIGPRO.

\section{ RC:Advvarrules}

\begin{description}
  \item[HEADCATS] VERBPPROP, ADJPPROP, ADVPPROP, PREPPPROP, ?NPPROP
  \item[IMPORTCATS] OPENPREPPPROP, OPENADVPPROP
  \item[EXPORTCATS] VERBPPROP, ADJPPROP, ADVPPROP, PREPPPROP, ?NPPROP
\end{description}

These rules introduce Prepositional phrases bearing the relation Advrel headed
by abstract (or non abstract) Prepositions plus variables. They function as
place holders for for adverbial expressions that are going to be inserted later

To be more concrete, I propose that this strategy will be followed for
{\em locative, temporal and  causal } adverbs and these only. In particular
I want to exclude Sentence adverbs (e.g. {\em waarschijnlijk, hopelijk etc. })
and for vp modifying adverbs. The latter are introduced here, or earlier (if
head modification is adopted), but not by means of abstract prepositions. 

The approach with abstract prepositions requires that there be formed 
ADVPs, i.e. adverbial phrases that are similar to ADVPPROPs in all respects,
except that they lack one argument variable.  This missing argument variable
is supplied by the PREPP dominating the abstract preposition.

The following will be a typical derivation:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] VERBPPROP[ x1 VERBP[ x2 kocht] ]  substituted for x3 in
  \item[] OPENPREPPPROP [x3 PREPP[ PREP x4 ]  $\rightarrow$
  \item[] VERBPPROP[ x1 PREPP[PREP x4] VERBP[ x2 kocht] ]
  \item[] + ADVP[head/ADV(*hier*)] substituted for x4 $\rightarrow$
  \item[] VERBPPROP[ x1 ADVP[head/ADV (*hier*) VERBP[ x2 kocht] ]     
\end{enumerate}

The abstract preposition disappears upon substitution.
There must be as many abstract prepositions as there are different type
requirements imposed by ADVPs on the variable functioning as the subject
of the abstract preposition.

Notice that the syntactic rules must take care of the fact that the syntactic 
behavior of PREPPs headed by abstract PREPs might differ from normal PREPP-s.

Special subgrammars to form ADVPs must be assumed for this treatment of
adverbs by means of abstact prepositions. They have been assumed.

Vpmodifying adverbs are inserted here in the following way.
First, they are themselves of the following form: OPENXPPROP[ subj/x1 mu2]
(X=ADV, PREP). The verbpprop is substituted for x1. The result is:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[]  VERBPPROP[ mu1, vpadvrel/XP[mu2], pred/VERBP, mu3]
\end{enumerate}

There are also {\em Agvpadv} adverbs (Agent oriented VP modifying adverbs).
These are introduced at the same point, but they are dyadic. The VERBPPROP
is substituted for their object variable, if the subject variables of the
VERBPPROP and the OPENADVPPROP bear the same index. An example is 
{\em opzettelijk }:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] VERBPPROP[ x1 VERBP[ x2 kocht] ]  substituted for x4 in
  \item[] OPENADVPPROP [x1 ADVP[ ADV x4 ]  $\rightarrow$
  \item[] VERBPPROP[ x1 ADVP[head/ADV] VERBP[ x2 kocht] ]
  \item[]  {\em hij kocht opzettelijk een boek }
\end{enumerate}

The insertion of these adverbs must apply {\em  before} the voice rules,
since it is the underlying subject ('the agent') that the adverb is 
taking as an argument. (cf. {\em De boeken werden opzettelijk door hem
verbrand }. An EMPTY can be an argument here as well (cf. {\em Er werd
opzettelijk gedanst}). If no subject is available at the right moment
(as in e.g. {\em de boot zonk}) then (at least in certain cases)
the adverb insertion does not yield a (?)grammatical result 
(cf.{\em *de jongen overleed opzettelijk}), or it does (cf. 
{\em de jongen viel opzettelijk}), if the argument can be interpreted as an
agent ({\em viel } in the latter sentence means {\em liet zich vallen })

Such adverbs {\bf can be dealt with by means of head modification },
only in a rather complex way, since the relevant  subject variables
are not present yet at the moment of insertion of the adverb.

There are also 'subject oriented' adverbs. These take an argument that must
be equal to the surface subject. These must be inserted later than the voice 
rules. An example is {\em  graag}, but this adverb gets a somewhat special
treatment anyway for other reasons. It does imply, however, that
{\em graag } {\bf cannot be handled adequately by head modification}  
(Other examples??), since their insertion must be applied after the voice rules
.

\begin{description}

                  VERBPFORMULA
  \item[IMPORTCATS] --

\subsection{ The simple Case}
\subsubsection{ Reflexives}
 If two or more variables bear the same index, then we will call one of them
the {\em antecedent} variable, the others the {\em anaphoric} variables. The status of
a variable as an antecedent variable or as an anaphoric  variable is determined
by its position in the S-tree relative to other coindexed variables and by the
grammatical relation it bears (relative to the grammatical relation other
coindexed variables bear). If certain requirements are met (e.g. the second one
must not be the subject of an infinitival complement of a verb like
{\em beloven}),then the anaphoric variables are to be spelled out as reflexive
pronouns. This spelling out of such variables cannot take place at the moment
of substitution of the antecedent variable, for the following reasons:

First, argument substitution can apply after the application of shift rules,
so that the antecedent can be at any distance from the variable to be replaced
by the reflexive pronoun:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(2)] {\em Wie} dacht jij dat Piet zei dat {\em zichzelf} gisteren had
opgehangen? 
\end{enumerate}

Second, the VAR to be replaced by the reflexive pronoun can have been shifted
itself as well, so that it is not clear whether it is to be replaced by a
reflexive anymore: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(3)] {\em Zichzelf} heeft {\em hij} nooit proberen te doden
\end{enumerate}

Third, in certain cases the reflexive must be spelled out without
its antecedent being substituted for. This is necessary in the case
of infinitives without an overt subject. In Rosetta2, the variable functioning
as the subject of an infinitival complement disappears upon insertion 
of the complement clause. There is no substitution for this subject variable
at all. A reflexive must have been spelled out before this subject variable 
disappears.

For these reasons, it seems necessary to spell out the reflexive element
at a point in the derivation before shift rules have applied, and it seems
necessary not to link it directly to the insertion of the antecedent

 We assume that there is transformation class spelling out argument
reflexives (TC:ARgreflspelling1) . It must contain transformations that spell
out a variable with index {\em i} as a reflexive pronoun (or some other
appropriate form, as indicated below) with the appropriate attribute-value
pairs if there is a VARIABLE with the same index that can serve as an
antecedent: e.g. a rel1/VAR can serve as an antecedent for a rel2/VAR if rel1
is {\em higher than} rel2, where {\em rel1 is higher than rel2} can be defined
as: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] rel1/.. asymmetrically c-commands rel2/.. OR\\
          there is mutual c-command and rel1 is higher than rel2 on the
following scale:
      \begin{enumerate}
	\item subjrel $\succ$ preadvrel $\succ$ indobjrel $\succ$ objrel 
      \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

The transformation class must contain rules for spelling reflexives
as obj-s, indobj-s, obj of prepobj-s, preadv-s, with differing antecedents:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] Hij zag zichzelf            (obj- ant: subj)
  \item[] Ik raadde hem zichzelf aan  (obj-ant: indobj)
  \item[] Hij gaf zichzelf een boek   (indobj- ant: subj)
  \item[] Hij keek naar zichzelf      (obj of prepobj- ant: subj)
  \item[] Zij stelden mij aan mezelf voor (aan-obj ant- subj)
  \item[] Ik stelde hen aan elkaar voor   (aan-obj ant- obj)
  \item[] Ik stelde hen elkaar voor       (obj ant: indobj) 
  \item[] Ik raadde Jan waarschijnlijk zichzelf aan (obj, ant: preadv)
\end{enumerate}

Example of possible formulation for one such transformation:

\begin{verbatim}

T:Reflexivisation1 \{ obj, indobj -VARs with element outside VP as antecedent\}
m1: CLAUSE\{\} [ mu4, rel2/VAR\{VARREC1\}, mu1, pred/VERBP\{VERBREC1\}
                                           [ mu2,
                                             rel1/VAR\{VARREC2\},
                                             mu3
                                           ]
             ]
m: CLAUSE\{\} [ subj/VAR\{VARREC1\}, mu1, pred/VERBP\{VERBREC1\}
                                           [ mu2,
                                             rel1/NP\{NPREC1\}
                                                 [ head/REFLPRO\{REFLPROREC1\}],
                                             mu3
                                           ]
            ]

GEN     COND    VARREC2.index  = VARREC1.index
                VARREC2.person = VARREC1.person
                VARREC2.number = VARREC1.number
                VARREC2.gender = VARREC1.gender
                VARREC2.cases * [Nominative] = []
                rel2 in [subjrel, preadvrel, postsubjrel ]
        ACTION  REFLPROREC1 := \{person: VARREC1.person,
                                number: VARREC1.number,
                                gender: VARREC1.gender
                                case  : VARREC2.case,
                                zelf  : true
                               \}
                  ..               

\end{verbatim}

The precise form of the word serving as a reflexive pronoun depends on
the direct environment the VAR is in:

\begin{description}
   \item[D]    {\em zich} if a complement of certain prepositions ({\em naast,
bij} )  and subject of the PP =/= object of PP !! 
                       (cf. {\em Hij is buiten zichzelf} vs 
                             {\em  Hij zag iets naast zich)} and 
                      if PREPP bears relation locadvrel, locargrel
   \item[E] {\em pronoun} if a complement of certain prepositions ({\em next
to, near}) 

   \item[D] {\em possadj}  in the environment NP\{\}[ -- head/NOUN ]
   \item[E] {\em possadj} in the environment NP\{\}[ -- GEN mu1 ]

   \item[D] {\em zich} in the case of long distance anaphors in certain
                      constructions (e.g. {\em hij hoorde haar op zich 
                         mopperen})
   \item[E] {\em pronoun} in this case 

   \item[D] {\em zich(zelf)} elsewhere
   \item[E] {\em  himself}   elsewhere
\end{description}

 Notice that possadj-s (e.g. {\em z'n} in {\em z'n boek} can now be derived in
certain cases in two ways, and thus have  two meanings in these cases. This is
required, since the two cases are formally distinguished in Spanish in
certain constructions, e.g. {\em Se lave las manos} (Hij wast z'n (eigen) handen)
vs. {\em Le lave las manos} ( Hij wast zijn(iemand anders') handen).

\subsubsection{Reciprocals}
Similar facts (concerning the syntax) hold for reciprocals, but it is not
clear whether they should be dealt with in the same way as reflexives are. If
so, however, then we must have some way of distinguishing reflexive spelling
from reciprocal spelling. We can do so by assuming a {\em rule} class of
reciprocal spelling (as opposed to the {\em transformation} class for reflexive
spelling). (Although we can make the reflexive spelling also a {\em rule}, as
far as I can see) 
It seems that this can be done. Syntactically, reciprocals behave as 
reflexives do (it must have an antecedent, it must entertain a certain
structural and/or functional relation with its antecedent etc.). Semantically
the reciprocal seems to take some subset from the set denoted by its antecedent
(a variable X ranging over this subset replaces the antecedent) and to have its
own function replaced by X', a variable ranging over the complement of this
subset w.r.t. the set denotated by the antecedent. Thus the meaning of {\em de
mensen zagen elkaar} (ignoring the meaning of {\em de }) can be represented as
{\em zagen (X, X')} where X $\in$ XS, X' $\in$ $\overline{XS}$, XS $\cup$
$\overline{XS}$ = [x | mens'(x)], XS $\cap$ $\overline{XS}$ = []. 
Hence we add the RC:reciprocalspelling1

The rules for reflexivespelling and reciprocalspelling must iterate (because
more than one reflexive or reciprocal can occur in a sentence) and they must
be followed by a filter to check whether all cases have been dealt with.

\subsection{ The more difficult case}

There are,however, some problems with the approach sketched, which make
it necessary to extend it somewhat:

Since reflexive elements can be inside NP-s, some reflexivization 
rules must apply at least after some substitution rules. This is
problematic for those cases where some NP has scope over the antecedent of a
reflexive contained in this NP: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(4)] Welk verhaal over zichzelf vond hij het leukst?
\end{enumerate}

 In (4) it is not clear how we can relate {\em hij} and the reflexive pronoun
{\em zichzelf}. cf. also:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(5)] Welk verhaal over {\em zichzelf} zei ik dat hij had verteld?
\end{enumerate}

 In the cases where scope and antecedent relationships run parallel, the
link can be made, if rules are written for these cases:

e.g. 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] VAR1 vertelde VAR2       $\rightarrow$ substitution for VAR2:
  \item[] VAR1 vertelde [ verhalen over VAR1 ]$\rightarrow$ reflexivisation
  \item[] VAR1 vertelde [ verhalen over zichzelf ] $\rightarrow$ subst for VAR1
  \item[] hij vertelde [ verhalen over zichzelf ] 
\end{enumerate}

 For this purpose the TC:ArgREflSpelling2, and RC: Reciprocalspelling2 are
intended.
 
Notice that an iteration of two rules is required here. Depending on 
whether this will be allowed or not, this can be kept this way, or some other
way should be taken into consideration, e.g. repeating it a finite number
(e.g. three), and then applying substitution rules without spelling out
reflexives or reciprocals.

\section{ TC:Case assignment}

\begin{description}
  \item[HEADCATS] CLAUSE, ADJPFORMULA, ADVPFORMULA, NPFORMULA, PREPPFORMULA,
                   VERBPFORMULA
  \item[IMPORTCATS] --
  \item[EXPORTCATS]  CLAUSE, ADJPFORMULA, ADVPFORMULA, NPFORMULA, PREPPFORMULA,
                   VERBPFORMULA
\end{description}

\subsection {General}
This  transformation class assigns the case of NPvar-s.
 Upon substitution of a NP for an NPVAR the case of the variable is 
assigned to this NP. If necessary, it percolates down into this NP
to case-mark the head, and e.g. determiners and adjectives. There is a
problem here w.r.t. coordinated NP's. See below

The transformation class assigns accusative Case to objects of active verbs;
to subjects of small clauses (CLOSEDADJPPROP-s etc) and
of E(xceptionally)C(ase)M(arked) structures (ACI-CONSTRUCTIONS) accusative
Case if the governing verb is active; it does not assign Dative Case to indirect
objects in Dutch and Spanish. This is assigned in the 
verbpatterntransformations. It assigns Nominative Case to subjects,
postsubjects in finite clauses and objects of ergative and passive . It does 
not assign (in Dutch) genitive Case to NP-s bearing the relation detrel, nor , in
English,  does the rule introduce the marker GEN after a NP functioning as a
detrel. This must apply in the NP-subgrammar and in the NPPROPformtion 
subgrammar. 
 In Spanish this rules adds the preposition {\em a} to animate NP-s that are
governed by an active verb 

 NP-s bear Nominative Case outside of a context: Nominative Case functions
as the Omega value. This facilitates local translation of NP-s. 

 This transformation class must apply after TC:OBjectOKrules (cf.
{\em Hij} werd gedood vs. *{\em Hem} werd gedood), and before the TC: pronounokrules,
NPoverAdvrules and the Shift rules (cf. {\em Hun} heb ik niets gegeven vs. *{\em 
Zij}
heb ik niets gegeven vs *{\em Hen} heb ik niets gegeven). In English the form of
the interrogative pronoun {\em who} as a complement of a preposition depends on two
factors: whether it is immediately preceded by a PREP (then both {\em who} and
{\em whom} are well-formed) or whether it is stranded (then only {\em who} is
well-formed) [ I'm not sure whether these facts are correct ]. If the facts
w.r.t. {\em who} are indeed as described, then I suggest to add a condition to the
rule that strands prepositions (shift rule), i.e. that the who-form must be
{\em who}, not {\em whom}. In this way this phenomenon does not affect the Case
assignment transformation class described here 

 Perhaps the transformation class described here must assign Case to
parts of idioms. These do not inherit their Case from some variable,
nor do they have it as an inherent property if the case can
change during the derivation (e.g. by passivization)

 In the languages under discussion (DES), it seems that the relevant rules
are relevant mainly for pronouns

 The percolation of Case into NP-s assumed runs into troubles in the case
of coordinated NP-s: The embedding can be indefinitely deep here, but the 
rules that account for percolation can not operate at arbitrary depths.
 The problem will arise really only if the coordination is of sufficient depth
(since it is  possible to write a finite number of rules that operate at 
fixed depths) and , in the language we are dealing with, it will show up only
if the coordination contains a pronoun (Since only pronouns have different
forms for the Cases). If the coordination consists of NP-s containing a
noun as its head, then these wil be marked with nominative in all cases if
they are at a sufficient deep level (assuming that all NP-s are 
marked with nominative by default):

\begin{verbatim}

                            NP\{acc\}

                   NP\{acc\}    NP\{acc\}
                


     NP\{nom\} NP \{nom\} NP\{nom\} NP\{nom\}NP\{nom\} NP\{nom\}
\end{verbatim}

 There are several possible solutions to this problem:
\begin{enumerate} 
  \item writing a special function {\em percolatecase} that can traverse through 
the tree at arbitrary depths
  \item writing a finite number of rules that guarantee that no problems will
arise until a certain depth d, chosen sufficiently large, has been reached.
E.g. coordinations beyond depth d = 3 are rare, and if they occur, their 
translation does not differ from the same coordination analyzed as being from
some lower depth, so that we can ignore readings with deeper embeddings. The
depth = 3 is chosen somewhat arbitrarily. Perhaps it is possible to choose 
a value such that the interpretations correspond to the performance of
native speakers in 'natural circumstances'.
  \item Making as many NP-s as there are values for their Case-attribute, and
filtering all but one of them in a Case-assignment context, and taking care
that coordination of unlike Case-marked NP-s is not allowed (* Ik en mij)
\end{enumerate}

The first method can handle all forms, but by going beyond the normal rule
formats. In Rosetta2 this method is used: a recursive function {\em assigncase}
assigns case and makes it percolate. (cf. doc 0016, p. 29)

The second method is from the point of view of a grammarian (construct 
a correct grammar) simply wrong. The performance of the grammar, however,
will be correct in almost all cases

The third method is theoreticaly the most preferable one ( a correct grammar
is construed using the normal rule formats), but it is very inefficient,
and it makes local (robust) translation more complicated

I think the first method is to be chosen

The following problems still arise w.r.t Case assignment:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item  The conjunction {\em for} must assign Case sometimes 
({\em  I prefer for him to leave}). However, it is inserted much too late to be
able to assign Case by the Caseassignment transformations. So perhaps a special
rule or subrule must account for Case assigned by {\em for}. The same holds for
{\em laat} in Dutch (as in {\em  laat hem maar komen}), and 
{\em let} in English, if they are inserted in the
mooddetermination rules or even later. But perhaps these must be inserted
before. It is also possible to insert {\em for} in an earlier phase. 

  \item A verb that assigns Case to the subject of the embedded infinitive
(e.g. believe in 'I believe him to ..') can assign its Case only after insertion
of the subordinate infinitive. But if the var has mood:wh, then it has been 
shifted already, and is no longer a subject. There are two possible 
solutions to this problem: either subj/..-s are never shifted within one
clause (at least when they are the subject of an infinitival clause). This
is the method chosen in Rosetta2. (In Rosetta2 no subject is shifted within
the clause: this is probably so because there must be isomorphy in the
derivations of finite and infinitival rules, and there are no transformations) 
Or the Case assignment rules are complicated: a verb can assign Case to a
shiftrel/VAR if it is in a complement sentence of this verb, this sentence is
infinitival, there is no conjrel/.. nor a subjrel/... in it.

Notice by the way that a verb such  as {\em believe} assigns Case not to variables
in all cases, but sometimes it assigns Case to a full NP (in e.g. {\em I
believed him to come}) 
\end{enumerate}

Care should be taken of the fact that in Dutch after Verb Raising the main
verb and the subordinate verb form a unit (of category VN), where the main
verb still has to assign case to one of its complements.


\subsection{ Case assignment in PREPFORMULA}

The preposition assigns Case to the VAR serving as an object. The Case
assigned is normally Accusative. Depending on how we categorize such words
as {\em behalve}, it is perhaps necessary to assign Nominative Case (cf.
e.g. {\em Behalve Ik gaat er niemand mee}. If the object is a Numeral, then
the so-called {\em -en} form must be accounted for here (e.g. {\em tegen
zevenen, tegen vieren} etc.), or perhaps in these cases these forms are derived
as a variant of {\em  tegen zeven uur} etc. 

\subsection{ Case assignment in ADJPPFORMULA}

 The adjective assigns Case to its complements, in particular Accusative
to obj/-s (cf. {\em  iets beu; het moe; dit zat}). Dative Case is
assigned to indobj-s (e.g. 
{\em  iemand dankbaar; hem dierbaar}) in the adjpatternrules.

\subsection{Substitution order}

I propose the following strategy for substitution as an elaboration of
the central idea that left-to-right order is significant for
scope relations.

I assume an auxiliary function RIGHTMOSTVAR(i:indextype,t:S-tree ):BOOLEAN, that
yields {\em true} if the VARcategory with index {\em i} is the rightmost one in
the S-tree {\em t } currently being evaluated. 

Substitution of  S for VAR{index:i} in tree t:
\begin{enumerate} 
  \item S is not quantificational: OK
  \item RIGHTMOSTVAR(i,t) = true: OK
  \item RIGHTMOSTVAR(i,t) = false  $\Rightarrow$ \\
	$\exists_{ VAR_{1}...VAR_{n}} \forall_{VAR_{j}}:
                         VAR_{j}$ {\em to the right of} $VAR_{i}$
	 \begin{enumerate} 
	   \item $\forall_{VAR_{j}} : VAR_{j}$ {\em is not quantificational}: OK
           \item otherwise: if there is exactly one quantificational VAR, then 
try to shift this VAR, and substitute S.
           \item if this fails, do not insert; or insert and raise the 
incorrectness bonus.
	 \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

In contrast to earlier versions of the subgrammar definitions, the negation
introduction rules do not form a separate rule class anymore. They are elements
of the rule class Nowhshiftsubstitution. The transformation class 
NegationOKrules do not exist anymore independently either. They are built into
the negation introduction rules.

The main reason for doing so, is to be able to account for such sentences as
{\em Niemand zag niemand}, that could not be handled before.

These rules introduce {\em niet} as a sentence negator. Furthermore this Rule 
Class contains a rule that makes a sentence positive. We may have it contain 
an emphatic positive formation rule as well. Such a rule would introduce the
adverb {\em w\'{e}l} (of category POS) in Dutch, and it would introduce some
element (e.g. in the position where NOT would be otherwise) in English
structures to block 
do-deletion, e.g. [He do POS  go ] $\rightarrow$ [He d\'{o}es go ] 

In these rules 
a number of changes that are 
required by the introduction of the negative element must be taken care of.

Examples:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[]  niet [ boeken ] $\rightarrow$ [geen boeken] in the sentence
 {\em Hij heeft niet boeken gelezen}, (which is a grammatical sentence, but
only under a contrastive reading) 
\end{enumerate}

Similarly, if the subject is filled, by e.g. {\em  iemand}, then {\em niet} +
{\em iemand} is turned into {\em  niemand}: 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] niet + iemand heeft dit gedaan $\rightarrow$ Niemand heeft dit gedaan
\end{enumerate}

Or if the object contains {\em  iets}, then {\em  niet} + {\em  iets}
 $\rightarrow$ {\em niets}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] niet + VAR heeft iets gedaan $\rightarrow$ VAR heeft niets gedaan
\end{enumerate}

If in more than one relation such words are present, then {\em niet} is  placed
at the leftmost one, cf.:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[]  Niet + Iemand heeft iets gedaan $\rightarrow$ Niemand heeft iets gedaan
  \item[]  Niet + Hij heeft aan iemand iets gegeven $\rightarrow$ Hij heeft aan niemand
iets                                                   gegeven 
  \item[]  ? Niet + Hij heeft iets aan iemand gegeven $\rightarrow$ 
                                      Hij heeft niets aan iemand gegeven

\end{enumerate}

Notice that for sentences such as {\em  Iemand heeft niets gezien}, 
{\em  Hij heeft
iets niet gezien}, the relevant structures at the moment of Negation insertion
contain VAR-s for {\em iemand, iets} respectively, because they are outside
the scope of negation, hence introduced later. Therefore, adaptation rules
such as the transformation class Negation OK rules are simply not applicable:

e.g.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[] subj/VAR1 obj/VAR2 gezien heeft + iets,2 $\rightarrow$ 
  \item[] subj/VAR1 obj/iets gezien heeft + niet   $\rightarrow$
  \item[] subj/VAR1 neg/niet obj/iets gezien heeft $\rightarrow$ adaptation
  \item[] subj/VAR1 obj/niets gezien heeft + iemand,1 $\rightarrow$
  \item[] subj/iemand  obj/niets gezien heeft   $\rightarrow$ (other rules, e.g. V/2 
etc.) 
  \item[]   iemand heeft niets gezien
\end{enumerate}

Sentences such as {\em  Niets heeft hij gedaan; Niemand heeft hij gezien.
Niemand heeft niets gedaan}  musy be taken care of as well.

For English and Spanish comparable rules must be written.

We must distinguish between:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item wh-shifting (questions)
  \item relative shifting
  \item topicalization
  \item ?focussing
\end{enumerate}


 These transformations position a variable or a constituent containing a
variable (e.g. prepobjrel/PP-s) to the beginning of the sentence and assign
them the relation shiftrel. Furthermore a constituent bearing the relation
shiftrel is positioned from a deeper clause to the higher clause (successive
cyclic movement), cf. Rosetta2. 

Certain other elements must be shifted as well, e.g. NP's headed by
{\em  zichzelf}, NP's headed by BDEMPRO's, in particular when there is also
a leftdislocrel/SENTENCE.

in the case of topicalisation in Dutch almost any constituent 
can be put in front. It might make sense to distinguish topicalization 
and focussing. Both place elements to the beginning of the sentence;
topicalization however does so only in the matrix clause, whereas focussing
can do so successive cyclically. The constituent moved by focussing
is heavily stressed, the constituent preposed by topicalization is not
See also TC:Subjectshift in doc. 152: the local shifting of subjects 
differs from other cases of shifting. See also TC:Case assignment.

If the dominating clause has the attribute {\em  prosubject} set to {\em  true}
then the subject cannot be shifted.

\section{ TC:Subject-verb agreement}

\begin{description}
  \item[HEADCATS] CLAUSE
  \item[IMPORTCATS] --
  \item[EXPORTCATS] CLAUSE
\end{description}

 It accounts for the agreement between finite verb and subject, if present,
or, if necessary, between verb and {\em er} in rposrel. There must be both a 
singular and a plural expletive {\em er}. In English the NP dominating
THERE can be singular and plural. Similarly, the NP dominating 
the BDEMPROs {\em dit,dat } can be singular and plural in subject position.
 
The reason is the following: The sentences (1) and (2)
will be analyzed as under (iii):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item There seem to be three men in the garden
  \item There seems to be a man in the garden
  \item Derivation:
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item  to be NP PP $\rightarrow$ 
      \item there to be NP PP , +[ seem VAR ] $\rightarrow$ 
      \item seem [there to be NP PP] $\rightarrow$ 
      \item there seem [to be NP PP] $\rightarrow$ 
      \item there seem (s) to be a man/three men in the garden.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

 Notice that there is agreement between the object of the embedded infinitival
and the verb of the main sentence. The distance between these two elements (in
terms of depth) is unlimited. Therefore it is better to split this type of
agreement in three parts: Agreement between {\em there} and the obj (or
postsubj, in Dutch), raising of {\em there} upwards (independently necessary)
and agreement between {\em there}  and the finite verb (in Dutch erpos/ER and
the finite verb). 

Notice that {\em dit, dat} must agree with the pred also in NPPROPs etc.
