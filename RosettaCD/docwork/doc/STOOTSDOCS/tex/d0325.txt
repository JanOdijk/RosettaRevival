\section{Introduction}

This document serves as a guide to determining verbpatterns. It contains some
general remarks on the concept of argument and how to determine argumenthood,
and a miscellaneous set of tests to determine verbpatterns or to distinguish
two verbpatterns.

This document does not contain a full list of the existing verbpatterns. This
list is given in a separate document (doc. R0240). 
Knowledge of this list is presupposed.

This document does not contain a flow diagram to determine a verbpattern. It is 
assumed that most verbpatterns can be determined rather easily by studying 
doc. R0240 and the examples given there. This document only supplies some
general tests concerning the notion of argument, and some specific tests
(most of them based on the criteria to determine argumenthood or on 
certain language specific pecularities) to distinguish two verbpatterns 
that are difficult to distinguish.

The document is written language-independently. For every test it will be 
indicated whether the test is valid for all languages dealt with (U=universal)
or whether it holds only for a specific subset of the languages dealt with
(D=Dutch, E=English, S=Spanish) 

This document contains a slightly extended version of appendix A of doc. R0313
 (Jan Odijk - BVERB Attributes. Guide to dictionary filling), translated into
English, and a copy of appendix B of this document. This has been done
because these parts naturally belong in this document, and to have an English
version of appendix A of doc. R0313.


The section on the treatment of {\em  met} contains suggestions on how they 
should be dealt with in the future, and how the current treatment
should be changed.

\section{Arguments and Argumenthood}
\label{arghood}

A first requirement to determine the verbpattern of a certain verb is the
determination of the arguments of the verb. It is not possible to give a
operational definition of argument. However, the following tests can be used
to determine whether some phrase XP is an argument of some verb V:

\begin{description}
  \item[Obligatoriness test(U)]
If XP must appear obligatorily in combination 
with V, then it is an argument of V. One should
also show that the obligatoriness is caused by V for this test to be valid. 
It is nevertheless not perfect, since in certain cases certain elements 
must appear obligatorily though they are not arguments (e.g. {\em  het} in
{\em  het regent}). 

Related to the obligatoriness test is the {\em Minimal Sentence Test}.
The phrases that occur in a {\em minimal sentence} headed by V are
arguments of V. A {\em  minimal sentence} is a sentence containing as
few elements as possible, e.g. taking pronouns such as  {\em someone, 
something, somewhere, etc.} or {\em  he, she, it, etc. } as phrases combined 
with V. 
Note that this test does not necessarily yield {\em all} arguments of 
V, e.g. optional arguments are not found in this way.

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[houden]  {\em *houdt}, {\em *Hij houdt}, {\em *houdt het}, 
{\em Hij houdt het}: {\em  hij} and {\em  het} are arguments of the verb 
{\em  houden}    
  \item[wonen] {\em  *woont}, {\em  *Hij woont}, {\em *woont ergens}, 
{\em  Hij woont ergens}:
{\em  hij} and {\em  ergens} are arguments of the verb {\em  wonen}
  \item[beschuldigen] {\em  *beschuldigde}, {\em  *hij beschuldigde },
{\em  *beschuldigde haar}, {\em  hij beschuldigde haar}, 
{\em  *beschuldigde van moord}, {\em  *beschuldigde haar van moord},
{\em  *hij beschuldigde van moord}, {\em  hij beschuldigde haar van moord}:\\
{\em  hij} and {\em  haar} are arguments of {\em  beschuldigen}, the status
of {\em  van moord} as argument of the verb {\em  beschuldigen} is unclear
(on the basis of this test).
\end{description}

  \item[Maximal Projection Test(U)] Arguments must be maximal projections, i.e.
NPs, SENTENCES, PREPPs, ADVPs, ADJPs etc. can be arguments, but prepositions,
single adverbs, single nouns, particles, conjunctions  etc. cannot be arguments.

Example:
\begin{description}
  \item[Aan] {\em  Aan} in {\em  hij kondigde hem aan} is not an argument.
 \end{description}


  \item[Reference Test(U)] Arguments must have reference. One can check
whether an NP has reference by replacing it by {\em  wie} or {\em  wat} and
check whether a well-formed sentence results. If it does, then the NP
has reference, if it does not, the NP has no reference. Similarly, adverb
phrases and PREPPs can be replaced by {\em  waar, wanneer, waarheen}.
This excludes {\em  het}
in {\em  het regent} (cf. {\em  *wat regent (er)}), 
{\em  er} in such sentences as {\em  er werd gedanst} (cf.  {\em  *wat werd 
gedanst})\footnote{The sentence {\em  er werd gedanst} is ambiguous: {\em  er} 
is
either a meaningless element, or it has a locative meaning. In its locative 
meaning, {\em  er} can be replaced by {\em  waar}.}
etc. This test does not apply in the case of idioms.
It also excludes the PREPP {\em  van chocola} as in {\em  hij houdt van chocola}
(cf. {\em  *Waar houdt hij?}), but it does not excludes {\em  in Amsterdam} as
in {\em  hij woont in Amsterdam} (cf. {\em Waar woont hij}).
On the status of {\em  wheather-it}, see also doc R0308.


  \item[Selection Restriction Test(U)] A verb W can impose selectional 
restrictions only on arguments of W. This implies: if selectional 
restrictions are imposed by V on XP, then XP is an 
argument of V. If no selectional restrictions are imposed at all, then XP is 
not an argument of V.

Ways to determine whether selectional restrictions are imposed onto some 
phrase XP by some verb V:

\begin{description}
  \item[Wheather-it] Check whether XP can be replaced by {\em  weather-it}. If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then in most cases no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP
(if so, then all the other tests must yield the same result). In some cases, 
however, this latter conclusion cannot be drawn (example: {\em  ophouden})

In general it is impossible to prove that some occurrence of {\em  het} is 
{\em  wheather-it}, and not some other type of {\em  het}, unless the 
occuring {\em  het} serves a double function: it is the understood 
subject of some {\em  wheather}-verb and the subject (or object) of V. 
This is possible only if V is a verb taking an infinitival sentential 
complement. 

Example:

\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert te zwemmen} vs. 
                   {\em  *het probeert te regenen}:  {\em  Jan} is an argument 
of {\em  proberen}
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em Jan blijkt te zwemmen } vs.
                  {\em  Het blijkt te regenen}: {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  blijken}. In this example it will turn out to be the case that {\em  Jan} 
is indeed {\em not} an argument of {\em  proberen} (see the other tests).
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan is opgehouden te zwemmen} vs.
           {\em  Het is opgehouden te regenen}:  {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  ophouden}. In this example, however, it will turn out to be the 
case that {\em  Jan} 
is in fact an argument of {\em  ophouden} (see the other tests).
\end{description}

  \item[Expletives] Check whether XP can be replaced by {Expletives} 
(e.g. {\em  er} (as in Dutch impersonal passives), {\em  there} in English)
If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP
(if so, then all the other tests must yield the same result).

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen,XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Er probeert gedanst te worden}: {\em  Jan} is an 
argument of {\em  proberen}.
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan blijkt te dansen} vs. 
{\em  Er blijkt gedanst te worden}: {\em  Jan} is not an argument of {\em  
blijken} 
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan hield op te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Er hield op gedanst te worden}: {\em  Jan} is an 
argument of {\em  ophouden}.

  \item[V=see, XP=John] {\em John saw a man} vs. {\em  * There saw a man}: 
{\em  John} 
is an argument of {\em  see}.
\end{description}


  \item[Extraposition-it]  Check whether XP can be replaced by 
{\em  Extraposition-it} (i.e. {\em  het} or {\em  it} functioning as an
anticipatory {\em  subject, object etc.} for a sentential argument). 

If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then in most cases no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP
(if so, then all the other tests must yield the same result).

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen,XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert duidelijk te zijn} vs.
                   {\em  *het probeert duidelijk te zijn dat dit fout is}: 
{\em  Jan} is an argument of {\em  proberen}.
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan blijkt duidelijk te zijn} vs. 
{\em  Het blijkt duidelijk te zijn dat dit fout is}: {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  blijken}.
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan hield op duidelijk te zijn} vs.
                   {\em  ?Het is opgehouden duidelijk te zijn dat dit fout is}:
{\em  Jan} is an argument of {\em  ophouden}

\end{description}


  \item[Idiom Chunks]   Check whether XP can be replaced by 
an {\em  Idiom Chunk} (i.e. some part of an idiomatic phrase).

If 
this is not possible, V imposes selectional restrictions upon XP; if it is 
possible then no selectional restrictions are imposed by V onto XP.

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=proberen,XP=Jan] {\em  Jan probeert te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Het bijltje probeert er bij neer gegooid te zijn}: 
{\em  Jan} is an argument of {\em  proberen}.
  \item[V=blijken, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan blijkt te dansen} vs. 
{\em  Het bijltje blijkt er bij neer gegooid te zijn}: {\em  Jan} is probably 
not an argument of {\em  blijken}.
  \item[V=ophouden, XP=Jan] {\em  Jan hield op te dansen} vs.
                   {\em  *Het bijltje is opgehouden er bij 
neergegooid te zijn}: {\em  Jan } is an argument of {\em  ophouden}

\end{description}


  \item[Other Type(U)] replace XP by some other phrase with the same syntax but
with a different type, e.g. replace a {\em human} XP by a {\em nonhuman} XP',
etc. (Examples of types; {\em  animate -nonanimate}; 
{\em  concrete-abstract}; {\em  masculine-feminine}; 
{\em  food-nonfood}; {\em  institution-noninstitution} etc. 

If there are cases where this yields semantically ill-formed sentences
(or only metaphorical readings), then
XP is an argument, and if V is the only selection restriction imposing verb
present, then XP is an argument of V.
 
Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=verven,XP=de deur] {\em  Hij verft de deur }, cf. {\em  *hij 
verft de muziek }: {\em  de muziek} is an argument of {\em  verven} 
  \item[V=beloven,XP=de jongen] {\em  hij beloofde de jongen het boek } vs.
{\em  *hij beloofde de eerlijkheid het boek }: {\em  de jongen} is an
argument of {\em  beloven}.
  \item[V=surprise,XP=the boy] {\em  her honesty surprised the boy} vs.
{\em  *her honesty surprised his shame}: {\em  the boy} is an argument of
{\em  surprise}.
\end{description}


\end{description}
 

\item[Category Restriction Test(U)] If V restricts the category of XP in any way,
then XP is an argument of V. It is important to note that one must really show 
that the categorial restriction is imposed by V, and not by other factors.

Example:
\begin{description}
  \item[V=schijnen, XP=ziek te zijn] {\em  Hij schijnt ziek te zijn} vs.
{\em  *Hij schijnt ziek}. The phrase {\em  ziek te zijn} is an argument of
{\em  schijnen} since {\em  schijnen} imposes the restriction that the phrase
functioning as its argument can be a SENTENCE, but not a AdjP.
\end{description}


%\item[Oblique Case Test(U)] If XP bears an oblique (=nonnominative 
%nonaccusative) case assigned by V, then XP is an argument of V. 

%This test is not very useful in the languages dealt with in Rosetta3. But
%all dative NPs are arguments of the verb (adjective) that assigns this dative 
%case.

\item[XP Integrity Test(U)] V cannot determine the form of the head X or of
complements of X if X is a member of the open categories (=N,Adj,Adv,Prep,V)

 
Take care: 
many form aspects that appear to be internal to XP are in fact 
(also) features on XP: these c\'{a}n be restricted by V! 
(example: case )
)

In many cases XP-internal form aspects can appear to be imposed by V, though in
fact they are the (indirect) result of semantic restrictions imposed by the 
semantics of V onto the semantics of XP. 


This test does not apply to idioms and semi-idioms.


\begin{description}
  \item[V=rekenen, XP=op de overwinning] {\em  Hij rekende op de overwinning} 
The phrase {\em  op de overwinning} is not (as a whole)  an argument of {\em  
rekenen} since the presence of the preposition {\em  op} inside this PREPP
is determined by 
the verb  {\em  rekenen}. In fact, the NP {\em  de overwinning} is here an 
argument (though this cannot be concluded on the basis of this test). 
\end{description}

\end{description}

\section{Arguments with more than one function}

In many sentences one phrase serves at the same time as an argument of more
than one verb or adjective, etc. Some examples are given below:

\begin{trivlist}
\item[]
\begin{tabular}{|p{.5\textwidth}|p{.2\textwidth}|p{.3\textwidth}|}
\hline
sentence & phrase & argument of\\
\hline
hij belooft mij te komen & hij & belooft, komen\\
hij dwingt hem te komen & hem & dwingt, komen\\
dat ruikt lekker & dat & ruikt, lekker\\
ik verf de deur groen & de deur & verf, groen\\
De os weegt drie kilo & de os & weegt, drie kilo\\
ik bracht het kind naar huis & het kind & bracht, naar huis\\
zij is naar huis gefietst & zij & gefietst, naar huis\\
hij zwemt graag & hij & zwemt, graag\\
Er werd door hem enthousiast gedanst & hem & gedanst, enthousiast\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{trivlist}

In all  such cases it is assumed that an additional variable bound by the
relevant phrase is present to serve the other function. So in D-structures
no element is an argument of two functions. This is reflected (as far as
arguments are concerned)
in the pattern names, which for the verbs mentioned here.
contain the specification
{\em  OPEN}.

\section{Ergativity}

This subsection contains criteria to distinguish ergative from nonergative 
verbs. It is an slight extension of the criteria given in doc. R0313
(Jan Odijk - BVERB Attributes. Guide to dictionary filling), appendix
A. It has been repeated here, for convenience, and to have an English version 
of this appendix.

Each criterion has been marked as being {\em  weak} or {\em strong}, indicating
the importance  of the criterion to determine ergativity. Violations of
weak criteria do not make it impossible to assign a given verb to a certain 
class. Violations of strong criteria, however, do make this impossible, unless
independent reasons can be found and motivated to account for the deviancy.

Ergative verbs are verbs with thetavp=vp010 or thetavp=vp012, with an NP
argument.
The criteria hold for verbs with these thetavps and a sentential 
or closedxpprop argument
(e.g. raising verbs) as well.

\begin{description}
  \item[auxiliary(weak)(D)] If a verb is conjugated with {\em  zijn}, then it is
ergative. If it is conjugated with {\em  hebben}, it is not ergative.
  \item[Passivization(strong)(U)] If a verb can be passivized, then it is not
ergative.
  \item[Dyadic verbs(strong)(D)] If the indirect object can precede the other
argument, then it the verb is ergative.
Example
 \begin{description}
  \item[] ..omdat {\em  de man} {\bf het boek} beviel 
\end{description}

The indirect object argument {\em de man} can precede
the other argument {\bf het boek} in this example, hence the verb 
{\em  bevallen} is ergatief. 

\item[Thematic role(weak)(U)] 
The argument of ergative verbs bears the thematic role
{\em Theme} or {\em Patiens}. This implies that it undergoes `movement' 
(in a very abstract and vague sense).

\item[Thematic role(strong, but vague)(U)] 
If an argument of a verb bears the thematic role {\em Agent}, then the verb is
not ergative. 

\item[Participle used attributively (strong)(U)] 

If the passive participle of a one-place verb can occur as an attributive 
modifier of some noun N, N being a a noun that can occur normally as the
subject of the verb, then the verb is ergative. If this is not possible, then
the verb is not ergative. 

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em De jongen valt}, {\em De gevallen jongen},   hence {\em vallen}  
              is ergative
  \item[] {\em  De boek zijn aangekomen}, {\em  De aangekomen boeken}, hence
              {\em aankomen} is ergative
  \item[] {\em  De jongens dansen}, {\em *De gedanste jongens}, hence {\em  
dansen} is not ergative
   \item[] {\em  De mannen werken}, {\em  *de gewerkte mannen}, hence
{\em  werken} is not ergative.
\end{description}

A similar test can be used for dyadic ergative verbs. In Dutch the contexts are 
then: {\em  de indobj V$_{part}$ N}. 

Examples:

\begin{description}
  \item[] {\em Het boek valt mij op}, {\em  het mij opgevallen boek}, hence
{\em opvallen} is ergative.
  \item[] {\em  De resultaten vallen mij mee}, {\em  de mij meegevallen 
resultaten}, hence {\em  meevallen} is ergative
  \item[] {\em De man koopt het boek}, {\em  * De het boek gekocht(e) man}, 
hence
{\em  kopen} is not ergative.
       
\end{description}


\item[{\em -er/-aar}-formation(strong)(D)] 
If a word ending in {\em  -er/-aar} can be derived from V 
with the meaning {\em  someone who Vs (by profession)}, then V is not an 
ergative verb.  If it is not possible, then the verb probably is ergative.

Examples
\begin{description}
  \item[vallen] {\em *valler}, hence {\em  vallen} probably is ergative. 
  \item[aankomen] {\em  *aankomer}, hence {\em  aankomen}  probably is ergative. 
  \item[dansen] {\em  danser}, hence {\em  dansen} is not ergative
  \item[werken] {\em  werker}, hence {\em  werken} is not ergative
\end{description}

\item[Reflexives(weak)(D,?S,?E)] Reflexives (and reciprocals)
cannot appear very easily as an 
argument of an ergative verb if they are bound by the subject, cf.
{\em  ?Hij beviel zichzelf, ?Hij viel zichzelf op, ?Hij viel zichzelf mee,
*Hij schoot zichzelf te binnen, *Hij leek zichzelf ziek, *zij lijken elkaar 
ziek}. With nonergative verbs there are no problems whatsoever:
{\em  hij zag zichzelf, hij hoorde zichzelf, de katten roken elkaar} etc.
For some verbs reflexives are difficult for semantic reasons, e.g. {\em  ?hij 
ontmoette zichzelf}, but notice that with the ergative examples given above
there is no semantic reason whatsoever for their strangeness.

\end{description}


\section{The treatment of {\em  het, er} and extraposed sentences}

Sentential arguments of verbs can occur in principle as `subject arguments',
as `complement' arguments or as `prepositional object arguments'. They
are usually realized syntactically in a special way. This section describes
the treatment of these phenomena in the Rosetta3 Grammar of Dutch.

\subsection{Sentential subject arguments}

Sentential subject arguments cannot be realized as subjects in the 
grammar of Dutch. A sentential argument that must function as a subject
can be realized in one of the following two ways:
\begin{description}
\item[leftdislocrel] (main clauses only) The sentence is put in a special 
relation called {\em leftdislocrel} (in the beginning of the sentence)
and the subject position 
is either empty or occupied by the DEMPRO {\em  dat}:
\begin{description}
  \item leftdislocrel/[Dat hij vervelend is](, subjrel/dat) verbaast mij  
  \item leftdislocrel/[dat hij ziek is]( subjrel/dat) impliceert dat hij niet 
aanwezig kan zijn.
\end{description}
Such sentences are impossible or very marginal in subordinate clauses:

\begin{description}
  \item[] *hij zei dat dat hij vervelend is(, dat) mij verbaast
  \item[] ?hij zegt dat dat hij ziek is(, dat) impliceert dat hij niet aanwezig kan 
zijn.
\end{description}
 
 
\item[extraposrel] The sentence is extraposed (to the end of the sentence)
and bears the relation {\em  extraposrel}.
In this case the subject position is occupied by the pronoun {\em  het}:

\begin{description}
  \item[] subjrel/Het verbaast mij extraposrel/dat hij vervelend is  
\end{description}

\end{description}

Notice that sentential subject arguments can occur only with verbs that have
thetavp set to  vp100, vp120, or vp123.
Typical examples: {\em  verbazen, ergeren, interesseren, boeien}

The same type of behavior is found with sentential subject arguments of
adjectives.

\subsection{Sentential complement arguments}

Sentential complement arguments are  always extraposed to the position 
bearing the relation {\em  extraposrel} (unless they are subject to Verb-
raising). Depending on the verbpattern the pronoun {\em het} occurs as an
object. If the verbpattern of the verb contains the specification `HET'
(as in syn{\em HET}THATSENT, syn{\em HET}OPENOMTESENT) {\em  het} occurs,
if the verbpattern does not contain such a specification no {\em  het}
occurs. {\bf Notice that this object {\em  het} is realized as a subject
of verbs with thetavp=vp010 or vp012!}. If no {\em het} occurs for
verbs with thetavp =vp010, vp012, the `subject position' will contain 
{\em  er} in the surface

Some examples:

\begin{description}
  \item[vp120, het] Hij betreurt objrel/{\em  het}  extraposrel/[dat hij ziek is] 
  \item[vp120, no het] Hij vindt extraposrel/[dat hij gelijk heeft]
  \item[vp012, het] subjrel/{\em Het } lijkt mij extraposrel/[dat hij gelijk 
heeft]
  \item[vp010, no het] {\em Er} is gebleken extraposrel/[dat hij ziek was]
  \item[vp010, het] {\em  Het} schijnt dat hij gelijk heeft
\end{description}


\subsection{Sentential Prepositional Object Arguments}

Sentential prepositional arguments are always extraposed. They leave {\em  het}
as an object of the preposition behind. This {\em het} will be turned into
{\em  er} by the normal rules. Examples:

\begin{description}
  \item[] Hij rekent {\em  er}op extraposrel/[dat hij mag komen]
  \item[] Hij gaat {\em  er}van uit extraposrel/[dat dit mogelijk is]
\end{description}

\section{The treatment of {\em  met}}

The treatment of prepositional phrases heade by {\em  met} 
often caused problems for the lexico's to fill the dictionary. Therefore this
separate, somewhat speculative section is added here to clarify this area
somewhat. 

The following verbs have been assigned a pattern that indicates that
they take a prepositional object
headed by the preposition {\em  met} 
in the Dutch dictionary, as filled by the lexicos. 
They should not all be treated as prepositional
objects, in my opinion, though currently there is no possibility to deal 
with all of 
them in another way. I tentatively classified them into four groups:


\begin{description}
  \item[instrumental, optional] {\em  antwoorden met, iets betalen met, zwaaien met}
  \item[instrumental, subcategorized] {\em  ?beginnen met, ?eindigen met, besluiten met,
                    openen met}
  \item[subject oriented comitative] {\em   met iemand afwisselen, iets met iemand bespreken,  
met iemand corresponderen , met iemand onderhandelen, iets met iemand
overeenkomen, overeenkomen met iets, met iemand twisten, met iemand spreken} 
  \item[object oriented comitative] {\em  iemand verbinden met iemand, iets combineren met, 
iets met iets verenigen, iets met iets vergelijken}
  \item[real prepobj] {\em  zich amuseren met, zich bemoeien met, zich 
bezighouden met, iemand feliciteren met, iemand helpen met, instemmen met,
ophouden met, zich vleien met, beginnen met, eindigen met} and perhaps
{\em ?zich met iemand meten, zich met iemand onderhouden, ?zich met 
iemand verstaan}.
\end{description}


The groups can be characterized as follows:

\begin{description}
  \item[instrumental, optional] These cases have been 
assigned the wrong verbpattern.
The cases involved are instrumental adjuncts that are optional. The 
prepositional phrase can be headed by other prepositions indicating manner,
e.g. {\em  op de volgende manier} or by adverbial phrases such as {\em  zo}:
{\em  Hij antwoordde (met de volgende woorden/zo/op de volgende manier)}.
The object of the preposition must be an object that can be used as an 
instrument.
  \item[instrumental, subcategorized] These cases involve instrumental adjuncts
that the verb is subcategorized for. They must occur in the structure. 
(cf. {\em  *Hij besloot}, {\em  Hij besloot met de volgende woorden},
{\em  *Hij opende}, {\em  Hij opende met de volgende woorden}. The 
prepositional phrase can be replaced by other prepositional phrases
(cf. {\em Hij besloot op de volgende manier}) and by adverbial phrases
({\em  Hij opende zo}). I doubt about the status of verbs such as
{\em  beginnen met, eindigen met}. Is the 
{\em met}-phrase obligatorily present? Must a distinction between
instrumental adverb and prepositional object really be made? 
Notice that with these verbs 
the intended use here is as illustrated in: {\em  beginnen met deze woorden}, 
not as in: {\em 
beginnen met het werk}.
At the moment it is not possible yet to specify that an instrumental adverb
or prepositional phrase must be present. 
  \item[subject oriented comitative] These are cases where the {\em  met}-
phrase can be absent. The type of the object of the preposition must
be compatible with the type of the subject. In certain cases
a singular (non group non mass) 
subject can occur only if this prepositional phrase is present.
(cf. {\em ?Jan kwam dit overeen, Jan kwam dit overeen met 
Piet, Jan en Piet kwamen dit overeen}).

A criterion to distinguish this class is that it must be possible to
paraphrase a sentence of the form
A V X met B  Y (X,Y arbitrary strings; A,B NPs)  as

A en B V X Z (with the number of the verb adapted appropriately).

These cases should be dealt with as `subject-oriented comitative phrases',
not as prepositional objects.

Clearly, this is possible only if a local translation of such adjuncts is 
possible.

At the moment it is not possible yet to specify such adjuncts.


  \item[object oriented comitative] 
The same as above, but the relation is now between the {\em  met}-phrase
and
the direct object. A sentence of the form 

W V X A Y met B Z (W,X,Y,Z  arbitrary strings; B direct object NP)
should be paraphrasable as W V X A en B Z    

At the moment it is not possible yet to specify such adjuncts.


  \item[real prepobj] These are real cases of prepositional objects
headed by {\em  met}.

The status of {\em  zich met iemand meten, zich met iemand onderhouden, 
zich met iemand verstaan} is not clear to me.
\end{description}



\section{Other cases}

\begin{description} 
\item[DONP\_OPENTOSENT vs. CLOSEDTOSENT]
The following tests can be used to distinguish these two patterns

\begin{description}
  \item[Passive Test(U)] (Chomsky 1965:22) Given a structure of the form 
NP1 V1 NP2 {\em to} V2 NP3. Determine whether the verbpattern of V1 is
synDONP\_OPENTOSENT or synCLOSEDTOSENT.
Replace
V2 by a verb that can be passivized (if V2 can be passivized, it can be 
replaced by itself). Form structures of the form:
\begin{description}
  \item[] NP1 V1 NP3 to be V2-{\em ed} by NP2 
\end{description}

If this structure is well-formed and has 
the same truth-values  as the original
sentence (for any NP2, NP3), then V1 has pattern synCLOSEDTOSENT; otherwise
it has pattern synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.


Example:
\begin{description}
  \item[expect] I expected the doctor to examine the boy \to\\
                 I expected the boy to be examined by the doctor (OK)\\
                 I expected the boy to buy the  ball \to
                 I expected the ball to be bought by the boy (OK):
{\em Expect} has verbpattern {\em synCLOSEDTOSENT}

  \item[persuade] I persuaded the doctor to examine the boy \to
                  I persuaded the boy to be examined by the doctor (OK, 
different meaning)\\
                 I persuaded the boy to buy the  ball \to
                 *I persuaded the ball to be bought by the boy:\\
{\em persuade} has verbpattern {\em synDONP\_OPENTOSENT}
\end{description}

 
  \item[Selection Restriction Test(U)] Given a structure of the form 
NP1 V1 NP2 {\em to} V2 NP3. Check whether V1 imposes selectional restrictions
upon NP2 by using the Selection Restriction Test from section~\ref{arghood}. If it 
does, then NP2 is an argument of V1, hence V1 has pattern synDONP\_OPENTOSENT;
if it does not, then NP2 is not an argument of V1, hence V1 has pattern
synDONPCLOSEDTOSENT.

Examples. 
Consider the sentences {\em  I believe the doctor to examine the boy} and
{\em  I persuaded the doctor to examine the boy} (V1=believe, persuade;
NP2 = the doctor)
\begin{description}
  \item[Wheather-It]  {\em  I believe it to rain}: OK, hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
                      {\em  I persuaded it to rain}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT
  \item[Expletives] {\em  I believed there to be a problem}:  OK, hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
                    {\em  I persuaded there to be a problem}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT
  \item[Extraposition-it] {\em  I believe it to be clear that this is correct}: 
OK, hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
{\em  I persuaded it to be clear that this is correct}: *, 
hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT
  \item[Idiom Chunks] {\em  I believed the cat to have Mary's tongue}: OK,
hence synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
{\em  I persuaded the cat to have Mary's tongue}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.
  \item[Other type] {\em  I believed the music to get louder}: OK, hence
synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
{\em  I persuaded the music to get louder}: *, hence synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.
\end{description}

\item[Replacement test(U)] Given a structure of the form 
NP1 V1 NP2 {\em to} V2 NP3. Check whether the part NP2 {\em  to} V2 NP3
can be replaced by finite sentence of the form 
{\em that} NP2 V2-{\em  s} NP3 (with preservation of grammaticality, and meaning). 
Check furthermore, if the part {\em  to} V2 NP3 can be replaced by 
a finite sentences of the form {\em  that he/she/it (should)} V2 NP3 or
{\em P the fact} {\em  that he/she/it (should)} V2 NP3, P some (simple) 
preposition
(with 
preservation of grammaticality and meaning). This can yield the following 
results:
\begin{description}
\item[] \mbox{}\\
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|l|}
\hline
Replacement 1 & Replacement 2 & Comments\\
\hline
possible      & impossible    & synCLOSEDTOSENT\\
impossible    & possible      & synDONP\_OPENTOSENT\\
impossible    & impossible    & nothing can be said\\
possible      & possible      & cannot occur. \\
\hline
\end{tabular} 
\end{description}

If the last situation does occur, it probably implies that V1 is in fact 
ambiguous.

Examples.
\begin{description}
  \item[V1=believe] {\em  I believe John to be ill},
{\em  I believe that John is ill}, {\em  *I believe John (of the fact) that he is ill} : synCLOSEDTOSENT
  \item[V1=persuade] {\em  I persuade John to come}, {\em *I persuade (of the 
fact) that John comes}, {\em  I persuade John (of the fact) that he should 
come }: synDONP\_OPENTOSENT.
\end{description}

For other languages, the replacement sentences must of course 
be adapted to the specific 
language.
\end{description}

\item[DONP\_OPENADJPPROP vs. CLOSEDADJPPROP]
The follwing tests can be used:

\begin{description}
  \item[Meaning(U)] In sentences of the form {\em  NP1 V NP2 AP} the 
interpretation of the AP is {\em resultative}  if V has pattern DONP\_
OPENADJPPROP, the interpretation is stative if V has pattern
CLOSEDADJPPROP. The resultative interpretation 
can be paraphrased as {\em  NP1 V NP2 so that
NP2 became AP}. The stative interpretation can be paraphrased as
{\em  NP1 V that NP2 is AP}. Cf. {\em He painted the door green} \to
{\em  He painted the door, so that it(the door) became green} vs.
{\em  He considers her smart} \to {\em  he considers that she is smart}.
For both examples the other paraphrase is completely out:
{\em  *He painted that the door was green} and {\em  *He considers her so that
she is smart}.

\item[Replacement by THAT-sent(U)] If NP2 AP (in NP1 V NP2 AP)
can be replaced by a finite sentence
introduced by  {\em  that}({\em  dat, que}), then V has pattern 
synCLOSEDADJPPROP. 
\end{description}

\item[Locative/Directional Argument vs. Prepositional Object] The following 
tests can be used:
\begin{description}
  \item[Replacement by ADVP(U)] A locative or directional PREPP argument can be
replaced by locative adverbial phrases such as {\em  thuis}, {\em  hier},
{\em  ergens}, {\em  here}, {\em  zuidwaarts}, {\em  home}, etc. Prepositional
objects cannot. 
  \item[Replacement by other Ps(U)] In a locative or directional PREPP the head P
can be replaced freely by all other locative or directional PREPs. In a 
prepositional object these replacement possibilities are absent or severely
restricted.
\end{description}

\item[Locative/Directional Argument vs. Locative Adverbial] The following tests
can be used:
\begin{description}
  \item[Postverbal PREPPs(D)] Locative (and directional)
arguments cannot appear to the right of
the verb. Locative adverbials, however, can.
Examples:
The sentence {\em  Hij is in de sloot gevallen} is ambiguous. In its most 
natural reading it means that he fell from the bank of some ditch into this 
ditch. Under a second reading it means that he fell while being in a ditch.
Under the first reading the PREPP {\em  in de sloot} is a directional argument 
of the verb {\em  vallen}, in the second example it is a locative adverbial.
When the PREPP is put to the right of the verb {\em  gevallen}, the first 
reading disappears: {\em  hij is gevallen in de sloot} can only mean that he
fell while being in the ditch. Cf. also {\em  *Hij heeft lange tijd 
gewoond in Amsterdam,
*Hij zou verblijven in Amsterdam, *Hij was gegaan naar Amsterdam}

  \item[Neutral Sentential Accent(D)] The neutral sentential accent falls on the
PREPP/ADVP if this PREPP/ADVP is a locative or directional argument. 
It falls on the verb if  the PREPP is a locative adverbial. The example used 
above shows this directly: {\em  hij is in de SLOOT gevallen} (directional 
argument) vs. {\em  hij is in de sloot GEVALLEN} (locative adverbial).

 \item[Obligatoriness(U)] If some locative or directional PREPP is 
obligatorily present if some verb V is present, then it is an argument of V.
Cf. {\em  Hij verbleef lange tijd *(in Amsterdam), hij is *(naar huis) gefietst}

  \item[Directional PPs and ADvPs(U)] are always arguments.
\end{description}

\item[synPREPNP vs. synPREPOPENNPROP(D)]
(similarly: synDONP\_PREPNP vs. synDONP\_PREPOPENNPPROP). These can be 
distinguished in the following way:
\begin{description}
  \item[Absence of article(D)] If the NP can consist of such words as 
{\em  directeur}, {\em boekhouder}, {\em professor} etc. 
(which normally require the presence of an article in singular)
without there being an article, and such bare Noun NPs can occur as
predicative NPs in such sentences as {\em  hij/zij/het is NP} as well,
then the pattern is synPREPOPENNPPROP
(synDONP\_PREPOPENNPPROP). 
Otherwise it is  synPREPNP (synDONP\_PREPNP).

Examples:
\begin{description}
  \item[benoemen] {\em Ik benoem hem tot directeur}, {\em  Hij is directeur}: 
OK, hence 
synDONP\_PREPOPENNPROP
  \item[kijken] {\em  *Ik kijk naar directeur}, hence synPREPNP.
\end{description}


\end{description}
\item[synMEASUREPHRASE vs. synNP] The following tests can be used:
\begin{description}
  \item[Measure test] If NP2 in NP1 V (NP3) NP2 can be replaced by some NP that
does not express a measure, then the pattern of the verb is not 
syn(IONP\_)MEASUREphrase,
but syn(IONP\_DO)NP. Cf. {\em  Hij betaalde (hem) drie gulden} and 
{\em  Hij betaalde (hem) het geld}, hence   pattern= synIONP\_DONP.
{\em  De biefstuk kost drie gulden} and {\em  *De biefstuk kost het geld},
hence pattern = synMEASUREPHRASE.
Cf. also the verb {\em  wegen}, which is ambiguous between a 
pattern synMEASUREPHRASE (as in {\em De os weegt drie kilo}) and the pattern
synDONP (cf. {\em  De boer weegt de os}).
  \item[Passivization] If a structure of the form NP1 V NP2 can be passivized,
then the pattern of the verb is not synMEASUREPHRASE.
cf. {\em  Er werd drie gulden betaald} vs. {\em  *Er werd drie gulden gekost}.
Cf. also {\em  Er werd drie kilo gewogen} (* in the measurephrase 
interpretation) vs. {\em  De os werd gewogen}.
  \item[Thematic role] The thematic role of the subject of a verb with pattern
= synMEASUREPHRASE usually is  not {\em Agent}. If 
it is, then the verb probably does not have pattern 
synMEASUREPHRASE. 
  \item[Progressive Test] Verbs with pattern= synMEASUREPHRASE are usually 
stative and cannot appear in the {\em  progressive} (E) or in the 
{\em  aan het}-construction (D), cf. {\em  *he is costing three pounds}, 
{\em  *hij is drie gulden aan het kosten}, {\em  hij is drie kilo aan het wegen}
(unambiguous), vs. {\em  Hij is drie gulden aan het betalen}, {\em  Hij is drie 
ossen aan het wegen}.
\end{description}


\end{description}
