\section{Introduction}

This document gives a list of all verbpatterns of Dutch, and it supplies
some additional information on them. 

The verbpatterns treated are ordered alphabetically.

For each verbpattern the following additional information is supplied:

\begin{description}
  \ruleitem The name of the M-rule treating this verbpattern 
  \csritem  An indication of how this 
verbpattern is realized in structures that are the output of M-generator.
(Hence an indication of what kind of structures the surface parser must be
able to deal with). {\em  Canonical} is supposed to mean here that no
special rules such as {\em  shift rules}, rule to account for the somewhat
special distribution of {\em  het} etc. have applied. In certain cases
the specification {\em  \VR} has been given, meaning that the canonical
surface representations must be compatible with the form of 
Verb Raising structures.
  \remarksitem    Any remarks that seem appropriate. 
\end{description}

Furthermore for each {\em  thetavp} compatible with the verbpattern
being treated {\bf example verbs} and {\bf example sentences} have been
supplied in special subsections.

An index of all example verbs is added.

The experiences of the dictionary fillers led to new ideas concerning the 
proper treatment of certain verbpatterns. This is discussed in 
section~\ref{remarks}

Also certain verbpatterns have been added because of the dictionary filling.
For some of them there are no rules yet. Some of them must perhaps be rejected.
This will be indicated where appropriate

\section{Naming Conventions}

There is some system in the names chosen for the patterns. The syntax sketched
below defines possible pattern names. However, this syntax also allows 
meaningless names such as `synCLOSEDOPENNPPROP' etc., and clearly 
overgenerates.

\begin{verbatim}
pattern :: `syn' argdescr[`_' argdescr]
argdescr :: ([`HET']  [`ASIF' | `IF'] [`OPEN' | `CLOSED'] 
             [rel | subcat | `PREP'] (cat | `MEASUREPHRASE')
            )                                                  | 
              `aanactive' | `dooractive' | `zijn'
rel :: `DO' | `IO' | `AAN' | `VOOR'
subcat :: `THAT' | `TE' | `INF' | `OMTE' | `LOC' | `DIR' | 
          `OTHER' | `TEMP' | `Q' | `SO'

\end{verbatim}

The element {\em  cat} can be replaced by any category from LSDOMAINT.

\section{Problems and Suggestions for amelioration}
\label{remarks}


The following problems were encountered:

\begin{description}
  \item[Optionality] Optionality of arguments can be indicated only in a rather
laborious way (by adding a verbpattern). This is very annoying, especially
for certain arguments which seem to be optional very often (e.g. 
{\em aanobjrel}s). 
  \item[Semi-arguments] Certain phrases, which have a somewhat intermediate status between 
arguments and adjuncts have been treated as arguments. This yields very 
unnatural verbpatterns in certain cases. Examples: resultative adjuncts,
(e.g. {\em  hij verft de deur {\bf groen}}), benefactives ({\em  voorobjrel}s):
{\em  hij bouwt een boot {\bf voor haar}}.
  \item[Double prepositional Objects] We decided to allow for a verbpattern
that allows two prepositional objects. This was not a very wise decision. The problem is that it is
very difficult to find general rules for the order of the arguments in such 
cases. 
\item[Directionals etc.] At the moment verbs take patterns that indicate 
whether they take a directional, locative
or predicative prepositional
phrase or adverb phrase as argument.

This is problematic for a number of reasons:

\begin{description}
  \item[] No distinction can be made in syntax between
{\em  hij is in het water gevallen, *hij is het water in gevallen,
*hij is in het water gereden, hij is het water in gereden
*hij is op de uur af gevallen, hij is op de muur af gereden} 
  \item[] temporal prepps as arguments cannot be dealt with. 
{\em cf. Kerstmis valt op zondag. De wedstrijd eindigt om zeven uur})
\end{description}
 
\item[Adverbs] It is impossible to specify that a verb requires the presence
of an adverbial phrase or an instrumental or manner prepositional phrase, as
is required for such words as {\em  formuleren, spellen} and for many
middle verbs.

\item[Psych Verbs] There is a class of verbs that have been treated in Rosetta3 
as normal transitive verbs. It concerns verbs such as 
{\em
?aangaan, aangrijpen, amuseren, ?baten, beangstigen, behagen, bedrukken,
?bekruipen, believen, ?beroeren, berouwen, betamen, bevreemden, bijliggen,
bijstaan, boeien, ?decipieren, desillusioneren, dwarszitten, fascineren,
frustreren, grieven, hinderen, interesseren, irriteren, krenken, ?kwellen,
?lusten, mishagen, ??misstaan, opmonteren, opvrolijken, overrompelen,
overvallen, ?passen, raken, ?schaden, schikken, spijten, steken, teleurstellen,
treffen, troosten, verbazen, verbijsteren, verheugen, ?verlichten, ?vermaken,
verontrusten, verrassen, verwonderen, voegen, ?de keel uithangen, ?zorgen baren
}

It is well-known that these verbs have a number of unusual properties,
e.g. their subject cannot bind bound anaphors, {\em  ze} in subject position
cannot be interpreted as {\em  iemand}, they cannot appear without the subject
to the right of {\em  ik liet}, they cannot be passivized, the subject and the
object can occur in the order object-subject in subordinate clauses etc.
See doc. R0289 (Jan Odijk - Psych Verbs, to appear), for more detailed 
information. 
These special properties are not accounted for in Rosetta3.
\end{description}

I suggest the following ways to solve these problems:

\begin{description}
  \item[Optionality] One suggestion would be to divide arguments into two 
classes, so-called {\em  primary arguments} and so-called {\em  secondary 
arguments}. {\em  Primary arguments} are obligatory or optional depending on 
the specific verb. Optionality must be indicated by adding a verbpattern.
{\em  Secondary} arguments are always optional: their optionality is 
independent of the specific verb. Candidates for {\em  secondary argument}
status are: {\em  aanobjrel}, {\em  prepobjrel}.

For primary arguments one might also think of a simplified notation, e.g.
brackets around a pattern or a part of a pattern to indicate optionality.

\item[Semi-Arguments] It is better to treat these as adjuncts which can only be 
added if the verb is specified as such. It is then necessary to add an 
attribute to the BVERBrecord, the attribute {\bf adjuncts} with as possible 
values e.g. a SET OF (resultativeAP, resultativeNP, benefactive, path), and
an optional  rule introducing adjuncts must be added to the grammar.
A verb like
{\em  bouwen} can then be a two-place verb, with patterns [{\em  synNP, synEMPTY
}] and with the value [{\em benefactive}] for the attribute {\bf adjuncts}. 
Such an approach would yield more natural patterns, and it would yield simpler 
derivations, i.e. all sorts of empty's to indicate optionality of 
semi-arguments are not necessary anymore.

Notice that a specific type of argument need not be treated uniformly. The 
directional PP in {\em  hij is {\bf naar Amsterdam} gelopen} might be treated as
a (primary) argument (it is obligatory), though the directional PP in 
{\em  hij bracht het boek {\bf naar zijn vader}} might be treated as an
{\em  path-adjunct}.


The following table contains some examples of pattern filling for a comparison 
of the two systems:

\addtolength{\leftmargin}{-.5\leftmargin}

\begin{trivlist}
  \item[] \mbox{}\\
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{.4\textwidth}|p{.4\textwidth}|p{.2\textwidth}|}
\hline
verb   & current system                    & \multicolumn{2}{c}{modified system}\\
\hline
       & synvps                            &  synvps                   & adjuncts\\
\hline
sturen & [synDONP\_DIROPENPREPPPROP, 
          synDONP\_DIREMPTY]               & [synNP]                   & [path]\\
\hline
lopen  & [synDONP\_DIROPENNPPROP]          & [synDONP\_DIROPENNPPROP]  & [] \\       
\hline

         synEMPTY\_RESULTEMPTY,
         synDONP\_OPENADJPPROP]            & [synNP, synEMPTY]         & [resultativeAP]\\
\hline

          synEMPTY\_DONP,
          synEMPTY\_EMPTY,
          synVOORNP\_EMPTY]                & [synNP, synEMPTY]         & [benefactive]\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{trivlist}


\addtolength{\leftmargin}{\leftmargin}

The modified system also allows (incorrectly) {\em  *Hij verft groen}, but this 
will be excluded by the rules interpreting the `subject' of {\em  groen} 
(Control rules).

In the current system it is possible to avoid the many EMPTY's only by 
postulating an ambiguity (e.g. by assuming that {\em  verven} is ambiguous 
between a three-place verb and a two-place verb). This, however, leads to
counterintuitive ambiguities, and to efficiency problems in analysis.


\item[Double prepositional Objects]
It is 
better to deal  with such cases by using the idiom-method, where the argument
order can be stated for each individual case, and where argument switch can be 
performed rather easily. 

Certain cases of prepositional object can perhaps be 
treated as adjuncts (e.g. {\em  over}-{\em  about} as a topic-adjunct, or 
certain {\em  met}-PP's that allow a subject to be in singular though they must 
be in plural without this {\em  met}-phrase, as {\em  comitative adjuncts}. 
Especially when the relevant PPs are optional the idiom method will not yield 
natural results either.

\item[Directionals etc.]
Their treatment should be changed in such a way that verbs can be specified
to take a {\bf pre}positional phrase or adverbial phrase as an argument, or
to take a {\bf post-} or {\bf circum}positional phrase or adverbial phrase
as an argument. PREPPs, and PREPPROPs should be  marked for this.

Whether these phrases are directional, locative, predicative or temporal 
is then a matter of the semantics (type system).

The semantics should also account for the difference between
{\em  hij is in het water gevallen} vs. {\em  hij is in het huis gevallen},
i.e. for some reason the preposition {\em  in} cannot have a directional 
interpretation when used with {\em  huis} as an object. 

The specifications {\em  dir}, {\em  loc} and {\em  other} occurring in 
patern names should be
changed  as follows:

dir.. \to postPP\\
loc.. \to prePP\\
other.. \to prePP\\

All of this in Dutch only. In English and Spanish the distinction between
{\em loc..}, {\em dir..} and {\em other..} can disappear.

If temporal features are put in the semantics as well, 
then many of the ambiguities
of the prepositions in syntax can simply disappear.

\item[Adverbs] I see no way to extend the verbpatterns to deal with this 
problem,
but we might  assume a new attribute specifying whether an adverbial or
prepositional phrase is 
required, and if so what kind of phrase (manner, or instrumental) it should be.
A separate transformation might check whether such a phrase is present if 
required, and filter the structures if required.

\item[Psych Verbs] It is probably best to introduce new verbpatterns for these
verbs, and adapt relevant rules accordingly. 
See doc. R0308 for more specific details.
\end{description}

{\bf It is absolutely necessary to revise certain patterns before large-scale 
dictionary filling is going to be done!!}

\section{Other cases}

For the following cases no appropriate characterization of the verbpattern
has been established yet:

\begin{examples}
 \example We kunnen ervan op aan   (opaan?)
 \example Die jas staat haar goed (two-place middle verb?)
\end{examples}

The pattern {\em  synRESULTEMPTY} has been suggested by Andr\'{e} de Vos to 
deal with such cases as {\em  hij scheert zich} vs. 
{\em  hij scheert zich glad}. I think this is absurd. Under the modifed 
proposal we can classify {\em  zich scheren} simply as an inherently reflexive
intransitive verb (pattern:{\em  synnovpargs}), and allow {\em  resultativeAP}
as one of the values in the attribute {\bf adjuncts}.

This case should be dealt with in a manner comparable to such cases as
{\em  Hij at} vs. {\em  Hij at zijn buikje rond}, {\em  Hij at zijn bord leeg}
etc., which are CLOSEDADJPPROPs with intransitive (activity) verbs exressing
resultatives. (paraphrases: {\em hij at zodat zijn buikje rond werd}, {hij at
zodat zijn bord leeg raakte}. 

Nevertheless, there is a problem w.r.t. {\em  zich glad scheren}. Notice that
also the following are possible:

\begin{description}
  \item[] hij at zich vol (vs. hij at) 
  \item[] hij dronk zich dronken (vs. hij dronk)
  \item[] hij liep zich rot (vs. hij liep) 
\end{description}

In all these cases a simple intransitive verb is combined with a closedadjpprop
with {\em  zich} as a subject. The sentence {\em  Hij scheert zich glad}
cannot be derived in this manner, cf. also {\em  Hij schaamt zich rot/dood/
blauw} etc., because there is no intransitive {\em  scheren, schamen}. So 
a slightly diofferent derivation must be assumed.
