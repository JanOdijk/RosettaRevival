\section{Introduction}

This document describes how modals are translated in Rosetta3.
Modal verbs need special attention in a machine translation system because they 
have special characteristics that do not correspond over languages.

\begin{itemize}
\item {\bf Morphology: defective paradigm}\\
One of the most urgent problems is the lack of a full tense paradigm in some 
languages as e.g.\ in English. Most English modal verbs do not have an 
infinitive or past participle form. Some of them neither have a past tense form 
(e.g.\ {\em may}) or a present tense form (e.g.\ {\em might}). The 
corresponding verbs in other languages such as Dutch and Spanish behave more 
like `full' verbs. This causes the following type of translation problem as in 
example (1):\\

\begin{lxam}
&D&&Hij zei dat hij om 3 uur had kunnen komen\\
&E&*&He said that he had `could' come at 3 o'clock\\
&S&&Dijo que hab\'{\i}a podido venir a las tres
\end{lxam}

An acceptable translation into English would be:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&& He said that he had been able to come at 3 o'clock
\end{lxam}
or\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&He said that it had been possible for him to come at 3 o'clock
\end{lxam}

This means that {\em kunnen} and {\em poder} 
sometimes have to be translated into {\em be 
able} and {\em be possible}, i.e.\ the auxiliary verb {\em be} and an adjective
, which is possible thanks to the isomorphic subgrammar approach in Rosetta3.
This kind of escape exists for most of the English modal verbs. 

\item {\bf Syntax: main verb or auxiliary verb?}\\
Another problem is that the modal verbs in Dutch and Spanish behave as main 
verbs and the English ones as auxiliaries, i.e. they occur at different 
positions. The question that is related to this 
problem is whether these verbs should be basic expressions or inserted 
syncategorematically into the 
clause  by special rules. 
One reason for dealing with modal verbs as basic expressions is the fact
that some of them impose selectional restrictions on some of their arguments.
(cf. {\em  it can rain} does not mean {\em  it is able to rain}). Imposing
such selectional restrictions is  simple if these verbs are considered
to be basic expressions, but it is more complicated if they are not.\\
In Rosetta3 we have chosen for 
basic expressions that have an argument structure. All of them have at least 
one propositional argument. In English, these verbs get their syntactic 
auxiliary status at the moment their propositional argument is substituted.
This is done in RmodalComplSentSubst1 and RmodalComplSentSubst2.

\item {\bf Irrealis constructions}\\
Some past tense forms of modal verbs in English have an irrealis interpretation
 as is illustrated by the following examples:\\

\begin{lxam}
&E&&If he could come,...\\
&D&&Als hij zou kunnen komen,...\\
&S&&Si pudiera venir,..
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam}
&E&&He should come\\
&D&&Hij zou moeten komen\\
&S&&Deber\'{\i}a venir
\end{lxam}

Past tense forms of other verbs do not have this interpretation. 
\item {\bf Condicionalis}\\
In Dutch 
the o.v.t. forms of verbs can  have a similar interpretation:\\

\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij zou het alleen doen, als ik hem zou helpen\\
&&&Hij deed het alleen, als ik hem hielp\\
&&&Hij deed het niet, tenzij ik hem hielp
\end{lxam}

In Spanish indicative 
(non-future) past tense forms do not have an irrealis interpretation, 
except for the `imperfecto' in main clauses in informal speech.\\
It is necessary to keep this irrealis meaning apart from the past meaning. Some 
special rules were needed to account for these phenomena (see section 
\ref{remarks}).

\item {\bf Modal + have problem}\\
The translation into Dutch of the English example
(\ref{exam1}) is (\ref{exam2}a), (\ref{exam2}b) or 
(\ref{exam3}).\\

\begin{lxam}
\label{exam1}
&&&He could have come
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam}
\label{exam2}
&a&&Hij had kunnen komen\\
&b&&Hij zou hebben kunnen komen
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam}
\label{exam3}
&&&Hij zou gekomen kunnen zijn
\end{lxam}

(\ref{exam2}a) and (\ref{exam2}b) can be considered paraphrases, but 
(\ref{exam2}) is not synonymous with (\ref{exam3}). A regular compositional 
translation of 
(\ref{exam1}) would be (\ref{exam3}) and not (\ref{exam2}a) or (\ref{exam2}b).
(\ref{exam2}a) would be translated regularly into the impossible (\ref{exam4}) 
and (\ref{exam2}b) into (\ref{exam5}):\\

\begin{lxam}
\label{exam4}
&&*& He had `could' come
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam}
\label{exam5}
&&*&He would have `could' come
\end{lxam}
We needed some special rules to account for this problem as will be explained 
in section \ref{remarks}.
\end{itemize}

In the next section the modal `meanings' or topics
 we want to distinguish will be summed up 
and illustrated with examples of Dutch, English and Spanish. If a modal topic
has not been implemented in Rosetta3, this will be indicated. In section 
\ref{trans} the translation relations as implemented in Rosetta3 
will be 
given in a table. This section will be followed by a section with remarks on 
that translation scheme. Section \ref{special}
will contain a summary of all special rules that 
have been written or that still have to be written
to be able to translate modals.

\section{The Modal Expressions of Rosetta3}
\label{modals}
\subsection{Modality}
\label{modality}
We distinguished the following modal `meanings' or topics:
\begin{enumerate}
\item volition
\item ability
\item obligation
\item possibility
\item necessity
\item permission
\item future
\item tendency
\item concession
\item reported speech
\item irrealis
\item condition
\end{enumerate}

We will give examples of each of them for all languages in the order 
Dutch-English-Spanish below. Because of the defective paradigms of the English
modal verbs, an example of the `escape solution' also will be given.
\begin{enumerate}
\item volition:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em wil} komen\\
&&&He {\em wants} to come\\
&&&{\em Quiere} venir
\end{lxam}

\item ability:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em kan} komen\\
&&&He {\em can} come/ He {\em is able} to come\\
&&&{\em Puede} venir
\end{lxam}

\item obligation:
\begin{enumerate}
\item no restriction on scope of negation:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em moet} komen\\
&&&He {\em must} come/He {\em has} to come/He {\em is obliged} to come\\
&&&{\em Debe} venir/{\em tiene que} venir
\end{lxam}
\item obligatory in scope of negation:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em hoeft} niet te komen\\
&&&He {\em need} not come/He does not {\em need} to come\\
&&&No {\em debe} venir/no {\em tiene que} venir
\end{lxam}
\end{enumerate}

\item possibility:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em kan} komen\\
&&&He {\em can} come/ He {\em may} come/it {\em is possible} for him to come\\
&&&{\em Puede} venir
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij komt {\em misschien}\\
&&&He {\em might} come/he {\em may} come\\
&&&{\em Quiz\'{a}s} venga
\end{lxam}

\item necessity:
\begin{enumerate}
\item no restriction on scope of negation:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em moet} komen\\
&&&He {\em must} come/He {\em has} to come/It {\em is} 
 {\em necessary} for him to come\\
&&&{\em Debe} venir/{\em tiene que} venir
\end{lxam}
\item obligatorily in scope of negation:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em hoeft} niet te komen\\
&&&He {\em need} not come/He does not {\em need} to come\\
&&&No {\em debe} venir/no {\em tiene que} venir
\end{lxam}
\end{enumerate}

\item permission:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em mag} komen\\
&&&He {\em can} come/he {\em may} come/he {\em is allowed} to come\\
&&&{\em Puede} venir
\end{lxam}

\item future:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em zal} komen\\
&&&He {\em will} come\\
&&&Ven{\em dr\'{a}}
\end{lxam}

\item tendency:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Het {\em wil} maar niet regenen\\
&&&It does not want to rain???\\
&&&???
\end{lxam}
It appears that this use of the verb {\em  willen} requires the presence of
the word {\em  maar} and the presence of negation.

\item concession:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Het {\em mag} dan regenen,..\\
&&&Rain though it might???\\
&&&???
\end{lxam}
It seems that the word {\em dan} is required.

\item reported speech:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em zou} dan komen...\\
&&&???\\
&&&???
\end{lxam}

\item irrealis:
\begin{lxam}
&&&Als hij {\em zou kunnen} komen\\
&&&If he {\em could} come\\
&&&Si {\em podr\'{\i}a} venir
\end{lxam}
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij {\em zou moeten} komen\\
&&&He {\em should} come\\
&&&{\em Deber\'{\i}a} venir
\end{lxam}

\item condition:
\begin{lxam}
&&&Als hij  proberen {\em zou} te komen/als hij probeer{\em de} te komen\\
&&&If he {\em would} try to come\\
&&&Si tratar{\em \'{\i}a} de venir
\end{lxam}
\end{enumerate}

{\bf Tendency, concessive} and {\bf reported speech} have not been implemented 
in Rosetta3, because more research is needed here. 
Concession and condition can probably be treated best by assuming a special 
substitution 
rule for concessive 
adverbial subordinate sentences, that turns a structure of e.g.\
the form [hoewel....V] into [.... mogen...dan...V] when substituting it, in a manner
resembling the method used for
introducing conditional verb-initial subordinate clauses  ({\em {\bf 
Komt hij op tijd,} 
dan halen we het nog}).\\
Rules for {\bf irrealis} 
with modal verbs have been written, but have not been tested yet. Rules for
conditional sentences have not been written yet.

\subsection{The modal expressions of Dutch}
\label{modalsdutch}
The modal expressions of Dutch can be listed in the following table. Their 
modal `meaning' or topic is indicated.\\

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
expression  &  topic\\
\hline
willen      &  volition\\
kunnen (2x)      &  ability, possibility\\
moeten (2x)      &  obligation, necessity (no restriction on scope of negation)\\
hoeven (2x)      &  obligation, necessity (obligatorily in scope of negation)\\
mogen       &  permission\\
zullen      &  future\\
\hline
misschien   &  possiblity\\
\hline
zou         &  irrealis, condition\\
zou kunnen (2x)  &  irrealis + possibility, ability\\
zou moeten (2x)  &  irrealis + necessity, obligation\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\ \\

Remarks:
\begin{itemize}
\item
{\em Zou kunnen} and {\em zou moeten} are regular irrealis forms in Dutch. They 
are listed here because of the English translation. They are made by special 
deixis rules.

\item 
There is a special transformation, called {\em  Tmodalswitch1} 
(in file dutch:tc\_modalswitch.mrule, mrules13.mrule) that accounts 
for
the fact that in Dutch certain modal verbs allow two orders when they are 
finite and combined with some infinitive, cf. {\em  ..omdat hij moet komen 
} vs. {\em  omdat hij komen moet}. This special transformation applies
in analysis only and turns a structure where a finite modal verb taking
an infinitive without {\em  te} follows this infinitive into a structure
where the modal verb precedes the infinitive.

\item 
There is special transformation, called {\em  Tgaandeletion}
(in file dutch:tc\_modalswitch.mrule, mrules13.mrule),
that performs the following tasks:
If a modal verb has the pattern {\em  synDIROPENPREPPPROP}, and it has
the infinitive {\em  gaan} in its infinitival complement, and if there is
a directional argument ({\em  dirargrel}), then {\em  gaan} is deleted.

This transformation applies in analysis only. It is intended to account for 
such translation relations as {\em  hij wil naar school} - {\em  he wants to go 
to school}.

The following rules are still to be added:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a transformation dealing with slightly more complex cases, e.g.
{\em  hij heeft naar school gewild} cannot be dealt with adequately yet
(it should be turned into {\em  hij heeft naar school willen gaan})
  \item an obligatory transformation or filter should filter all occurrences
of modal verbs that have  been subject to the transformations mentioned 
above (so it should precede these transformations), in order to stop the
derivations of these sentences as normal  directional argument taking 
verbs. This is necessary, because otherwise the grammar of English
will attempt to derive {\em  he wants to school}, in which it
will not succeed, so that the isomorphy requirement is violated.
\end{enumerate}


Also a similar transformation should be added (for certain modal verbs)
to deal with {\em  doen}-deletion
for such cases as {\em  hij kan het, mag het}  (cf. {\em  he can, may do it}
and perhaps for {\em  hebben}-deletion (cf. {\em  Hij wil het, hij moet het}
and {\em  he wants to have it, he must have it} etc.

A question that arose when dealing with these and similar examples, is which 
grammar should contain special rules to deal with such cases as these. 
It can be argued
that for cases such as these the grammar of English should contain
special rules. For motivation, see doc R0???.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{The modal expressions of English}
\label{modalsenglish}
The modal expressions of English can be listed in the following table. Their 
modal `meaning' or topic and syntactic category are indicated.\\

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
\hline
syn. cat. & expression  &  topic\\
\hline
verb      & want        & volition\\
          & can (3x)         & ability, possibility, permission\\
          & could (2x)       & ability, possibility,\\
          &             & irrealis + ability, possibility\\
          & may (2x)         & possibility, permission\\
          & might       & possibility\\
          & must (2x)        & obligation, necessity (no restriction on scope 
of negation)\\
          & have to (2x)    & obligation, necessity (no restriction on scope ofnegation)\\
          & to be to (2x)    & obligation, necessity (no restriction on scope ofnegation)\\
          & ought to (2x)    & ?\\
          & need(mod) (2x)   & obligation, necessity (no obligatorily in scope of negation)\\
          & need(main) (2x)  & obligation, necessity (no obligatorily in scope of  negation)\\
          & will        & future\\
          & would (2x)       & future, irrealis, condition\\
          & shall       & future\\
          & should (2x)      & irrealis + necessity, obligation\\
          & dare             & ?\\
\hline
adj       & able        & ability\\
          & possible    & possibility\\
          & obliged     & obligation\\
          & necessary   & necessity\\
          & allowed     & permission\\
\hline  
\end{tabular}
\ \\

Remarks:
\begin{itemize}
  \item {\bf shall} should be considered as a variant of {\em will} in case of a 
               first person (singular) subject. It will be introduced (only
               in analysis) as a variant of {\em will} in cases of first 
               person, by exchanging it syncategorematically. 
This special transformation applies after the
mood rules and before NegAuxAdapt.

  \item {\bf Auxiliary status}:\\
The auxiliary status of English modal verbs is accounted for in 
RmodalComplSentSubst1 + 2.\\
The modal is inserted in aux position in its argument. These rules are
`subgrammar switch' rules of the kind also needed for {\em graag/like}-cases.

  \item There are some problems superdeixis adaptation and subgrammar switch
in general that also apply to the treatment of English modals, cf. Rdoc.\ 320. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Modal expressions of Spanish}
\label{modalsspanish}
The modal expressions of Spanish can be listed in the following table. Their 
modal `meaning' or topic is indicated.\\

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
expression  &  topic\\
\hline
querer      & volition\\
poder (2x)       & ability, possibility, permission\\
deber (4x)      & necessity, obligation (+/- restriction negation)\\
tener que (4x)  & necessity, obligation (+/- restriction negation)\\
hay que (2x)    & necessity, obligation\\
`abstract future verb' & future\\
\hline
quiz\'{a}s & possibility\\
\hline
`subjuntivo del pasado', `condicional' & irrealis, condition\\
pudiera, podr\'{\i}a (2x)   & irrealis + possibility, ability\\
debiera, deber\'{\i}a (2x)  & irrealis + necessity, obligation\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\ \\
Remarks:
\begin{itemize}
  \item {\bf Future}:\\
The `abstract future verb' is spelled out as the `futuro' or 
`condicional' of the main verb in the deixis rules.
  \item {\bf irrealis}:\\
{\em Pudiera, podr\'{\i}a, deber\'{\i}a}
 and {\em debiera} are regular irrealis forms in Spanish. 
They are listed here because of the translation into English. They are made by 
special deixis rules. Those for subjuntivo have not been written yet.\\
In an adverbial irrealis clause with the conjunction {\em si} the subjuntivo 
del pasado should be used. In main clauses the condicional is used, but in the 
perfect tenses the subjuntivo del pasado also can be used.\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Ik had het gedaan, als jij me geholpen had.\\
&&&Lo habr\'{\i}a/hubiera hecho si me hubieras ayudado t\'{u}.
\end{lxam}
\item {\bf Hay que}:\\
{\em Hay} is an impersonal verb. The basis  is the verb {\em haber} and the 
rule Tyinsertion (+ filter) accounts for the insertion of {\em -y} in the 
appropriate cases. We use this verb not only for the existential {\em hay}
({\em  haberkey}) but also for the modal {\em hay que} ({\em haberque010key, 
haberque120key}).
It is assumed that {\em  haberque120} is a normal personal verb. A special
rule (cf. Se-spelling rules: Thaberquesubjdel + filter) 
check whether the subject of this verb
equals a specific EMPTY-category, and if so it deletes
 it. The structure is filtered
out otherwise. The specific EMPTY is either the SE-empty-key, corresponding to 
the Dutch {\em men}, or a special one
which is mapped onto {\em  wij/we}, and which can occur only in this 
construction, but for this case no rules have been written yet. (See also doc.\
398 on Spanish SE). 
\item {\bf Tense raising for epistemic modals}:\\
The epistemic modals (possibility and necessity, thetavp 010) in Spanish have a 
special characteristic: if their argument sentence has a {\em Pr\'{e}terito} 
or {\em Imperfecto}, this tense is raised to the modal verb. This does not 
hold for the {\em Perfecto}!\\
So we have to translate:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&& Dat moet Jan gisteren geweest zijn
\end{lxam}
into\\
\begin{lxam}
\label{exam30}
&&& Tuvo que ser Jan ayer
\end{lxam}
but\\
\begin{lxam}
&&& Ik kan hem verkeerd begrepen hebben
\end{lxam}
into\\
\begin{lxam}
&&& Puedo haberle comprendido mal
\end{lxam}
It has not been possible yet to implement this tense raising phenomenon , 
because modal verbs only allow for infinite sentence arguments
, in which case it is not possible anymore to see if it was an imperfective or 
a perfective sentence because the attribute {\em aspecto} 
already has been set to {\em 
omegaaspecto} on behalf of the surface parser. We also assumed in Rosetta3 that 
infinitives except for {\em hebben/zijn, have, haber} are temporally dependent, 
so it will be impossible to have a present tense in the main sentence and a 
past infinitive in the complement sentence. The only possibility seems to be to 
derive (\ref{exam30}) from {\em Tiene que haber sido Jan ayer} by deleting {\em 
haber}, giving {\em ser} an infinitive form and {\em tener} the Pret\'{e}rito.
But now we never will derive an imperfecto, as {\em haber sido} should be 
interpreted here as perfective. The question is whether that is necessary, because 
this Imperfecto maybe has an habitual meaning and in Rosetta3 habituality
has not been 
accounted for yet. Because of the problematic status, the rules 
for this kind of tense raising have not been written. 
\end{itemize}

\section{Remarks}
\label{remarks}
\begin{description}
  \item[hoeven,moeten] Originally {\em hoeven} was also translated into
{\em  have to}. This has been dropped because we want to keep the difference in
meaning between e.g. {\em  hij moet dit niet doen} vs. {\em  hij hoeft dit niet 
te doen}, or  between e.g. {\em  Moet hij dit niet doen?} and {\em  Hoeft hij 
dit niet te doen?}. For this reason {\em  hoeven} will be translated into
{\em  need} (modal and main) only.\\
In order to maintain isomorphy it is required to distinguish in Spanish two
verbs {\em  deber}, one negatively polar, and one not. The reason for this 
is the following. Suppose we would not distinguish these two verbs in Spanish.
In that case the following mapping would be required (for modals expressing
obligation):\\

\begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
\hline
Dutch & Interlingua &Spanish\\
\hline
hoeven & hoevenneeddeber2 & deber2\\
\hline
moeten & moetenmustdeber2 & deber2\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\ \\

If this mapping is assumed the following problem will arise. Suppose some
Spanish sentence (e.g. {\em  Debe hacerlo})
 contains the verb {\em deber} but no negation. This sentence
will be accepted, and one possible IL-representation is a semantic derivation 
tree containing the basic meaning {\em  hoevenneeddeber2}. This semantic 
derivation tree will now be translated into Dutch, yielding a syntactic 
derivation tree containing the basic expression {\em  hoeven}. This syntactic
derivation tree, however, does not yield any Dutch expression, because it 
contains the verb {\em  hoeven}, but no negation at all( cf. * {\em  hij hoeft 
het te doen}). In this situation 
there is no isomorphy. Distinguishing two verbs {\em  deber}, one which is
negatively polar and translates into Dutch {\em hoeven}, and one which
is not negatively polar and translates into Dutch {\em  moeten} solves this
situation. 
An alternative would be to define polarity restrictions on IL-trees, but this
will yield unnatural analysis of {\em  iemand} vs. {\em  someone} and {\em  
anyone}.


  \item[defective paradigm]:\\
\begin{description}
  \item[may, might] We translate {\em he might come}(Paste tense?) into 
      {\em hij komt misschien} (Present Tense). We assume that {\em  misschien}
translates into an abstract verb. This abstract verb is turned into {\em  may}
or {\em  might}, depending on the deixis. If {\em  would} is present (e.g.
{\em hij zou misschien komen}) this abstract verb is turned into {\em  might}, and
{\em would} is deleted. This approach implies that {\em  hij kwam misschien} is 
translated into {\em  he might come}. If someone has some idea why
{\em  hij kwam misschien} sounds strange, we can perhaps exclude it.
Because this case is still problematic, the special rules have not been written 
yet.
  \item[deixis] We can account for the difference between:
{\em  Er had gedanst kunnen worden} vs. {\em *Er heeft gedanst kunnen worden}
by assuming that the finite perfect tense of epistemic modal verbs cannot be formed, en
by deriving {\em  Er had gedanst kunnen worden} from {\em  Er zou gedanst 
hebben kunnen worden}, i.e. {\em  zou} + {\em  hebben/zijn} $\leftarrow$ 
{\em  had, was}? (See below).\\
But these conditions might be too restrictive, because it will then be 
impossible to derive:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&& Hij heeft daar altijd goed kunnen dansen
\end{lxam}
which is a correct sentence for some people. Note that {\em kunnen} is a 
two-place-verb here. 
In the Dutch special past deixis rule 
the perfect tense is not generated for modal verbs, but it is allowed in 
analysis, cf for example:\\
\begin{lxam}
&&&Hij heeft daar gisteren goed kunnen dansen
\end{lxam}
In English the perfect tense for modal verbs is impossible. The Spanish case is 
not clear yet. Because of these problems, the conditions have not been added to 
the retro rules yet.\\

Maybe these examples can be accounted for by assuming that {\em  kunnen} is
in fact even more ambiguous. Suppose that two-place {\em  kunnen} is ambigous
between {\em  in staat zijn} and (something like) 
{\em  in staat gesteld worden}. Then, the generalization that
epistemic modal {\em  kunnen}, meaning {\em  mogelijk zijn} cannot appear in
perfect tenses can perhaps be maintained, though two-place {\em  kunnen}
can appear in perfect tenses. The latter two examples would then be examples
where the reading {\em  in staat gesteld worden} is possible, and this
fact gives one the impression that epistemic {\em  kunnen} can occur here.
Clearly, this requires further investigation.

\end{description}

  \item[Irrealis]:\\
\begin{description}
  \item[could, should] 
We translate these into {\em  zou kunnen, zou moeten} and 
{\em  podr\'{\i}a/pudiera, deber\'{\i}a/debiera} respectively, by a special 
deixisrule that 
introduces {\em zou} in Dutch, puts {\em  deber, poder} in the past future or 
past subjuntivo in
Spanish , and
 turns {\em  can, must} into {\em  could, should} in English. These special
deixisrules are RFinIrrmodaldeixis and RFinIrrmodalsuperdeixis.
The subjuntivo variant in Spanish has not been written yet, because this also 
depends on the treatment of irrealis conditional sentences.

\item[zou +V] In main clauses {\em  zou} + V is rewritten as the past tense
of V if this V is followed by more verbs, e.g. {\em  hij zou proberen te komen}
is turned into {\em  hij probeerde te komen}. Perhaps this must be done if and 
only if an irreal conditional sentence is present.

In subordinate clauses (conditional only?) {\em  zou} + V is rewritten
as the past tense of V. All this is optional. These transformations apply
after the mood rules, but have not been written yet.
\end{description}

\item[modal + have]:\\
{\em  hij had kunnen komen}, {\em  hij zou gekomen kunnen
zijn}, {\em  hij zou hebben kunnen komen} will be accounted for in the 
following way: {\em  Hij had kunnen komen} is considered to be variant of
{\em  hij zou hebben kunnen komen}. This will be achieved by the rule 
mentioned immediately above. 

The sentences {\em  hij had kunnen komen} and {\em  hij zou gekomen kunnen zijn}
are not considered to be paraphrases of each other, but both must be translated
into English: {\em  he could have come}. For this purpose a special Retro rule
(Rretrospec) has been
 written in English that introduces {\em  have} before {\em  can} (which is
normally not allowed, of course). The normal deixis rules apply, and after
the deixis rules the combination of {\em  had} + {\em  can} is turned into
{\em  could} + {\em  have} (provided no {\em  have} is present already). 
Other combinations of {\em  have} ({\em  have, has}) + {\em  can} 
are filtered out here. A similar story holds for {\em  have} + 
{\em  must} (turned  into {\em  should have}) or
{\em  have + may} (turned into {\em  might have}). This transformation and 
filter are called Thavemodaladaptation and Fhavemodaladaptation and induce 
lowering of bonus. They have not been tested yet.

In a schema:

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
\hline
DUTCH                  & ENGLISH\\
\hline
hij had kunnen komen   & he would have been able  to come\\
                       & it would have been possible for him to come\\
                       & he could have come \\
                       &  (from: {\em he had can come})\\
hij zou gekomen kunnen zijn & he could have come\\
hij zou hebben kunnen komen & he would have been able to come\\
                            & it would have been possible for him to come\\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\end{description}
