\section{Introduction}
The purpose of this paper is to propose a translation method for semi-idioms.
As the name indicates, semi-idioms are different from idioms; this can be
inferred from the syntactic and semantic behaviour. The syntactic behaviour of
semi-idioms is also different from literal expressions. 

\paragraph{Preview Sectioning}
In section~\ref{CSI} general characteristics of semi-idioms will be given. In
section~\ref{CRIT} a number of criteria will be given that a translation method 
for semi-idioms should meet. In section~\ref{MET} a
method will be given that meets these criteria. Under
this method semi-idioms are treated in syntax. 
The method given in Rosetta document 213 (Jan Landsbergen, Joep Rous; 
The Transfer Modules of
Rosetta3) treats semi-idioms in Transfer. This method, however does not
meet all of the criteria.

\section{Characteristics of Semi-idioms}
\label{CSI}

Semi-idioms are expressions that consist of more than word, of which a part is
used idiomatically and of which another part is non-idiomatic (in the following
we will call the first part the head and the second part the complement).
Examples are given in (\ref{si1a}-\ref{si1g}). 

\begin{lxam} \label{si1a}
&a&& give a lecture\\
&b&& een  les  geven\\
&c&& een voordracht geven\\
&d&& een voordracht houden\\
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam} \label{si1b}
&a&& pay attention to\\
&b&& aandacht schenken aan\\
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam} \label{si1c}
&a&& make progress\\
&b&& make headway\\
&c&& vooruitgang boeken
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam} \label{si1d}
&a&& make a blunder\\
&b&& een flater slaan\\
&c&& een blunder maken
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam} \label{si1e}
&a&& give sth/sb a kick\\
&b&& iets/iemand een trap geven
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam} \label{si1f}
&a&& white lie\\
&b&& zware straf\\
&c&& zwart geld
\end{lxam}

\begin{lxam} \label{si1g}
&a&& zijn oren tuiten\\
&b&& zijn oren suizen
\end{lxam}


As can be seen from the examples in (\ref{si1f}) there are semi-idioms with a
non-verbal head; here only semi-idioms with a verbal head will be discussed
since they represent the most interesting class. The examples in (\ref{si1g}) 
show that, apparently, the subject can be the complement of a semi-idiom.

\paragraph{Characteristics of Semi-idioms}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The head is used idiomatically, so that there is no guarantee that the
literal translation is the correct one. The term idiomatic is somewhat
misleading; what is meant is that the head does not have its 'regular meaning'.
For example {\em pay} normally means give somebody money, but not in the
expression {\em pay attention to}. A more general formulation is that the
predicate is not used in its regular meaning; in this way also semi-idioms like
{\em white lie } are captured. The adjective {\em white} is the predicate and
the noun {\em lie} is an argument of this predicate. In {\em pay attention to}, 
{\em pay} is the predicate and {\em attention} an argument of this predicate.
  \item The complement of the head has a regular meaning, i.e. it can be used 
outside the context of the head in the same meaning.
  \item There are no restrictions on transformations for semi-idioms. That is,
semi-idioms undergo the same transformations the expression would undergo if it
were literal\footnote{There is one exception: Semi-idioms cannot occur in
pseudo-cleft constructions (cf. \star What he took of John, was advantage).
Considering the absence of a theory of pseudo-cleft constructions we will
disregard this issue for the moment.}. 
  \item Semi-idioms have a normal compositional meaning. The head has its
'nonstandard' meaning and the complement has a regular meaning; from the
meanings of the head and the complement the meaning of the expression as a
whole can be determined compositionally. 
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Discussion Characteristics}
Note that these characteristics are superficial and that there are borderline 
cases. In particular there are exceptions to the second characteristic, as in 
the complex expressions {\em keep tabs on} and {\em rekening houden met} where 
the complements {\em tabs} and {\em rekening} cannot be used outside the 
context of the semi-idiom in the same meaning. These expressions do, however,
show the syntactic behaviour of semi-idioms, i.e. they behave syntactically as
if they were literal. The discriminating test to check whether a complex
expression is an semi-idiom or a literal expression would be that the parts of
a semi-idiom cannot be split up over a question and its answer as in
(\ref{si2}). 

\begin{lxam} \label{si2}
&a&& What did he give John?\\
&b&\star& A kick\\
&c&& Wat sloeg hij?\\
&d&\star& Een flater
\end{lxam}

However, other complex expressions cannot occur in this configuration either. 
Consider the examples in (\ref{si3}).

\begin{lxam} \label{si3}
&a&& He has a heart-attack\\
&b&& What does he have?\\
&c&\star& A heart-attack\\
&d&& He has three children\\
&e&& What does he have?\\
&f&\star& Three children
\end{lxam}

The difference between the expressions in (\ref{si3}) and semi-idioms is that
the verbs in (\ref{si3}) select an argument that meets certain type
restrictions, while the heads of semi-idioms select an element of a limited
class of words. 

It will be clear from the characteristics that semi-idioms are different from
idioms. 

Informally, what has to be done is that a relation should be established
between the two parts of the semi-idiom, such that there is a check whether the
two parts of the semi-idiom cooccur. 

This relation between the two parts of the semi-idiom has to be established on
skeys rather than on mkeys. If we take for example {\em make a blunder}, then
possible translations are {\em een flater slaan} and {\em een blunder maken}.
If there were only one mkey corresponding to {\em make}, {\em maken} and {\em
slaan} and only one mkey corresponding to {\em blunder} and {\em flater} and if
there were no check on skeys, then generation of ill-formed expressions like
{\em een blunder slaan} and {\em een flater maken} cannot be prevented. This 
relation should be listed and checked in syntax or transfer.

IL-test applies as customary. This is necessary to prevent the translation of 
{\em een voordracht geven} into {\em give a nomination} when {\em give} is part 
of a semi-idiom. In this document we will not discuss IL-test.

\section{Criteria}
\label{CRIT}

In this section a number of criteria will be given that a solution for the 
above problem should meet.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A lexical relation has to be established between skeys.
  \item It has to be possible to deal with overlapping semi-idioms, eg. {\em
hij geeft hem een zware straf} in which {\em straf geven} and {\em zware straf}
are semi-idioms. 
  \item Efficiency, ease of implementation.
  \item Flexibility. Future changes or extensions should be easy to add.
  \item Locality. When an expression has been found which possibly is the head 
of a semi-idiom, it should be easy to find its complement. This is possible if 
the head and the complement can occur in a limited number of configurations and 
if they are in any sense 'near' to each other.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Method}
\label{MET}
\subsection{Global description}
The treatment of semi-idoms comprises a number of steps:
\begin {itemize}
\item The introduction of a set SKEYS-SID of {\em skeys}. The skeys of this set
correspond to the 
heads of semi-idoms. For instance, apart from an ordinary skey for the verb 
{\em geven} there will be an skey for each semi-idiomatic reading of 
{\em geven}: there will be an skey for {\em geven} in {\em een voordracht
geven}, another skey for {\em geven} in {\em een schreeuw geven} etc..
All skeys in SKEYS-SID are related to semi-idioms, that is, the set of ordinary
skeys and SKEYS-SID are disjunct.

The skeys from SKEYS-SID will not occur in S-DICT (which defines the relation
between fkeys and skeys) because they are only relevant in the M-PARSER,
M-GENERATOR and TRANSFER modules. BLEX, however, will contain one entry
for each skey in SKEYS-SID. Finally, in IL-DICT the relation is specified 
between the semi-idiom skeys and mkeys. 

\item Furhermore, we propose to add to each set of START-rules 
(for VERB's, ADJ's, etc.) a special START-rule for the treatment of semi-idioms. 
The task of this rule is (from an analytical point of view) to
check whether a 
the basic expression (BVERB, BADJ, etc.) occurs in the context of a specified 
argument. If that is the case, the rule replaces this basic expression 
by a basic expression with an skey 
from the set SKEYS-SID which is the head of the semi-idiom. E.g. the basic 
expression BVERB\{$geven$\} is
replaced by the basic expression BVERB\{$geven_{voorkeur}$\} if the basic
expression BNOUN\{$voorkeur$\} occurs in the sentence. In order to express the
condition on the semi-idiom argument in an M-rule, the M-rule notation has to 
be extended according to [Rous:R0258].

\item We also need a new dictionary ( to be called SEMI-ID-DICT) which defines 
the relation between 
basic expressions with a literal interpretation and their corresponding
semi-idiomatic counterparts. The dictionary contains tuples of the form:\\
\begin{verbatim}
           < skey1, skey2, skey3, argnr >
\end{verbatim}
Here, skey1 is the skey of an ordinary basic expression,\\
skey2 is the skey of the set SKEYS-SID which expresses the corresponding semi-idiom reading,\\
skey3 is the skey of the argument of the semi-idiom and, finally, 
argnr defines the argument position of the semi-idiom argument skey3.

Because this dictionary must be accessed inside an M-rule some extra
provisions in the M-rule notation have to be made.
\item The ordinary START rule and the START rule for semi-idioms have the same
corresponding IL-rule because a semi-idiom in one language may correspond with
a literal expression in the other. However, if we don't take any precautions
the ordinary START rules will also be applicable (in generation) to expressions
with skeys from SKEYS-SID causing a lot of unwanted ambiguities.
In order to prevent these ambiguities both rules must be complementary,
that is, ordinary START rules may not be applicable to expressions with 
skeys form SKEYS-SID and semi-idiom START rules may not be applicable
to expression with ordinary skeys.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Detailed description}

A 2-argument START-rule for semi-idioms will have the following form:\\

\begin{verbatim}
<m1: SUBVERB{SUBVERB1}[BVERB(key1)]
>
<m2: NPVAR{NPVARREC1}
>
<m3: NPVAR{NPVARREC2}^NP{NPREC1}[mu1,
                                 head/NOUN{NOUNREC1}
                                        [SUBNOUN{SUBNOUNREC1}
                                           [BNOUN(key2)]
                                        ]
                                 mu2
                                ]
>

<m: VERBPPROP{VERBPPROPREC1}
       [subjrel/NPVAR{NPVARREC1},
        head/VERBP{VERBPrec1}
                [head/VERB{VERBREC1}
                         [head/SUBVERB{SUBVERB1}
                                  [head/BVERB(key3)]
                         ],
                 argrel/NPVAR{NPVARREC1}^NP{NPREC1}
                                           [mu1,
                                            head/NOUN{NOUNREC1}
                                                   [SUBNOUN{SUBNOUNREC1}
                                                       [BNOUN(key2)]
                                                   ]
                                            mu2
                                           ]

                ]
       ]
>
\end{verbatim}
\begin{verbatim}
DECOMP
   INIT
      <
         Quote_DecompFindSemiIdioms(key3, key2, 2)
      >

      { C2: Quote_StillSemiIdKeys 
        A2: key1 := Quote_GetSemiIdKey
      } 

COMP
   INIT
      <
        Quote_CompFindSemiIdioms(key1, key2, 2)
      >

      { C2: Quote_StillSemiIdKeys 
        A2: key3 := Quote_GetSemiIdKey
      } 

\end{verbatim}

This rule contains some new M-rule notation extensions. First, it now is 
possible
to put a condition on the substituent S-tree which corresponds with a 
variable. Such a condition is preceded by the `$\wedge$' symbol. Secondly, the 
INIT part is introduced. The INIT part may only contain actions. These
actions are executed before the actual condition action pairs are evaluated.
Here, the INIT part is used to look up all relevant entries in SEMI-ID-DICT
by means of the function Quote\_DeCompFindSemiIdioms. Finally, for each of these
relevant entries the right or left models must be instantiated. This is 
realized by means of the new syntactical construction 
\begin{verbatim}
     { C : "condition"
       A : "action"
     }
\end{verbatim}
The meaning of this construction is equivalent to the meaning of a WHILE DO OD
loop in ordinary programming languages,
\begin{verbatim}
     WHILE "condition"
     DO "action" OD
\end{verbatim}

\section{Conclusions}
The method described in section~\ref{MET}  meets the criteria given in
section~\ref{CRIT}. There remain several translation problems which have not 
been discussed in this paper. It is not clear to us, however, how these 
problems could be solved under alternative methods.
