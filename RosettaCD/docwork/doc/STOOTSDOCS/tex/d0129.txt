\section{Example: Tables}
In this appendix we give an example of how to produce a {\em table}
in \LaTeX.

\begin{table}[h]
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l||r|c|}
\hline
First & Second & Third \\
\hline \hline
a & b & 100 \\
\hline
d & e & 1 \\
\cline{2-3}
g & h & 10 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\caption{Example of a {\em table}}
\end{table}
The table is produced by the following input sequence:
\begin{quote}
\begin{verbatim}
1       \begin{table}[htbp]
2       \begin{tabular}{|l||r|c|}
3       \hline
4       First & Second & Third \\
5       \hline \hline
6       a & b & 100 \\
7       \hline
8       d & e & 1 \\
9       \cline{2-3}
10      g & h & 10 \\
11      \hline
12      \end{tabular}
13      \caption{Example of a {\bf table}}
14      \end{table}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
The first and last line delimites the table environment.
The arguments {\em h}, {\em t}, {\em b} and {\em p} gives the
place where the table must be put ({\em here}, {\em top} of the page,
{\em bottom} of the page or {\em Page of floats}).
Appendix C.8 of the \LaTeX\ reference manual \cite{Lam85}
explains in more detail the meaning of these options.
The options used in this example give the result desired
most of the time.
The second argument of the command in the second line defines 
the number of columns of the table and the layout of the items 
in the table. 
It consist of a sequence of {\em l}, {\em r} and {\em c} with
horizontal bars between them.
When an {\em l} apears in a column then the items in that column are shift
to the left; {\em r} does the same for to the right. 
A {\em c} produces items which are centered.
Vertical bars draw vertical lines between the items in the
table.
Two bars close to each other produce a double vertical bar.
\\[1ex]
Lines $3,\ldots,11$ define the contents of the table. 
Each line produces a row of the table.
Such a row can be a horizontal line or a real item.
Lines 3,7 and 11 produce horizontal lines.
Line 5 produces a double horizontal line. 
Line 9 produces a horizontal line from column 2 till 3.
The rest of the lines produces the real items.
The items within a row are seperated by \verb@&@ characters.
A row must be ended by two backslashes (\verb+\\+)

Line 13 produces the title of the table.
Each table in the document is numbered.
All tablenumbers with the title and the pagenumber are put into the file with
extention {\bf .lot} (list of tables).
This list can be put into the document by issuing the command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\listoftables@
\end{quote}

\section{Example: Figure}
In this appendix we give an example of how to produce a {\em figure}
in \LaTeX.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\begin{center}
\begin{picture}(300,200)
   \put(0,20){\framebox(200,150){}}

   \put(100,150){D}
   \put(50, 95) {O}
   \put(150,95) {O}
   \put(100,50) {M}

   \put(0,20) {\line(4,3) {200}}
   \put(0,170){\line(4,-3){200}}

   \put(100,95){\circle{20}}

   \put(0,10)  {\vector(1,0) {200}}
   \put(200,10){\vector(-1,0){200}}
   \put(100,0) {200}

   \put(210,20) {\vector(0,1) {150}}
   \put(210,170){\vector(0,-1){150}}
   \put(220,95) {150}
\end{picture}
\end{center}
\caption{Example of a figure}
\end{figure}
The figure is produced by the following input sequence:
\begin{quote}
\begin{verbatim}
 1      \begin{figure}[htbp]
 2      \begin{picture}(300,200)
 3             \put(0,20){\framebox(200,150){}}
 4
 5             \put(100,150){D}
 6             \put(50, 95) {O}
 7             \put(150,95) {O}
 8             \put(100,50) {M}
 9             
10             \put(0,20) {\line(4,3)  {200}}
11             \put(0,170){\line(-4,-3){200}}
12            
13             \put(100,95){\circle{20}}
14
15             \put(0,10)  {\vector(1,0) {200}}
16             \put(200,10){\vector(-1,0){200}}
17             \put(100,0) {200}
18
19             \put(210,20) {\vector(0,1) {150}}
20             \put(210,170){\vector(0,-1){150}}
21             \put(220,95) {150}
22
23      \end{picture}
24      \caption{Example of a figure}
25      \end{figure}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
The first and the two last lines have the same meaning as
mentioned in appendix B.
The list of figures is put into the file with extension {\em .lof} which
can be put into the document with the command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\listoffigures@
\end{quote}

Line 2 defines the figure environment.
The argument of the command on line 2 defines the size of the figure.
The basic length unit is $1/72$ inch ($\simeq$ 0.35278mm).
The first argument is the horizontal size; the second the vertical
size.

Lines 3 till 20 draw the elements of the picture.
Each element of the picture is put on a particular point.

The command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\put(@ $x_{0},y_{0}$\verb@){@{\em element}\verb@}@
\end{quote}
puts an element on
the point ($x_{0},y_{0}$).
There are a number of different elements.
In the example a few of them are given (framebox, line, vector and
circle).
You can also put ordinary text (line $ 5, \ldots, 8 $, 17 and 21)
on any point.

Examples of elements are:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\framebox(@{\em h,v}\verb@){@{\em text}\verb@}@
\end{quote}
having two arguments: the horizontal and vertical size
of the box and the text to be put in the box.
\newline
A line can be drawn with the element:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\line(@{\em r,s}\verb@){@{\em len}\verb@}@
\\[1ex]
With: $r, s \in [-4, \ldots, 4]$ 
\end{quote}
A line is drawn between point ($x_{0},y_{0}$) and point
($x_{1},y_{1}$):
\[ \left. 
       \begin{array}{ll}
          x_{1}  & = x_{0} + len \\
          y_{1}  & = y_{0} + \frac{len}{\mid r \mid} * s
       \end{array}
    \right\} \mbox{if $r \neq 0$}
\]
\[ \left.
       \begin{array}{ll}
          x_{1} & = x_{0} \\
          y_{1} & = y_{0} + len
       \end{array}
   \right\} \mbox{if $r = 0$ and $s > 0$}
\]
\[ \left.
       \begin{array}{ll}
          x_{1} & = x_{0} \\
          y_{1} & = y_{0} - len
       \end{array}
   \right\} \mbox{if $r = 0$ and $s < 0$}
\]
The element:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\vector(@{\em r,s}\verb@){@{\em len}\verb@}@
\end{quote}
draws a vector. 
It works exactly like the \verb@\line@ command.
The tip of the arrowhead lies on the endpoint of the line ($x_{1}$,$y_{1}$).

The element:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\circle{@{\em dia}\verb@}@
\end{quote}
draws a circle with diameter {\em dia}.

\section{Introduction}
In this document the basic concepts of the \LaTeX\  document
preparation system will be described.
This introductory section describes how to create a \LaTeX\ file, how to
call \LaTeX,
what output to expect, how to print the output generated by
\LaTeX and how to have your document distributed.
Section 2 deals with the global layout of a document,
being the sections, subsections, appendices, paragraphs and
lists.
Rosetta special macro's are introduced here too.
Section 3 introduces some local layout commands e.g. to
{\em emphasize} text or how to print text in {\bf boldface}.
Section 4 gives some hints on how to work with \LaTeX\  in a more
convenient way.
These are observations we made in working with \LaTeX.

The four appendices contain examples of \LaTeX\  input and output.
Appendix A considers the normal text formatting;
Appendix B gives an example of how to construct a table;
Appendic C shows how to construct a figure and
appendic D describes how to grow trees.

\begin{quote}
   modify R{\em doc-nr}.tex

document file.

The first thing to do when ever you are editing a document is to press the

\begin{verbatim}
   \documentstyle{Rosetta}
\end{verbatim}

Every Rosetta document should start with a title page. To get this title page
press the {\bf GOLD} key and then {\bf d}. This will insert the proper \LaTeX\ 
sequence to create a title page. Replace the default values by the actual
values.

\subsection{\LaTeX\  calling sequence and results}
You can start the \LaTeX\  program by typing:
\begin{quote}
   dbuild doc:{\em sourcefile}.dvi
\end{quote}
The dbuild command thinks that {\bf doc:{\em sourcefile}.tex} is the sourcefile
containing your text and \LaTeX\  commands. It also thinks that this source
file includes the files {\bf doc:Rosetta.sty} and all
\linebreak[3]{\bf doc:{\em sourcefile}\_$*$.tex}
(see also section 4, How to produce large documents). Therefore if any of
these files have changed since the last time you gave the dbuild command the
{\bf doc:{\em sourcefile}.dvi} file will be constructed. 

When \LaTeX\  encounters an error,
this is printed on the terminal.
You can then react on this error immediately or if you don't know
what to do by typing {\bf h} for {\bf help}.
\LaTeX\  error messages are usually very cryptic.
You can order \LaTeX\  to just ignore the error and continue by
typing a {\bf return} and if the same error occurs again,
it is time you try to figure out what is wrong.
Typing an {\bf x} exits \LaTeX\  and makes it possible for you to
examine your inputfile. The logging file
(see below)
might be of hand with
this.

The following files will be produced:
\begin{description}

\item [doc:sourcefile.dvi] The most important outputfile; this is the file
   than can be converted to a file suited to be printed on the laserprinter.
   To do so, you should issue the command:
   \begin{quote}
      dbuild doc:{\em sourcefile}.ln3
   \end{quote}
   If the {\bf doc:{\em sourcefile}.dvi}
   file is not uptodate then it is constructed in
   the same way as by the previous described command.
   To actually print this file on the laser printed you should issue the
   following command:
   \begin{quote}
      dbuild doc:{\em sourcefile}.laser
   \end{quote}
   If the {\bf doc:{\em sourcefile}.ln3} file is not uptodate then again
   it is constructed in the same way as by the previous described command.

   BE AWARE: Even in the case that the updating
   process was not succesfull the file {\bf doc:{\em sourcefile}.ln3}
   will be send to the laser printer!

\item [doc:sourcefile.log] In this file a logging is kept of all the
   output and error messages \LaTeX\  normally produces on your
   terminal.

\item [doc:sourcefile.aux] This file is used for further processing,
   especially when using BibTeX.
   We will not discuss this here.

\item [doc:sourcefile.toc/lof/lot] These files contain the table of
   contents, the list of figures and the list of tables
   respectively.
   These are not included in the output yet.
   If you want the output to contain them,
   you should run \LaTeX\  again by typing the following command:
   \begin{quote}
      dbuild doc:{\em sourcefile}.dvi  \verb'"'/FROM\_SOURCES\verb'"'
   \end{quote}

\end{description}

\subsection{\LaTeX\  command format}
\LaTeX\  commands have some sort of format,
that is used most of the time.
Some aspects of this format are:
every \LaTeX\  command begins with a $\backslash$ .
After the backslash follows a \LaTeX\  keyword.
After the keyword, parameters or text on which the command
operates can follow.
Optional parameters are enclosed between square brackets ([ and ]) and prescribed
parameters are enclosed between curly braces (\{ and \}).
Text on which a \LaTeX\  command executes is always included
between curly braces (\{ and \}).

Section 2.3 to 2.5 deal with various levels of layout indications:
Chapters and appendixes in 2.3,
Paragraphs in 2.4
and item lists in 2.5.
\subsection{ESPRIT 415-A document styling}
Every \LaTeX\  document for ESPRIT should start with:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\documentstyle{esprit}@
\end{quote}
If you are using \LaTeX\  in a different way,
you can use another style for the {\em esprit} mentioned above.
Available are: {\em article}, {\em letter} and {\em report}.
In fact the {\em esprit} style is the {\em article} style with
some special features.

After the documentstyle, you should add
\begin{quote}
\verb@\begin{document}@
\end{quote}
After this you can type the document and at the last line of the
file you then should add:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\end{document}@
\end{quote}
These are the three main commands that always surround every
document.

\subsection{ESPRIT title page}
Now it is time to generate the ESPRIT title page.
you can do this by issuing the command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\esprittitle@
\verb@{@
{\em Title}
\verb@} {@
{\em Author}
\verb@} {@
{\em Doc. nr.}
\verb@} {@
{\em Date}
\verb@} {@
{\em Status}
\verb@}@
\newline
\verb@{@
{\em Supersedes}
\verb@} {@
{\em Distribution}
\verb@} {@
{\em Clearance}
\verb@} {@
{\em Keywords}
\verb@}@
\end{quote}
All 9 arguments should be present!
If you want the argument to be empty,
you can type e.g. \{-\} for that argument,
but remember that it must be present.
Arguments may be separated by spaces,
tabs, newlines or any mixture of these.

This command will automatically generate the titlepage and after this
you can start with the real document.
\subsection{Chapters and appendixes}
In \LaTeX\  chapters are usually called sections.
To start a new section issue the command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\section{@{\em section-title}\verb@}@
\end{quote}
This command automatically generates a section header with an appropriate
number and automatically generates an element in the table of contents
present in every ESPRIT document.
The header contains the {\em section-title} argument specified in the
command.
If you want to turn off numbering and listing in the table of contents,
you can insert an asterisk (*) between the word \verb@section@
and the opening curly brace;
this specific section will then be generated without a number and it
will not appear in the table of contents.

After the section header \LaTeX\ will generate one blank line,
and after that the first text of the section will be printed.

Sections are the main level of layout.
Subsections can be obtained by issuing:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\subsection{@{\em subsection-title}\verb@}@
\newline
and
\newline
\verb@\subsubsection{@{\em subsubsection-title}\verb@}@
\end{quote}
Again each of these commands generates a header with appropriate
numbering and an empty line before the text is started.
More levels of nesting (e.g. subsubsubsections) do not exist!
This is not a real problem as a document needing such a deep level
of section nesting is not structured well anyhow.
\subsection{Paragraphs}
The \LaTeX\  command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\paragraph@
\end{quote}
is a little bit a misnomer.
In fact it is used to separate parts of the text by means of
a white line.
The text after this command is started at the leftmost position of
the next line after the insertion of an empty line.

A different way to separate textual bodies is achieved by typing
an empty line in the input file.
When \LaTeX\  encounters such an empty line in a piece of
{\em text input},
it will immediately end the current line and start the following text
at about the fourth leftmost position of the next line
(see above at "A different way to ....").
\subsection{Item lists}
Item lists can be used to sequence a set of items.
Each item must be started with a:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\item[@{\em label}\verb@]@
\end{quote}
The label is optional.
Items are separated from each other by a blank line and from
the surrounding text by the fact that the items are labeled
and the items are indented
(displayed paragraph).
The {\em label} is used to label the item.
If no label is present a default label will be used,
depending on the kind of list you are using.
Possible lists are:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\begin{itemize}@
\newline
{\em items}
\newline
\verb@\end{itemize}@
\end{quote}
producing a bullit list,
\begin{quote}
\verb@\begin{enumerate}@
\newline
{\em items}
\newline
\verb@\end{enumerate}@
\end{quote}
producing a numbered list and
\begin{quote}
\verb@\begin{description}@
\newline
{\em items}
\newline
\verb@\end{description}@
\end{quote}
producing a list with no default label.

\section{Local layout}

In this section we describe some commands that process a part of a
sentence or a group of sentences.

\subsection{Emphasize}

{To \em emphasize a piece of text} issue the following command:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\em@
\end{quote}
The text you want to emphasize is delimited by a {\bf \{} and {\bf \}}.
\{ defines a new scope and \} ends it.
When you put the declaration {\bf \verb@\em@} within the new
scope the {\em rest} of this scope will be emphasized.
The text which proceeds the {\bf \verb@\em@} command 
will {\em not} be emphasized.
The first sentence of this subsection can be produced by:
\begin{verbatim}
{To \em emphasize a piece of text} issue the following declaration:
\end{verbatim}
After the \} the setting from before the \{ is restored.
When you want to emphasize a large piece of text you can use the
environment mode:
\\[1ex]
\begin{quote}
\begin{verbatim}
\begin{em}
    :
\end{em}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Boldface}

With the {\bf \verb@\bf@} command you can print a piece of text in
{\bf boldface}.
Changing \verb@em@ to \verb@bf@ in the previous subsection gives the 
desired result.

\subsection{Newline}

The commands:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\\[@{\em len}\verb@]@
\newline
\verb@\newline@
\end{quote}
start a new line without justifying the current one.
The optional argument {\em len} produces extra vertical space
between the current line and the next one.
The length is specified by a number followed by a unit length.
The unit length can be one of:
\begin{itemize}
\item \verb@in@ (= inch)
\item \verb@cm@
\item \verb@mm@
\item \verb@pt@ ($\simeq 0.357$ in)
\item \verb@ex@ (= the height of the character `X')
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Miscellaneous}
You cannot produce the following characters in normal text:
\begin{quote}
  \$ 
  \& 
  \% 
  \# 
  \_ 
  \{ 
  \}
\end{quote}
They can be produced however by:
\begin{quote}
\begin{verbatim}
\$ \& \% \# \_ \{ \}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
The characters
\begin{quote}
\begin{verbatim}
~ ^ \
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
are not easy to produce.
They can be produced by the {\bf \verb@\verb@} command.
The argument of this command is not enclosed by braces, but by a pair
of identical characters used as delimeters
(we suggest to use the at: @).
\begin{quote}
 \verb@~ ^ \@ can be produced by \verb% \verb@~ ^ \@%
\end{quote}
This command can also be used to print text exactly the way you 
want it to appear in the document.
Large piece of text can be obtained by issuing:
\begin{quote}
\verb@\begin{verbatim}@\newline
\verb@    :@\newline
\verb@\end{verbatim}@
\end{quote}
You can type anything in the body except for the fourteen-character
sequence
\newline
`\verb@\end{verbatim}@'.

\section{How to produce large documents}

When you are writing a large document it is better to put each
chapter into {\em a separate} file.
Each of these files can be processed by \LaTeX\ independently.
This has the advantage that you can pay attention to one chapter
at a time and make the output correct.
When the output is correct you can go on with the next chapter.
Take care that the chapter is surrounded by 
\verb$\documentstyle, \begin{document},\end{document}$.
When all the chapters are correct you can put these files into one
large file.
Or, you can make a master file in which you include the chapter files:
\begin{quote}
\begin{verbatim}
\documentstyle{esprit}
\begin{document}
\esprittitle{..}{..}{..}{..}{..}{..}{..}{..}{..}
\input{chapter1.tex}
\input{chapter2.tex}
      :
\end{document}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
This style is also recommended when you want to make {\em tables}\/(see
appendix B), {\em figures} (see appendix C) or {\em mathematical formulas}.

In fact we are advising you to use separate compilation,
quite a common practise in programming.
